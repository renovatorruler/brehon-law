# THE FOUR OLDS — story spine v1 (2026-07-07)

**Source sentiment:** the July-4 monologue (2026-07-07_SOURCE-TRANSCRIPT.txt).
Its thesis: a country is a home; memories — especially the bad ones — are what
anchor you to it; the communist method is to erase the memories first ("the four
olds") so the home can be taken and rewritten. Its climax: celebrate unashamed;
it's INDEPENDENCE DAY, not "the fourth"; God bless America.

**THE TWO LAWS (user, non-negotiable):**
1. If it does not have commies in it, it's commie gobbledygook. NAMED, on the page.
2. If it does not have the greatness of America in it, it's commie gobbledygook.
Both present, unashamed. No coding, no vinyl.

**The Braveheart law (setup fix):** open on LOVE of the precious thing, then an
INTIMATE atrocity in the flesh, and only then the rise. Never open on apathy.

**Comp:** Braveheart's arc at neighborhood scale, Capra's block, deadpan menace
in the middle, sincere and swelling at the end. It must goosebump, not smirk.

## 1 · THE TRANSFORMATION (the mirror the audience rides)
A man who wants his own past erased becomes the keeper of everyone's.
FRANK NOVAK grew up with a father whose marks on the house are both kinds — the
pencil heights in his handwriting, and the cellar Frank still won't open. The
Committee's gospel ("you don't want that painful memory hanging around") works
on Frank FIRST because he's wanted it his whole life. The story teaches him —
and the audience, holding their own deletable memories — that the wounds are
load-bearing: you cannot resent the marks without resenting the man they made.
**The Sod (held, never stated):** Frank's war with the Committee is his war
with his father. Keeping the country's ugly history and keeping the rafter
mark are the same act. The cellar door carries it; no one ever says it.

## 2 · TWO WORLDS
**The marked world:** Kosciuszko Avenue, a Rust Belt block built by immigrants
— pencil heights on doorframes, a coffin flag folded in a triangle case, the
rafter Dad cracked his head on (they laughed about it for thirty years), the
avocado tree that broke an arm, plaques with names on them.
**The sanded world:** Unit Way 4 — vinyl plank, numbered streets, a
"recontextualized" veterans' hall, appliances new and warm and no one's.
The camera can always tell you which world a scene is in without a word.

## 3 · LOGLINE (for-loop + exception)
Every house on the block gets erased, one work order at a time, by a
contractor who has spent his whole life wishing his own past could be — until
the last work order on the list is his family's house.

## 4 · TWO DOORWAYS
**Doorway 1 (in — irreversible, chosen):** Frank signs with the Cultural
Renewal Committee. The pay is triple, but the hook is personal: "biographical
remediation" — his father's file (the DUIs, the CPS report, all of it) sealed
and shredded as part of "healing the past." Erasure, offered as mercy. His
first job seals it: the widow ADA's house, her late husband's marks on every
wall. She serves him lemonade and asks him to leave one wall. He sands it,
takes the check, the block watches. That night he can't remember which wall it
was. Erasure works. That's the horror.
**Doorway 2 (no return — done TO him, in the flesh):** the veterans' cemetery
"relocation for consolidated memorial efficiency." The Committee contracts
Frank for site prep — and his grandfather's name is on the manifest.
STANISLAW NOVAK, OMAHA BEACH, his stone in the grinder queue, his remains in a
numbered box. The Murron moment: intimate, physical, unforgivable. Frank takes
the stone and the flag and drives through the fence. Warrant out. No way back.

## 5 · MIDPOINT — THE MIRROR
The work order for his own family house. Frank walks it alone, sander in hand,
dust mask on: the doorframe with his own heights in his father's handwriting,
the rafter, the cellar door. In the hallway mirror — a man dressed to erase
himself. He cannot pull the trigger on the sander.
**The transformation is TESTED, not declared:** Director CRANE, no fool,
offers the deal — the house listed as "exemplar renewal," a district-lead
promotion, June's scholarship secured, and the copy of the father-file that
Crane kept as insurance shredded for real. All Frank has to do is sand the
house himself, publicly, as a model citizen. Frank asks for a week — the
coward's pause, human and real. What answers for him is Doorway 2.

## 6 · THE BEATS (connected prose)

**ACT 1 — LOVE FIRST (~25).** Last year's Independence Day on Kosciuszko
Avenue, small and true: the block party, Ada's husband still alive at the
grill, the rafter story told laughing, the folded flag in its case, June (17)
being measured against the doorframe — the pencil going ABOVE Frank's own
childhood marks, in the same wall, different hands. And once, quietly: the
cellar door Frank doesn't open, June's question about Grandpa Novak that
Frank doesn't answer. The wound planted inside the love.
Then the election. Director Crane at the community center — young, gentle,
credentialed, funny — announcing renewal: debts forgiven, appliances free,
everything "brought up to code." The statue "relocated." The crowd mostly
nods. The seduction is real, and it costs nothing. At the intake table, the
biographical-remediation brochure. Frank signs. (Doorway 1; Ada's wall.)

**ACT 2A — THE FOR-LOOP (~25).** The jobs run. Street signs come down for
numbers; the school is renamed; the library's "old ideas, old culture, old
customs, old habits" go to pulping — Crane's staff seminar names the method
with admiration and cites its inventor by name: Mao. The commies are IN it,
on the page, teaching it as best practice. June wins a Committee scholarship
and is proud of her father. Ada, moved to a renewal unit, gets lost on her own
street because none of it answers to her memory anymore. Frank's shredded
father-file arrives — and the promised relief never comes. The evidence is
gone; the wound is exactly where it was. Erasure doesn't heal. It only
disarms. MIDPOINT: his own house on the work order. The mirror. The stall.

**ACT 2B — THE ARCHIVIST (~25).** Frank keeps taking jobs — and starts
stealing from them. The contractor has access: a widow's photograph out of a
dumpster, the school's bronze plaque unscrewed at night, the statue's head
bought back from the scrapyard for beer money. His garage becomes a secret
museum of everything the block was ordered to forget. June finds it — and the
one real debate of the film happens between father and daughter, not enemies:
"Some of this SHOULD be shameful, Dad." And Frank finally says the wound out
loud — the cellar, the belt, the nights — and why the rafter mark stays
anyway: hate the mark and you hate the man it made. She wavers. Crane makes
the exemplar offer (the test). Frank asks for his week. Then the cemetery
manifest. (Doorway 2 — the stone, the flag, the fence.)

**ACT 3 — INDEPENDENCE DAY (~25).** The Committee schedules the block's
"completion ceremony" for July 4th — renamed Unity Day, fireworks banned,
flags collected door to door, voluntarily, with a receipt. Frank's answer is
the garage: in one night, with June and Ada and the veterans' hall men, the
whole museum goes BACK — plaques re-screwed, the statue's head set on its
plinth, the old street names hand-painted over the numbers, pencil marks
re-drawn on twenty doorframes from memory, Ada's wall re-painted from a
photograph. The for-loop run backward. At dawn: Crane, enforcement, cameras —
and his own wall: gentleness was his weapon, and gentleness cannot order a
crackdown on lawn furniture live on camera while a whole block stands in
front of its marks. The neighbors come out — even the ones who took the
appliances. Consent evaporates. Crane withdraws the way bureaucracies do:
"this neighborhood is designated non-priority." Not repentance — retreat. The
war isn't over; the block is just unconquerable now.
The vet hall's contraband fireworks go up. June pencils her own height on the
doorframe under Frank's. Frank opens the cellar door, stands in it, leaves
everything exactly where it is, and comes back up. A neighbor calls across
the yard, "Happy Fourth, Frank!" — and Frank, grinning, gives the correction
the whole film has earned: "It's Independence Day." Beat. "God bless America."
Fireworks. END.
**Final image:** the doorframe — three generations of pencil, the newest line
still shiny.

## 7 · PACING
Four ~25 sheets. The vise tightens in materials: incentives → renames → fines
→ door-to-door collections → the graves → force offered and refused. Breaths
placed at the block party (act 1), the garage reveal (2B), the night of
re-marking (act 3 — quiet, almost silent, before the dawn confrontation).
Register: earnest. The middle may be deadpan-menacing; the end swells without
apology. If the finale doesn't goosebump, it failed.

## CAST (tight)
FRANK NOVAK — contractor, the eraser who becomes the keeper.
JUNE NOVAK — 17, his daughter; Committee scholar; converted by witnessing.
DIRECTOR CRANE — Cultural Renewal Committee; young, warm, credentialed; the
gentleness IS the weapon; gets his own wall (superiors, cameras, consent).
ADA — the widow of the first job; the block's memory in a body.
STANISLAW NOVAK — grandfather; Omaha Beach; the flag; present as artifact and
one Act-1 story told aloud.
FRANK'S FATHER — dead before the film; present as marks: the pencil, the
rafter, the cellar. Both kinds of memory in one man.
