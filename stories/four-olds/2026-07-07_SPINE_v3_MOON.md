# THE FOUR OLDS — full spine v3 (2026-07-07)
_The approved v4 concept (Moon flags, Marwani, Hale, the six, the cookout) run
through the seven steps. Laws in force: commies named on the page; greatness of
America embodied; 70/30 swagger ratio; comedy points at the apparatus, never at
the flags; real-person ECHOES with invented names, zero biographical claims._

## 1 · TRANSFORMATION (whose, and the audience's mirror)
**A-story: CRICKET DAWES.** The country erased him once — Apollo 18, cut by a
budget line in '72 — and he kept the faith *privately* for fifty years: a
homebuilt simulator in a barn, currency maintained for a mission that no
longer existed. His transformation: from private, wounded faith to PUBLIC,
JOYFUL defiance — from "my country forgot me" to "the country is not the
government." **Tested, not declared:** mid-flight, the administration offers
him the one thing he's wanted for half a century — Apollo 18 restored to the
official record, the museum wing, the honors — if he turns the ship around.
The regime's ultimate weapon is the same as ever: it offers to un-erase you if
you'll help erase everything else. He declines: "You can't give back what you
never had."
**B-story mirror: MAGNUS HALE** — the same test at billionaire scale. Two men
the regime tried to buy; two refusals, one whispered in vacuum, one roared on
live TV.
**Audience mirror:** everyone who's been told the things they love are
obsolete — and everyone who suspects the erased might be the only ones who
remember how anything works.
**The Sod (held, never stated):** the erased keep the faith; shamelessness is
the shield. No character ever says either sentence.

## 2 · TWO WORLDS
**The networked world:** Marwani's Washington — Nobel ceremonies, the Harmony
office, consensus by committee, the fly-by-cloud fleet where every bolt phones
home, the Smithsonian gallery going into crates. Beige, smooth, monitored,
applauded.
**The analog world:** Cricket's barn simulator; the Texas boneyard; machine
shops running night shifts on wrong paperwork; stick-and-rudder, celestial
navigation, a grill treated as a sacrament. Cluttered, loud, greasy,
unconfiscatable. The camera always knows which world it's in.

## 3 · LOGLINE (for-loop + exception + counter-exception)
One old thing at a time — a gallery, a street, a mission record — the
administration erases America to purchase the world's applause, until the only
marks left beyond its reach are six flags on the Moon; so it nationalizes the
rockets to go take them down — not knowing about the one ship too old to be on
the network, or the six Americans too old to be on anyone's payroll.

## 4 · TWO DOORWAYS
**Doorway 1 (in — chosen, irreversible):** the boneyard. After the Smithsonian
crating (the act-one atrocity: Cricket watching his cancelled mission's
capsule boxed "for contextualized storage"), the barn phone rings. The crew
assembles under thirty years of tarps — and the moment the LIBERTY BELL rolls
out of the boneyard on a stolen transporter, every one of them is a felon.
There is no giving her back.
**Doorway 2 (no return — physics itself):** TLI. Trans-lunar injection, the
burn you cannot take back. The film gets its no-return doorway for free from
orbital mechanics, and says so out loud in procedure-speak: "Go for TLI" is
the last decision anyone on that ship gets to un-make.

## 5 · MIDPOINT — THE MIRROR
The dark launch is the midpoint's thunder; the mirror comes in the coast. The
administration finds them and brands them on every screen on Earth: **"four
obsolete men and two delinquents."** The crew floats in silence watching
themselves described as thieves and relics — the world's image of them, the
mirror. Cricket at the window, looking at the Moon he was promised in 1972.
Then the answer: Tito shakes a paint pen, and THE FOUR OLDS goes on the
bulkhead in grease-pencil capitals. The slur becomes the crew patch. Directly
on its heels, the test: Marwani himself calls the ship — charm first, always —
and makes Cricket the restoration offer. The refusal closes the midpoint:
they stopped flying home the moment they painted the name.

## 6 · BEATS (connected prose)

**ACT 1 — THE ANNOUNCEMENT (~27).** Open on greatness played straight: the
250th-anniversary year, and a montage of what the administration calls
"harmonization" — galleries crated, missions "recontextualized," the Accord
signed to a Geneva standing ovation, Marwani accepting the Nobel FOR THE
ANNOUNCEMENT, the Harmony Banner unveiled (a rainbow-gradient globe, designed
by a 44-nation committee in four years with three vetoes). Hale's public
capitulation — the compliance decree signed at a podium, the world calling
him a sellout, his face unreadable. The villain's soft side, early and
chilling: Marwani alone at night watching Apollo footage, genuinely moved —
and ordering it crated anyway. "That's why it has to go. It's too powerful."
The atrocity, intimate: the Smithsonian Apollo gallery decommissioned on
camera; Cricket Dawes, 79, watches his own cancelled capsule boxed like
evidence. That night, in the barn with the simulator he's kept warm for
fifty years, the phone rings. The crew assembles at the boneyard; the tarp
comes off the Bell. (Doorway 1: she rolls.)

**ACT 2A — THE BONEYARD (~27).** The build as heist-Americana: Mama Bear
re-welding her own famous crack by hand; parts made in three states on wrong
paperwork; Mission Control wired into the back office of a bass-boat
dealership; the olds' bodies retrained (the physicals montage — comedy with
teeth: Cricket's resting heart rate embarrasses the machines); the kids
learning analog under Stitch's growl — Joss flying approaches with the
network dark, Tito memorizing valve trees. Intercut, mirror-montage: the
Harmony crew's solemn training, dignity rehearsals, the banner's handling
protocol (gloves). The Monitorship sniffs the boneyard twice — near-miss
cat-and-mouse, Hale shrugging on camera: "I lose track of scrap." Act ends
at 2 A.M. on the mothballed Matagorda pad: the dark launch, announced to
nobody, eleven minutes before the world knows. (MIDPOINT thunder.)

**ACT 2B — THE COAST (~27).** Earth loses its mind in both directions. The
propaganda machine brands the crew (the MIRROR); the name goes on the
bulkhead; Marwani's charm call and Cricket's refusal (the TEST). The squeeze
on Hale tightens toward seizure; he plays dead-eyed compliance while
Frontier's own engineers quietly slow-walk the Harmony mission's countdown —
nothing provable, everything Texan. "Go for TLI" (DOORWAY 2, the burn with
no refund). The Bloc's fury; the debt-call threat rehearsed in the Oval —
and shelved, because the markets are already voting with the laughter. The
Bell lands first — Joss on the stick, Stitch calling numbers, old and new in
one cockpit — and the setup begins: chairs, umbrella, the resistor-plate
grill Gunny insists counts as a grill, rover donuts cutting fresh tracks in
a wide respectful circle around the sacred six, which no one touches, ever.
The 51st flag goes up beside them: ROOM FOR ONE MORE.

**ACT 3 — TRANQUILITY (~27).** The Harmony lander descends into the most
illegal Fourth of July cookout in human history, live on every screen on
Earth. The standoff the president cannot win: solemnity versus lawn chairs.
The banner ceremony upstaged by a nine-iron; the Bloc observer fuming into
his visor; the hot mic: "...is that a burger?" On Earth, Marwani demands
Hale bring them down — and gets the detonation two acts in the making, live:
**"You want to nationalize me? Go. Fuck. Yourself."** Frontier's stock
craters for an hour, then soars on a tide of pre-orders. Allies peel; the
Nobel curdles into a punchline; the Accord dies of ridicule — no shot fired,
no flag touched. The human beat before the end: the ESA commander accepts a
burger for the photograph — professionals, both crews, one of them just
assigned to the wrong side of history. Over the open loop, to the whole
listening planet: **"It's Independence Day."** Beat. **"God bless America."**
Tito's three smuggled shells go up and burn strange and perfect, unslowed by
any air. WHITE OUT on the third.
**EPILOGUE:** Tranquility Base declared a National Historic Park — the
lawyers discover the preservation statute made the cookout crew the only
LEGAL party on site all along. The Bell never comes home: first exhibit,
THE FOUR OLDS still grease-penciled on her hull, visible from Earth on a
clear night through a good telescope. Cricket's Apollo 18 goes back into
the record — restored by Congress, not offered by a king.

## 7 · PACING
Four ~27 sheets. Act 1 accelerates through applause into the crated capsule;
2A is joy-of-competence (the build must FEEL like the greatness law: making
things is the American sacrament) with the Monitorship as metronome; 2B
alternates vacuum-quiet and Earth-bedlam, the temptation as its still center;
Act 3 is a rocket that keeps topping itself: cookout → hot mic → fuck-you →
ridicule collapse → burger → the line → fireworks. Nobody mourns anything at
any point; the closest thing to tears is Cricket's hand on the hull. The
finale must goosebump twice — once at the line, once at the shells.

## CAST WALLS (frictionless-antagonist law)
MARWANI — wall: his power IS image, and image cannot survive arresting
grandfathers on the Moon; pressure from his own patron (the Bloc holds the
debt and his leash); soft side: he loves what he crates — and fears it.
THE HARMONY CREW — not villains; professionals embarrassed by their orders;
their dignity is the joke's fuel and their burger is the film's mercy.
THE MONITORSHIP — procedural menace on Earth; the near-miss engine of 2A.
