## LOCKED — THE BOOMER-CRAFT TEXTURE (user directive: lean into it)
The whole toolkit of the plan is the hobby shelf of a 1960s boyhood — the
stuff boomers grew up on and nobody young cares about, which is exactly why
the surveillance state can't read it. The regime's machines scan every
packet on Earth; they cannot read a knock, a knot, a slide rule, or a coffee
can of undeveloped film. Old habits save the day BECAUSE they're old (Mao's
list inverted, again). Working set:
- HAM RADIO as the covert mission control — call signs, QSL cards pinned to
  the barn wall; the offer and the refusal happen over it.
- MORSE KNOCK-CODE between the crates — the olds talk in taps for three days.
- SLIDE RULES — Stitch re-checks the computer's burn numbers by hand
  (Apollo crews really flew with one).
- The barn SIMULATOR — surplus panels, Heathkit guts, fifty years of care.
- FILM CAMERAS — the only record of the build is Kodachrome in a coffee
  can; nothing digital exists to seize.
- BOOK CIPHER for the letters between shops (Revolutionary-era craft; the
  key is a Reader's Digest).
- CB RADIO convoy slang on the crate transport; Boy Scout knots on cargo;
  the flag-folding protocol as Gunny's liturgy; a cassette Walkman on AA
  batteries for the coast.
- The kids' role: an amateur-astronomy club with backyard telescopes
  confirms the landing before any network does.

## THE PREMISE (unchanged)
President MARWANI — youngest ever, Nobel Prize for ANNOUNCING the Lunar
Heritage Neutralization Accord — orders the six Apollo flags retrieved and
"contextualized" for a UN museum, replaced with the Harmony Banner (designed
by a 44-nation committee: four years, three vetoes). MAGNUS HALE's company
is forced to fly the mission under threat of nationalization. The de-flagging
is scheduled as the global broadcast centerpiece of Unity Day — July 4th,
the 250th. Our rockets, our pads, our taxes, our flags.

## THE PLAN (new spine)
The mission must bring six flags back, so the manifest carries six
museum-grade artifact containers — climate-controlled, nitrogen-purged.
A preservation crate is already a life-support system with a cover story.
Frontier's own shops build them (MAMA BEAR designs, TITO welds; the
paperwork reads "Class-A archival containment, 6 units"). Four crates carry
the four olds. Two carry the cookout. They ride the regime's own ship to
Tranquility.

**The playbook is chosen out loud — 1775.** Not homage: operational logic.
Every modern tactic lives on a network the regime watches; nobody alive
fights like 1775. Handwritten letters between machine shops (Committees of
Correspondence). Spies inside the occupied machine — the loaders, the
inspectors, the launch techs (the Culper Ring). Boarding the king's ship in
disguise (the Tea Party). Betsy's flag — Tito's grandmother's hand-sewn
51-star — folded in crate five. Gunny's line: "They've got every algorithm
ever written. We've got Valley Forge."

## THE SEVEN STEPS

**1 · Transformation.** Cricket: from privately keeping faith (fifty years
current in a barn simulator, for a mission that no longer exists) to public
joyful defiance. Tested twice: mid-ride, by Marwani's offer (Apollo 18
restored to the record if they stand down — "You can't give back what you
never had"); and at the end, by the ride home he refuses. Hale is the
mirror at billionaire scale — two men the regime tried to buy.
**The held card (never said):** the erased keep the faith; shamelessness is
the shield.

**2 · Two worlds.** The networked world: Nobel podiums, consensus, the
monitored fleet, galleries into crates. The analog world: the barn, the
shops, handwritten letters, a ham radio, a grill. The film's texture war.

**3 · Logline.** To please the world, a captured administration sends
America's own rockets to take down the six flags on the Moon — not knowing
the mission's cargo includes four retired Americans in museum crates, a
grill, and a 51-star flag.

**4 · Doorways.** In: the crates seal on the pad — four lids close over
four faces; no abort from inside. No return: trans-lunar injection — the
burn physics won't refund. Both doors close ON CAMERA, honestly.

**5 · Midpoint mirror.** The inspection. The Bloc observer walks the cargo
bay with the manifest, stops at crate four (Cricket's), lays his palm flat
on the lid. Hold. Inside: an eighty-year-old heartbeat. The "vibration
dampener" hums its cover story. He moves on. The audience breathes. Then
the temptation call relayed through Joss's ham rig, and the refusal.

**6 · Beats.**
- ACT 1 — THE ANNOUNCEMENT (all of it screen-borne, per the camera rule).
  The Accord, the Nobel, Hale's public capitulation — arriving as TVs in
  diners, radios in shops, a phone passed around a break room; the world
  calls Hale a sellout; the audience gets the wink — "Write it off." The
  propaganda reel plants the white-flag claim: "Fifty years of radiation
  have bleached them white. White flags. Flags of surrender. Let us retire
  them with dignity." The intimate atrocity: the
  Smithsonian Apollo gallery crated on camera — Cricket watching his own
  cancelled capsule boxed like evidence. That night the barn phone rings.
  The pitch isn't "steal a ship." It's colder and funnier: "They need six
  coffins for six flags. We're going to be the coffins."
- ACT 2A — THE BUILD (Culper Ring act). The crates built in the open, on
  the regime's own work order; the four olds trained to live in a box
  (medical montage, comedy with teeth); handwritten letters between shops;
  the kids placed inside the loading crew; the dockworker beat — LEXINGTON:
  a loader pops crate four for a random check, meets Cricket's eyes, holds
  three seconds, closes the lid, signs ALL CLEAR. Nobody fires first; one
  man just refuses to kneel. The crates seal. (Doorway one.)
- ACT 2B — THE RIDE. Launch day played from inside crate four: sound and
  g-force and Cricket's face, intercut with the gala broadcast — Marwani in
  a tux toasting "a healed sky" while the thing that will end him rides his
  own rocket (Trenton: cross while the empire drinks). The inspection
  (MIDPOINT). Three days of dark and checklists heard through a bulkhead.
  The temptation and refusal. TLI (doorway two). Landing WITH the ceremony
  — the film rides honest all the way down, then leaves with the Harmony
  crew's mandated rest cycle. We never see the unpacking. (The one big
  withhold, kept: the audience knows the men, not the plan.)
- ACT 3 — TRANQUILITY. Morning. The Harmony commander raises the window
  shade for ceremony prep — lawn chairs. Umbrella. A grill's heat-shimmer.
  Rover-donut tracks in a wide respectful circle around the sacred six.
  Four suited old men, waving. The 51st already up: ROOM FOR ONE MORE. The
  world discovers it through the regime's own broadcast rig — they built
  the transmission that destroys them; theater, planet, and enemy converge
  on one frame. The grievances read to the open loop in Jefferson's cadence
  ("He has crated our museums. He has renamed our streets. He has sold our
  sky…") ending flat: "…so we're having a cookout." The flag beat
  [OPEN — see Open Questions: never-faded vs. already-replaced vs. hybrid;
  either way it detonates the regime's "bleached white surrender flags"
  claim on their own cameras, and it is Gunny's scene]. The ghost file: the ceremony script
  requires custody transfer signed by a representative of the owning nation
  — Cricket produces the $0.00 pay stub: ranking (only) active-status U.S.
  astronaut on site. Writes DENIED. The regime is beaten by its own
  unfinished paperwork. Marwani orders Hale to end it; live TV: "You want
  to nationalize me? Go. Fuck. Yourself." Then one word on the platform he
  still owns — "Look up." — and the PEOPLE answer: porch lights, stadiums,
  city grids flickering in waves across the night side of Earth, visible
  from Tranquility. The crew didn't know it was coming; they get amazed
  with us. Gunny reads Adams' 1776 letter as grace — "…bonfires and
  illuminations from one end of this continent to the other" — a 250-year-
  old prophecy landing on schedule. Burgers for the Harmony crew (the
  film's mercy — the hot-mic "…is that a burger?"). Over the open loop, to
  the whole listening planet: "It's Independence Day." Beat. "God bless
  America."
- EPILOGUE. The Accord dies of ridicule; the Bloc's debt-call bluff fails
  (markets side with the laughter). Public pressure forces the regime to
  fly the defenders home on the Harmony ship — the erasure mission returns
  carrying the men who beat it, cramped, eating cold burgers. Three go
  home. CRICKET STAYS: first Ranger of Tranquility Base National Historic
  Park (the preservation statute made the cookout crew the only legal
  party on site all along), resupply forced by the same public pressure.
  The man the country erased becomes the permanent presence. Apollo 18
  restored by Congress, not offered by a king. Last image: the Ranger
  waving up at the night side of Earth — and the Earth, city by city,
  waving back.

## KNOWLEDGE DESIGN (carried from the audience model)
No cheating: crates, loading, inspection, landing all shown honestly. The
withhold is the COOKOUT — misread props (crates five/six are "Gunny's
gear"), the cutaway ("Gunny, show 'em" — CUT), the rest-cycle coverage gap,
the reveal through the enemy's own camera. Cookout stays out of the trailer.
Hale = irony with a wink; destruct-button dread is replaced in this version
by inspection dread (the palm on the lid); ghost file planted as an Act-1
joke, audience five seconds ahead at the payoff; Marwani fully legible, no
mystery — the film's mystery budget is spent entirely on our side.

## WALLS (so no one is cardboard)

grandfathers at a cookout on live TV; the Bloc holds his leash and his debt.

and their commander's choice to accept a burger is a decision, not a gag.
The regime's competence: the Monitorship catches two of the Ring's letters;
one shop gets raided; the margin is always thin.
