# THE FOUR OLDS — spine v2 (2026-07-07)
_Supersedes v1, which was TOO MOROSE (user note): v1 wrote the defense-of-memory
30% of the source and buried the 70% homage-to-greatness. The video's bad
memories are told LAUGHING (the rafter, the ER, the broken arm) — affection,
not trauma. v2 deletes the imported prestige-drama wounds (the cellar, the
abusive father, the dying Rust Belt) and builds on swagger._

**THE TWO LAWS stand:** commies IN it, named. Greatness of America IN it. Both
on the page, unashamed.
**The register:** Braveheart scope, Footloose engine (joy banned → joy wins),
Jaws-Amity Fourth-of-July texture. Told with a grin, ends at full roar.
**The structural motif:** give an inch, they take a mile — Act 2 is the inches;
Act 3 takes the mile back.

## 1 · TRANSFORMATION
A man who believed greatness defends itself learns greatness must be DEFENDED —
loudly, unashamed. FRANK NOVAK's flaw isn't a wound; it's the giant's
complacency: "They can't touch the Fourth. This town would riot." He laughs at
the first permit form. He signs the first waiver. He is Gulliver, tied down one
reasonable string at a time — and the audience rides it because everyone
believes "they'd never take X" about something they love.
**The Sod (held, never stated):** shame is the weapon; shamelessness is the
shield. Nobody says it. The film just gets louder as it learns it.

## 2 · TWO WORLDS
**The loud world:** New Halas, PA — a town that WORKS and knows it. The Novak
machine shop (grandpa's B-17 parts on the wall, the Omaha Beach helmet, the
shrapnel, displayed PROUD), little league under lights, the drag strip, and
the crown: the Novak Independence Day show — three generations of hand-rolled
shells, the biggest Fourth in the state, people drive two hours for it. The
town's bad memories hang framed: "THE GAZEBO THAT GOT IT — 1987" (the year the
finale misfired; they rebuilt it bigger). Messy, violent, glorious. Fuck yeah.
**The dim world:** what the Committee builds — Unity Day, a licensed drone
show (silent, beige, 20 minutes, pre-approved shapes), the memorial melted
into an abstract loop called "Progress," streets with numbers, appetite with
a permit. Not poverty — SEDATION. They don't come to fix failure; they come to
dim success. Envy wears a lanyard.

## 3 · LOGLINE
The loudest town in America gives up its Fourth of July one reasonable inch at
a time — until the family that has fired the show for three generations
decides to take back the whole mile in a single illegal night.

## 4 · TWO DOORWAYS
**Doorway 1 (the first inch, signed with a grin):** the Committee's first ask
is nothing — a noise variance, a "cultural impact review," the cannon salute
swapped for a recording "just this year." Frank signs it laughing, to be a
good neighbor. Irreversible not because of the paper but because of the
precedent: the town watched Frank Novak sign. The inches now have his name on
the ledger.
**Doorway 2 (the atrocity, in the flesh):** having taken the parade (permits),
the names (Unity Way), the memorial (melted for "Progress"), the Committee
comes for the magazine: the Novak powder house — a century of the family's
craft, grandpa's hand-rolled shells from 1946, the show notebooks in three
hands' handwriting — seized as a "public safety hazard" and DETONATED in a
controlled pit while the town watches behind tape and a Committee drone films
it for the newsletter. Frank's whole inheritance goes up as someone else's
safety demonstration — the one fireworks show the Committee ever approved.
No way back. (The Murron moment — and it's made of fireworks.)

## 5 · MIDPOINT — THE MIRROR
Before the magazine burn: Unity Day. Frank — having conceded inch by inch —
stands in the beige crowd watching the licensed drones spell a pre-approved
dove, and catches his own reflection in the dark window of his own shuttered
shop: a man holding a PERMITTED sparkler, in the town his family taught to
roar. That's the mirror: the giant discovers he's already tied down.
**The test:** Crane offers the exemplar deal — the Novak name on the Unity
Day program as "heritage consultant," the shop's back taxes forgiven, the
family "story" preserved in the official museum (one glass case, curated).
Legacy as taxidermy. Frank asks for a week. The magazine burn answers.

## 6 · BEATS

**ACT 1 — THE ROAR (love first, ~25).** Open on the greatest Fourth the town
ever threw — the whole first act is one Independence Day, played FULL: the
parade, the cannon, the ball game, Stan's helmet worn by a ten-year-old for
the photo, the rafter story told laughing at the cookout, June (17) rolling
her first shell under Frank's hands — the family craft passing down ON
SCREEN. The bad memories are jokes with plaques. The greatness montage is
diegetic: the shop wall — B-17 gears, a Moon-program part, five generations
of patents ("batting a thousand" as set dressing, never a speech). And in the
last minutes of the act: the election result on a muted TV nobody watches,
and a young man with a lanyard at the edge of the party, taking notes,
smiling. (Crane. He loved the show. That's what makes him dangerous — he
thinks it needs SUPERVISING.)

**ACT 2A — THE INCHES (~25).** The for-loop, each iteration reasonable,
signed, thanked: the noise variance → the cannon recorded → the parade
rerouted ("accessibility") → the route shortened → the fireworks "reviewed" →
capped → replaced by drones ("this year only") → the memorial melted for
"Progress" → Kosciuszko Ave unscrewed for Unit Way 4 → the school's books
pulped — and in the staff seminar Crane's deputy teaches the method by name,
with admiration: old ideas, old culture, old customs, old habits. Mao, cited
on a slide, in an American municipal building, to applause. Frank keeps
signing — good neighbor, big man, "it's one year." June takes the Committee
scholarship. The town thanks him for being reasonable. MIDPOINT: Unity Day,
the sparkler, the reflection, Crane's taxidermy offer.

**ACT 2B — THE SPARK (~25).** Frank stops signing. Quietly first: he
"loses" paperwork, misquotes inventory, and starts the count — what's left,
who's left: the machine shop men, the vet hall, Ada who kept every program of
every show since 1961, the drag-strip kids who can fabricate anything. The
one real argument is Frank and June — she's inside the Committee now and
argues its case sincerely ("nobody BANNED anything, Dad, it's all still
available, digitally, in context") and Frank takes her to the shop wall and
tells the messy violent glorious stories behind every object — not a lecture,
a comedy set with shrapnel — and she laughs, and then she can't stop looking
at the ONE empty hook (the piece already "archived" by the Committee). The
inch made visible. Then DOORWAY 2: the magazine seizure and the pit burn.
June watches the Committee drone film it. She's done. So is the town.

**ACT 3 — THE MILE (~25).** They don't march. They BUILD — the greatness
finale is a build montage played like a heist: the machine shop roaring at
2 AM, the drag-strip kids turning brake lines into mortar racks, Ada's
programs becoming the show's blueprint, June re-rolling shells from her one
lesson, the vet hall "misplacing" its ceremonial powder, farmers' barges
lining up on the river. America doesn't protest; it MANUFACTURES. July 4th,
renamed Unity Day, drone show scheduled — and at 9:07 PM the river lights up:
the illegal Novak show, biggest in family history, fired off barges and
pickup beds, the old street signs going back up BY LANTERN while every camera
in town points at the sky. Crane orders enforcement and meets his wall: you
cannot arrest a town mid-anthem, on camera, without becoming the newsreel
that ends you — his superiors pull HIM. The bureaucratic retreat: "New Halas
is designated non-priority." The war isn't over. The town is just too loud
to hold.
Close: the cookout at dawn, the gazebo plaque gets a sibling — "THE SHOW THEY
COULDN'T STOP — 20XX." A neighbor, grinning, baiting him: "Helluva fourth,
Frank." Frank, lighting the last shell of the night: "It's Independence Day."
Beat. "God bless America." The shell goes up. WHITE OUT on the boom.
**Final image:** June's initials freshly scratched beside her grandfather's
and great-grandfather's on the powder-house door — salvaged from the pit,
re-hung on the new magazine, char and all. The bad memory kept, laughing.

## 7 · PACING
Four ~25 sheets. Act 1 is a party the audience must ache to return to; the
inches accelerate through 2A (each concession shorter on screen than the
last — the erosion FELT in the cutting); 2B coils; Act 3 is a rocket. The
register never mourns: even the pit burn plays as fury, not grief — nobody
cries; somebody spits, and the next cut is the first lathe spinning up.

## CAST
FRANK NOVAK — third-generation pyrotechnician + machine-shop owner; the
laughing giant; flaw = complacent confidence, not trauma.
JUNE NOVAK — 17; Committee scholar; sincere on the inside of the machine —
converted not by argument but by the empty hook and the pit burn.
DIRECTOR CRANE — young, warm, LOVED the show once; wants it supervised into
safety; his gentleness is the weapon; his wall = cameras, superiors, consent.
ADA — kept every show program since 1961; the town's memory, cheerful.
STAN NOVAK — grandfather; Omaha Beach helmet on the shop wall; hand-rolled
shells from '46 in the magazine; present as craft, not grief.
THE DRAG-STRIP KIDS / THE VET HALL / THE SHOP MEN — the build-montage army.
