# Favorite Son — prose, rendered by the engine

The hotel keycard slides into the lock—plastic worn smooth at the edges, the LED green light blinking once, twice, then steady. The door opens to a room with curtains drawn tight, the air thick with the scent of cheap linen spray and yesterday’s coffee. Reese stands just inside, hands in his pockets, watching the woman pack a suitcase on the bed. Her fingers pause over a silk blouse, then move on. He doesn’t look at her face. He doesn’t need to. The camera in the corner of the ceiling hums softly, its lens unblinking, its red light a tiny, steady pulse. Outside, a car horn blares once, sharp and distant. Reese turns, walks to the window, lifts the curtain just enough to see the street below—no one loitering, no cameras pointed up. He drops the curtain. The woman zips the suitcase. The sound is clean, final. Reese doesn’t speak. He doesn’t nod. He simply steps back, opens the door, and walks out, letting it close behind him without a click. The hallway is quiet, carpeted, lit by recessed bulbs that cast no shadows. At the end of the hall, a housekeeper pushes a cart, her cart, her rhythm. She glances up as he passes, gives a small, practiced nod, and keeps walking. The elevator doors open. He steps in. The doors close. The button for the lobby lights up beneath his thumb.

Claire slides the file across the desk. The paper is thick, the ink still damp on the last page. Reese picks it up, flips to the wire transfers—each line a number, each number a path, each path a name he already knows will never be used. He doesn’t check the totals. He doesn’t ask where she got them. He simply sets the file down, smooths a crease with his thumb, and pushes it back toward her. She takes it. Her fingers brush the edge of the desk. She stands. The chair scrapes once, sharp, then stills. She doesn’t look at him as she walks to the door. He watches her hand hover over the knob—hesitates, then turns the brass. The door opens. She steps into the hallway. The light there is brighter, harder, no shadows to hide in. She doesn’t close the door all the way. A sliver remains, dark behind her. Then it closes. The click is soft. The hallway is empty. The file stays on the edge of the desk, slightly askew. The lamp above it flickers once, then steadies.

Reese leans back in his chair, the leather sighing under his weight. He taps a pen twice against the desk—once, twice—then sets it down. Claire’s file lies open, the cover half-lifted by her grip. He reaches for it, not to take it, but to smooth the top sheet with the heel of his palm. The ink bleeds slightly under pressure, letters smudging where they’d been sharp. He watches the blur spread, then lifts his hand. The paper is clean again, the words restored beneath his touch. He does not blink. Claire’s voice is still warm in his ears—*“You’re the only one who sees it clearly.”* He lets the silence stretch until the weight of it presses her shoulders down, just a fraction. Then he says, “They’ll try to make you doubt yourself. When they do, remember who trusted you first.” He says it like a promise, but his fingers are already turning the pen over in his hand—once, twice—ready for the next shape the silence might take.

The clerk’s wife sits in the third row, left side, her knuckles white around the edge of the bench. She does not cry. Her eyes follow the man’s back as he walks—shoulders hunched, shoes scuffing the marble—until the heavy door swallows him. Then she turns, slowly, and looks straight ahead at the wall behind the bench. A photograph hangs there, faded: Ruth in a wool coat, speaking at a rally, her mouth open mid-sentence, her hand raised like a promise. The wife’s gaze lingers on the photo’s edges, where the frame’s shadow pools thick and dark. She does not move when the bailiff clears his throat. She does not move when the lights dim for the next session. She just sits, breathing, until her fingers uncurl and press flat against the wood—once, twice—as if testing its grain, its weight, its silence.

Reese slides the folder across the scarred oak table. The cover is worn at the corners, the seal broken with a single tear along the spine. Claire doesn’t touch it. She pushes her chair back just enough for her left hand to rest on the edge of the table—fingers splayed, knuckles white. The folder’s weight is uneven, as if one side holds more paper, more silence. She looks at the man’s hands first: nails trimmed short, no rings, a faint scar across the right knuckle. Then she looks at the woman’s face in the photo taped to the inside cover—smiling, but her eyes are narrowed against the sun, her mouth half-open as if mid-argument. Claire’s voice comes out low, steady: “She’s a plant.” She says it like a verdict, not a question. Her fingers stay pressed flat against the wood.

The garage door groaned up on rusted hinges. Rain had pooled in the tire ruts, reflecting the bruised sky. Mara Ellis sat sideways in the driver’s seat, one hand on the wheel, the other resting on her lap, palm up—like she’d been waiting for someone to place something in it. Her coat was unzipped, collar turned up against the damp, but the cold had already stiffened her fingers into a half-curl. The engine was off. The ignition key hung loose in the lock, unbroken. No signs of struggle. No gun. No note. Just the hum of the building’s emergency lights, flickering once, twice, then settling into a steady, indifferent glow.  

Reese stood at the open door, rain dripping from his sleeves onto the concrete. He looked past her face to the dashboard—where the radio still played, a low static hiss under a distant jazz trumpet. He didn’t move to close the door. He didn’t speak. His throat worked once, once only. Then he turned, walked back to the steps, and read her name into the microphone. His voice cracked—not with grief, but with the sudden, precise weight of a calculation failing: *She was supposed to wait. She was supposed to be the key, not the lock.*

Reese laid the report on the desk—thick, bound in black leather, the Commission’s seal pressed deep into the spine. He uncapped the pen: black ink, steady flow. Claire’s fingers trembled as she took it, her left hand, the one that didn’t grip too hard. She signed her name—*Claire Soto*—each letter sharp, deliberate, as if carving into wood. The pen didn’t skip. The paper didn’t resist.  

After she set it down, Reese slid the report into a metal box lined with foam. The box clicked shut. He carried it to the archive door, inserted a key into a lock older than Claire’s grandmother, turned it twice. The door swung open, revealing rows of identical black boxes, each labeled with a year, a name, a reason that no one read anymore. He placed hers in the row marked *2024*. Turned. Locked. Walked back to the door.  

Outside, rain had stopped. The streetlights flickered once—just once—and then held steady, casting long, clean shadows on the wet pavement.

The coffee was black, two sugars, served in a paper cup too hot to hold long. The young lawyer—new to the city, new to the office, new to the idea that integrity could be expensive—accepted it with a nod, fingers brushing the rim, testing the heat. Reese watched the steam rise, thin and straight, then curl and fade against the cold glass of the storefront window. He didn’t smile. He didn’t need to. He simply said the words, low and calm, the same cadence, the same weight, the same quiet offer of a way out: *“You remind me of someone who tried to fix things the right way. She didn’t make it. But you will.”*  

The lawyer’s eyes flickered—not with gratitude, not with suspicion, but with the first flicker of recognition, the kind that comes when someone hands you a key and doesn’t tell you what door it opens. He tucked the cup into his briefcase, zipped it shut, and walked on, the steam long gone, the heat still seeping into his palm.
