DEEPER
(working title)

ACT TWO  --  fourth pass, real speech


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY ONE - MORNING

Gray light through a dirty window well. Overnight the basement has
become something between an office and a cell: a card table, the
binder, the wall map taped to cinderblock, a space heater angled
with awful courtesy at the prisoner.

RAY HALLORAN, zip-tied to the kitchen chair, the dish towel down
around his neck. Gary lifts a paper cup of water with a bent straw
to his lips.

                    GARY
          Small sips. You'll cramp up.

                    HALLORAN
          (drinks, then, fast, low)
          Okay, listen, listen to me a second. My wife,
          Carol, she's gonna call somebody this morning,
          she's probably already, so there's gonna be,
          today, there's gonna be people, and you can
          still— this is still a thing where I fell. You
          take me to a hospital, I say I fell down the
          basement stairs, I'll say it, I'll say whatever
          you, please. Please, Gary.

That word, today, goes into Gary like a clock starting. He looks at
the window well, at the daylight, at the world out there beginning
to move. He has no plan. He grabbed this man off a sidewalk two
nights ago with no plan at all, and the only road he can see runs
straight through. He clicks his pen.

                    GARY
          So we go quick. See, here's— you have to
          understand the thing first. It's a closed
          system. Everything is, if you actually look.
          Money goes in, waste goes out, that part
          anybody can see, but there's a middle, in
          between, that nobody's cleared to look at, and
          that's all I'm— that's the part. So. Where's it
          go.

                    HALLORAN
          It's a sewer, Gary! It's a— the pipe collapsed,
          there's nothing in there but—

                    GARY
          Yeah, no, see, and I get why you'd say that, I
          do, but you signed off on the disposal, so
          whatever this turns out to be, you're already
          in it, so honestly you're better off just— I'm
          trying to help you here. I am.

Halloran stares at a sane man's nightmare. We see it too. The
difference is which of them is in the chair.


INT. KEPLER HALL - FACILITIES OFFICE - DAY ONE - AFTERNOON

LOPEZ over a speakerphone, Halloran's dark office behind her.
BRINKMAN in the doorway.

                    LOPEZ
          He's not picking up, Carol's not picking up,
          so I called over to regional, 'cause she said
          they sent him to some, some summit thing? In
          Columbus?

                    BRINKMAN
          Okay.

                    LOPEZ
          There's no summit. They didn't send him
          anywhere. There's no— Dale, there's no
          Columbus. He told her he's in Columbus and
          there's just nothing there.

A beat. It landing on him.

                    BRINKMAN
          ...Alright. Call campus PD. And when they get
          to asking who'd've had a problem with Ray.

                    LOPEZ
          Yeah. I already know who.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY ONE - NIGHT

The TV low. Gary at Halloran's laptop, hunting and pecking the
cover email, mouthing it as he goes, fixing it, a man forging a
life. On the TV, the local news: weather, a water-main story, a
dog that saved a family. Gary watches it, and something starts to
turn over in him.

                    GARY
          (at the TV, half to himself)
          ...nothing. Not one— they got the whole thing
          sewn up and there's not, nobody's even looking.

He does not yet know the world is about to look very hard, in
exactly the wrong direction.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY TWO - NIGHT

A bowl of soup, half spooned into Halloran by a man who keeps
wiping the dribble off his chin with a paper towel. The grotesque
tenderness of it. Gary has been down here for hours, doing the
thing he has waited his whole life to do, which is talk to someone
who cannot leave.

                    GARY
          ...and she just stood up. Vorhees. In front of
          everybody, the other parents and all, and she
          says to my mother, she goes, Irene, this one.
          A mind like this comes along once. And I'm
          nine, I'm sitting right there. And honestly I
          think that's the last time anybody actually—
          and ever since, every job, every door that
          shut on me, there's this part of me just going,
          okay, alright, this is the part before. Before
          they figure out who I actually am. You know?
          Like the whole thing's just the part before.

Halloran, who has learned that listening buys him air, listens.
And somewhere in there he stops performing it and actually hears
him.

                    HALLORAN
          Can I— earlier you said something about a, you
          wrote a book? You kind of said it and then you
          went right past it, like it didn't— what was
          it about.

Gary goes still.

                    GARY
          What.

                    HALLORAN
          The book. You said three hundred pages, you
          said it and then you just kept going.

A long beat. The one wound he never says out loud, and he is so
starved to be heard that he says it to the man he kidnapped.

                    GARY
          (flat, the wound, plain)
          They sent it back. The press. There was a
          line, one line, "not for us." I didn't even
          open the envelope all the way. I read enough
          to see the shape of it and I put it in the
          drawer.

                    HALLORAN
          (carefully, reaching)
          Gary, that's— a press saying no, that's not, it
          doesn't mean— that's a form letter. That
          happens to everybody who ever sent one of those
          out. That's a Tuesday. That's just a Tuesday.

                    GARY
          (the door slamming)
          You don't have to manage me, Ray. Eat your
          soup.

He stands, takes the bowl, climbs the stairs. Halloran watches him
go and understands, all at once, that he is not chained to a
villain. He is chained to a wound, which is worse, because you can
negotiate with a villain.


INT. RIVERSIDE PRECINCT - FRONT DESK - DAY TWO - MORNING

CAROL HALLORAN, coat over her robe, her two grown KIDS flanking
her, across the counter from a DESK SERGEANT.

                    CAROL
          His car's in the driveway. The car hasn't even
          moved, it's right where he, and he didn't take
          his pills, he takes one in the morning and one
          at night and they're both still sitting in the,
          and he texts me, he texts me from the bathroom
          of our own house, so please don't, don't tell
          me men leave, he did not leave, something
          happened to him, he's— he's gone.

The car. The pills. The sergeant stops filing it under a man who
walked out and starts filing it under a man who was taken. He pulls
a different form, and somewhere in the building a machine that does
not get bored begins, quietly, to turn.


INT. GARY'S CAR - DAY TWO - NIGHT

A dark lot. Gary hunched over the prepaid.

                    GARY
          He gave me a— there's a name. The Founders'
          account. He says the money goes through the
          development side, Advancement, so it's gift
          money, so it never shows up on anything public.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          The donor side. See, that's it, friend, that's
          it exactly, 'cause that's the one place the
          money's got no daylight on it, none. You hear
          what that man just handed you? You understand
          what that is?

                    GARY
          He keeps taking it back, though. Says he made
          it up. Says he's scared.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          'Course he's scared. They always pull it back.
          Second the truth's out of their mouth, the
          fear comes and it's no, no, I made it up. You
          don't listen to the take-back, Gary. You
          already heard the true part. And you can feel
          it, can't you. How close.

Gary can. God help him, he can.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY THREE - DAY

Halloran has changed his strategy. Reason failed. The truth failed.
So now, with the last clear hours he may have, he tries the only
door left, and it is not a lie and it is not the truth.

                    HALLORAN
          You ever— okay. In my garage I got the, I do
          the model trains. The little, HO scale, whole
          little town, I been at it since the kids were
          small. Carol calls it my hobby and I let her,
          but it's—
          (loses it, finds it)
          I went to school for engineering. Two years. I
          was gonna build the real ones. And it just, it
          didn't, my dad got sick and, anyway. I ended up
          keeping other people's buildings standing
          instead of, and that's—
          (a breath, scared, pushing through)
          Look. Look at me a second. I'm nobody, Gary.
          I'm a guy who didn't turn into the thing he was
          supposed to turn into. And I think maybe that's,
          I think that's you too, the kid in the photo,
          and there's no goo, there's no account, it's a
          busted pipe, it's a sad pipe and two sad guys,
          and I'll give you the trains, I'll give you
          anything I got, if you'd just open that door
          and let us both go home.

For one second it is the closest anyone has ever come. We watch it
reach the very edge of him.

                    GARY
          (quiet, almost admiring)
          That's good. That's really— that might be the
          best one yet, Ray, the trains and the "we're
          the same." 'Cause see, that's how I know now.
          They wouldn't send a thug. A thug I'd believe.
          They sent me the saddest possible version of
          it and sat it in a chair. Nobody does that for
          nothing.

He goes back to the binder. Halloran closes his eyes. He has tried
reason, the truth, and the truth about the man himself, and all
three are spent. There is one country left where this man will let
him live, and it is the country of the lie, and Halloran, broken,
books his passage.


EXT. GARY'S NEIGHBORHOOD - DAY THREE - DAY

Gary, hood up, at his mailbox. He stops. Stapled to the pole at
the corner: a flyer. HALLORAN'S face, the good badge photo. MISSING.
A number. A reward. And the next pole. And the next. His own quiet
street papered with the face of the man in his basement.

To Gary, the street tilts and shows its true shape. He pulls one
down, folds it, pockets it. Evidence.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          That's when I started to get how big it was.
          You don't put a man's face on every pole on a
          street unless you need everybody on that street
          looking for one particular person.

He is right about that. He has the particular person wrong.

Inside, the TV. CAROL, on the local news, a microphone in her
face.

                    CAROL (ON TV)
          Ray, if you can— if somebody has him, he, he
          takes a pill in the morning and one at, and he
          hasn't had, it's been three days, he's not a
          strong, he just— he just looks—

Gary turns it off. His hand stays on the dark screen. Something
moves in him, the soup, the trains, "let us both go home."

Then he files it where it has to go to survive: she doesn't know
what her husband's part of, and if she's on television, they put
her there. He goes to the basement door.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY THREE - DUSK

SAL on the porch. Gary holds the door the familiar eight inches.

                    SAL
          Your ma's home called me. 'Cause you're her
          contact, and they said you missed two Sundays,
          and they didn't know who else to—

That lands somewhere Royce cannot reach.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          And a cop come by the site. Asking is Sobek
          the kind of guy who'd do something stupid. I
          told him no. Stood right there and told a cop
          no.
          (the thirty years in it, and he can't get to
           it straight, so he goes where he can)
          I told Petrocelli, hold the spot, my guy's
          coming. He goes what guy. I go, you remember
          Gary. And he don't, but— just come do the
          Whitmore with me, would you? Eight months. You
          read the prints, I'll do the talking, like
          always. Like we—
          (beat, he can't say it)
          I miss you, you dumb son of a— just come back
          to work. Please.

It lands. The man under the mission surfaces, reaches, almost.

From below them both: a chair leg, scraping concrete. Twice.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (alert)
          The hell was that.

                    GARY
          (door already closing)
          Furnace. I gotta go, Sal.

The door shuts. Sal looks at it a long moment, goes from worried to
afraid, and walks to his truck not knowing he stood three feet from
the answer and chose to believe his friend.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY THREE - NIGHT

Gary at the counter, shaking, Sal's voice and Halloran's voice both
still in the room. Right at the edge of putting it down. His hand
finds the prepaid.

                    GARY
          (unsteady)
          Sal came by. My oldest— and the guy downstairs,
          they're both, they're saying the same thing now.
          That there's nothing. That I'm sick.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          'Course they're both saying it. The friend, and
          the man in your basement most of all, you think
          he wants you to find it? It'd cost him
          everything. Listen to me. The small ones leave
          first. That's just how it goes, and it isn't
          'cause they're bad, son, it's 'cause what you're
          carrying is too big for 'em and it scares 'em,
          so they go. That's not your friend failing you.
          That's the size of the thing. Now you finish it.
          Before they get it buried. You're too close to
          fold on me now.

Gary closes his eyes. Chooses. We watch him choose the man who
needs him over every man who loves him.

                    GARY
          ...Yeah. No. I'm not gonna fold.

He hangs up. He doesn't look sick anymore. He looks decided. He
opens the basement door, and the last ordinary thing in him stays
up in the kitchen, alone, where he set it down.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY FOUR - DAY

Halloran is thinner, grayer, a bruise of fatigue under each eye. He
has bought his passage to the country of the lie, and the terrible
thing is how good he is at it now, because he was always a man who
knew how buildings really worked.

                    HALLORAN
          (low, not looking at him)
          It's not in facilities. What you're after. It
          goes through, there's three foundations, shell
          foundations, and the wire codes, I set those
          up myself, years ago, I didn't know what they
          were— there's a guy. Advancement. Voss. If you
          want where the material really goes, that's,
          you want to be looking at Voss.

Every word the desperate invention of a man trading sentences for
one more night of breathing. Gary writes with a shaking hand.

                    GARY
          (wet)
          Voss. Okay. Okay.
          (looking up)
          You did good just now, Ray. That was— you just
          did a brave thing. People are gonna know that.
          History's gonna know you did that.

Halloran turns his face to the cinderblock so Gary won't see what
a man looks like the moment he understands the lie is a sentence
with no end, because now he can never stop telling it.


EXT. UNIVERSITY ADVANCEMENT BUILDING - DAY FOUR - NIGHT

Glass and brick, dark for the night. Gary, good shirt, a visitor
tag he printed at home, walks the exterior like a man casing a
vault. Photographs a loading dock, a keypad, a donor wall of
engraved names, panel by panel, certain one of them is the thread.

Inside, through the glass, the most boring room on earth: cubicles,
a phone bank, a banner reading EVERY GIFT BUILDS THE FUTURE. A lone
DEVELOPMENT OFFICER works late, eats a sad desk salad, on the
phone.

                    DEVELOPMENT OFFICER
          (through the glass, muffled)
          ...and we'd just love for you to maybe think
          about, you know, a legacy gift, a bequest, it's
          a wonderful way to...

Gary watches him with the eyes of a man studying the enemy's nerve
center. We watch a guy hit his quota. A SECURITY GUARD rounds the
building. Gary turns his back, smooth, and walks unhurried to the
dark and the car, carrying photographs of a fundraising office he
is sure is the bottom of the world.


INT. MEADOWBROOK CARE CENTER - DAY FOUR - DAY

IRENE at her window, a puzzle half done, alone, watching a door
that doesn't open. The kind AIDE does the edges with her.

                    IRENE
          He's at the university. They keep him very
          busy up there. He's a very busy man.

                    AIDE
          I know he is, hon. Edges first.

Irene fits blue sky into blue sky, certain, content, waiting on a
son who is, this hour, three miles away photographing a wall of
donor names with a hostage in his basement, the busiest he has ever
been in his life, at nothing.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY FOUR - NIGHT

The corner booth, empty. DONNA wipes it down out of habit. She
takes the folded job clipping from her apron, soft at the creases,
looks at it a long moment.

Then she drops it in the bus tub with the dirty plates, turns the
booth card from RESERVED to OPEN, seats a family of four, pours
their coffee, laughs at the dad's joke. That is how a person stops
waiting.


INT. RIVERSIDE PRECINCT - DAY FIVE - MORNING

DETECTIVE FOSS plants a printout on the desk of her sergeant,
GUTHRIE, who doesn't look up from his own.

                    FOSS
          I need a warrant. Search warrant, a house.
          Today.

                    GUTHRIE
          On what.

                    FOSS
          Halloran's phone hit one tower for two days
          after he went missing, then it quits. That
          tower's a quarter-mile circle. The guy he fired
          that same week, the one who'd put a grievance
          in on him first, lives inside the circle.

                    GUTHRIE
          That's a grudge and a coincidence. That's not a
          body. Ramos isn't handing you a man's house off
          a phone ping.

                    FOSS
          He stood on his porch and told me his water
          main was out. Whole street, no water, all dug
          up. I went past public works on the way in.
          There's no job on that street. There's never
          been a job on that street.

A beat. Guthrie looks at her. Reaches for his phone.

                    GUTHRIE
          (dialing)
          Get me Ramos's clerk.

The machine, which does not get bored, picks up the phone.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY FIVE - DAY

Gary on the porch, keys out, leaving to chase Voss, and he stops.
Down the block, parked, engine off, a car that doesn't belong, two
people in it not looking at him too carefully.

To Gary: surveillance. They've put a car on him. He's so close they
stopped pretending. He goes back inside, heart up, and does not
leave, and does not understand that the only thing keeping that car
off his door is a magistrate's signature an hour away.


INT./EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - DAY FIVE - NIGHT

Gary's car is gone. INSIDE, the basement. Halloran, alone since the
porch voices came faint through the floor, has spent every hour
working his raw hand against the wet plastic of the tie until it's
finally slick enough.

His hand comes free.

Up the stairs on rubber legs, through the kitchen, fumbling the
chain, the night air on his face, one foot on the porch--

Headlights swing across the lawn. Gary, home.

A bad, brief, terrible scramble in the doorway, two soft men who
have never hit anyone, and it ends with Halloran back inside, the
door shut, both of them on the floor, gasping, neither hurt and
both undone.

Halloran has tasted the porch. And Gary, his back to the door,
knows at last the thing he has refused to know for five days: a
detective has his name, a car is on his street, a hostage has felt
the open air. The hours Halloran promised him that first morning
are spent.

He looks at Halloran ruined on the floor, at the binder, at the
photographs of the donor wall, and a terrible light comes up in
him.

                    GARY
          (quiet, almost calm, building)
          Okay. We can't keep it quiet anymore. So we
          don't. We don't even try. We make them do it
          out in the open, in front of everybody, we make
          them bury it with the whole world watching.
          (the light up now)
          We go public.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT TWO.
