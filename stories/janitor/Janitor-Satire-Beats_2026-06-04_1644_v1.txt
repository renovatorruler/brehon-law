The Janitor. Beat list, version one.

Working title still open. A dark comedy. Read top to bottom, this is the story in the order it happens. Whether we open in the middle of the action is a separate decision, noted at the end. Where a beat is a clue, the two lines under it show the readings the design requires: what Gary sees, and what the rest of us see.

Logline. A university janitor who knows in his bones that he is a misunderstood genius mistakes the gibberish he chalks on a professor's board, and the muck backing up in the basement, for the loose thread of a vast cover-up. The more nothing he finds when he pulls on it, the more certain he becomes that it goes deeper than anyone dares to admit.

The beats.

One. Gary mops the third floor of a good university after the students have gone home. He is somewhere in his forties, and he carries himself like a man the world has failed to notice. He watches a lecture through the door glass, and his lips move along with the professor, correcting him.

Two. At the bottom of a department blackboard there is an open, unsolved problem, the kind a professor leaves up for months. After hours, Gary fills the space beneath it with a dense and confident answer of his own. He steps back and looks at it the way a man looks at a sunrise.

Three. The next morning the professor, a tired and decent man named Lind, wipes it off without a second thought, because it is nonsense and he needs the board. Gary, watching from the hall with his cart, understands that the man is simply not equipped to see what he has been shown.
Gary sees an answer being suppressed. We see a busy man cleaning his board.

Four. Gary drinks with his friends, a crew of tradesmen led by Sal, who has known him since they were boys. They rib him and buy the round and love him. Sal can feel, the way you feel a change in the weather, that Gary has begun to think he is a cut above, and it worries him, but he lets it go.

Five. At the diner, Donna keeps his coffee full and laughs at his jokes and would say yes if he ever asked. Gary is too busy explaining the hidden order of things to notice her at all.

Six. On a call-in radio show at two in the morning, a man named Royce, who lost a university post years ago under what he calls suspicious circumstances, takes Gary's call seriously. He tells Gary that a mind like his is exactly the kind they are afraid of. It is the first time in years that anyone has agreed with Gary about Gary.

Seven. Cleaning the old wing, Gary finds a sub-basement he was never cleared to enter. There is a chemical smell, there are sealed drums, there is a logbook of entries made after midnight, and there is a contractor's van that only ever comes at night. He has found the edge of it at last.
Gary sees a hidden operation. We see a sewage main backed up under the old building, worked on at night because you cannot open a bathroom floor during lectures.

Eight. Gary investigates. He photographs license plates, he times the van, he starts a binder. Every ordinary thing he turns up, a budget line, a locked door, an old memo, fits the pattern perfectly, because a pattern that explains everything will explain anything. He stops returning Sal's calls.

Nine. He brings the binder to Royce, who turns the pages slowly, nodding, and ties Gary's basement to three other things Gary had not even known to be frightened of. Gary leaves taller than he walked in.

Ten. Gary delivers a sixty-page dossier to the office of the facilities director, a worn-out middle manager named Halloran, and demands an investigation. Two days later Halloran fires him, naming the missed shifts and the three restricted areas Gary has been caught in this month.
Gary sees them moving the instant he got close. We see paperwork that was already half written.

Eleven. This is the first doorway of no return. Gary does not go home and fall apart. Certain that the firing is the clearest proof yet, he follows Halloran to his car, and that night the facilities director wakes up zip-tied to a chair in Gary's basement, a few feet from the water heater. Gary means to hold him only until he can decide what to do.

Twelve. Gary questions him, politely, with a legal pad. Halloran explains, with rising panic, that he is a facilities director, that there is no program, that the basement is a plumbing failure, and that Gary has lost his mind. Gary writes all of it down as a denial.

Thirteen. By day, Gary covers the gap. He sends email from Halloran's account, tells the worried wife her husband has been sent to a conference, and carries soup down the basement stairs. Holding a hostage and keeping a secret turns out to be a full-time job, which Gary, freshly unemployed, now has the hours for.

Fourteen. Sal comes to the house, because no one has seen Gary in weeks. Gary talks to him through the screen door and does not let him in. Sal says the thing he came to say. That this is not him. That they miss him. That he is scaring people. He never once uses the word crazy, and he never argues about whether any of it is true.

Fifteen. This is the midpoint, and the mirror he refuses. That same night Royce calls to tell him that the great ones are always abandoned by the small people first, that it is simply the price, and that he must not lose his nerve now of all times. Gary stands in his kitchen with Sal's worry in one ear and Royce's faith in the other, and he chooses. He stops waiting for the world to catch up and goes on the offensive. He picks the man who flatters him over the man who loves him, and he does not look back.
A believer watches him shed his doubters. We watch him let go of the last hand on the rope.

Sixteen. In the basement, on the fourth day, Halloran does the arithmetic that fear teaches. The truth earns him a legal pad and a long night. Agreement earns him a blanket and a glass of water. So he begins to confess. He invents a program, he gives it a name, he points Gary toward a department on the far side of campus. Gary's hand shakes as he writes it down. The insider has finally cracked.
Gary sees a witness. We see a man buying his life one lie at a time.

Seventeen. Gary chases the new thread across the named department, and the further in he goes, the more of his real life quietly finishes falling away. Donna stops looking up when he comes in. The crew stops calling. The money runs out. He reads each loss as one more measure of the cost.

Eighteen. A missing-person report is filed on Halloran. A patient, unglamorous detective named Foss begins retracing his last weeks, which leads, among other places, to the complaint a just-fired janitor had filed against him.
Gary sees someone sent to silence him. We see a man missing and the police doing their job.

Nineteen. Foss comes by and asks Gary a few flat questions on the porch. Gary is calm and a little strange, and she leaves with no evidence and a bad feeling, and she starts pulling the thread of Gary the way Gary pulls his threads, except that hers is real. The net begins to draw in.

Twenty. This is the second doorway of no return. While Gary is out chasing the invented department, Halloran works a hand loose and makes it halfway up the basement stairs before Gary gets home. They struggle. No one is badly hurt, but the spell is broken. Halloran has tasted the door, and Gary knows the police have his name. There is no waiting any of it out now. It ends here, one way or another.

Twenty-one. Gary decides to drag it all into the light himself, before they can bury him with it. He calls Foss back, he calls the radio station, he calls a reporter, and he tells them to come to the university, that he will open the door and show them what is underneath.
A believer leans forward. We brace.

Twenty-two. A small crowd gathers in the old wing, the police among them, and Gary, lit up and certain, has the seal broken on the sub-basement. It is sewage. A ruptured main, a sump pump, a contractor's invoice taped to the wall. His binder, read out loud in the daylight, is a long string of coincidences and chalk. The dark thing at the bottom of everything is, exactly and only, excrement.

Twenty-three. They arrest him for the kidnapping, which is the single real crime in the whole affair, and it is his. Halloran is walked out in a blanket, shaken and alive. From across the parking lot Sal watches them fold Gary into the car and does not come over, because there is nothing left to say. Donna does not come at all.

Twenty-four. The loop starts over. In the back of the cruiser the radio crackles a dispatch code, and the young officer at the wheel repeats the number to his partner. Gary's eyes move. He has heard that number before, in the logbook, in the basement. Of course. It was never the sewage. The sewage was the cover. It goes deeper than he thought. He almost smiles. We hold on his face as the whole thing begins again.

End of beats.

A note on pacing. This is a slow burn by nature, so it can open with a short cold teaser lifted from near the end, played dead straight, before beat one. The strongest candidate is the moment from beat twenty-two: Gary throwing open the basement door for a crowd with total confidence, then a cut away just before we see what is inside. Then we go back to the beginning and let the story walk all the way up to it. This is optional, and it stays a teaser sitting on top of this chronological list, never a reordering of it.

A note on the blade. I have kept it mostly gentle, so a skeptic can laugh, but I left a faint edge. The detective and the crew are right, and also a little sure of themselves, so the viewer who is certain he could never be Gary is given a reason or two to wonder.
