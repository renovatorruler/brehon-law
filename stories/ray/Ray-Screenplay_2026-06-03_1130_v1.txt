RAY

FADE IN:

INT. DELANEY'S TAVERN - NIGHT

Smoke, a low jukebox, a back booth the whole room angles toward. RAY (38, slight, quick-eyed) lets a BIG MAN in a VFW cap run all the way out to the end of his rope.

BIG MAN
Somebody's got to go and stand up for it. That's all I'm saying.

RAY
Name the company that owns the oil under it.

BIG MAN
The —

RAY
You can't. But the man who signed the lease can, and he won't be in any ditch, and neither will his boys. Yours will.

The Big Man's mouth works. Nothing comes. Laughter rolls down the bar. SULLY (40s, soft, devoted) slaps the table.

SULLY
Call the coroner. He's done.

A fresh beer slides over. Under the table, Ray's right fist is bone-white, the whole arm trembling — a fight with nowhere to go. He flattens it on his thigh.

At the end of the bar, TOMMY (22, big, plain, Ray's brother) has watched the whole thing. When the laughter dies he says it, quiet, not unkind.

TOMMY
You've got all the words, Ray. You just never had to use them.

Ray doesn't answer.

EXT. CREEK - DAY (FLASHBACK)

Twenty years back. TOMMY, nine, drags himself up the bank — split lip, one eye swelling shut. A BIGGER BOY stands over him, fists still up, the more shaken of the two. Tommy spits red in the dirt. And grins. He turns and walks back down to take more.

EXT. FARMHOUSE PORCH - CONTINUOUS

Forty yards off, YOUNG RAY (14) sits on the rail with a book on his knee, doing the arithmetic. He does not get up. He turns a page.

INT. FARMHOUSE KITCHEN - MORNING (PRESENT)

A folded Army form, squared on the oilcloth. Tommy smooths it under his thumb. Ray comes in, reads the table.

TOMMY
Went down to the courthouse.

Ray sits, and the machine starts.

RAY
You're a body to them, Tommy. A line in a ledger. You come home in a bag or you don't come home, and it changes nothing, because this was a lie before either of us was born.

Tommy turns his coffee cup, slow, and takes the whole barrage. When Ray runs dry, the kid nods.

TOMMY
I know all that, Ray.

He stands. Goes to pack.

INT. FARMHOUSE - DAY

A government car in the lane. RUTH (60s, iron) takes the telegram on the porch. She does not fall. She reads it once, folds it, and goes inside to strip the beds.

Ray reads it standing in the yard. He does not sit down. He does not make a sound. His face goes cold, and stays that way.

INT. CHURCH - DAY

A flag-draped box. By the door, a photograph: EARL, in an older war's uniform — Tommy's build, Tommy's squint. The line files past Ray with their soft hands. He takes each, gives nothing back.

OLD WOMAN
The Lord never sends more than we can carry, Raymond.

RAY
He didn't send it. A man in an office did. I can get you the name.

He walks out. Across the aisle, ANNIE (late 20s) watches him go — Tommy's girl, black still in her clothes. Her eyes follow Ray, not the box.

INT. RAY'S HOUSE - NIGHT

One bulb. On the table: EARL's revolver, the oilcloth peeled back, a box of shells. Ray loads it. The hands sure. He pockets it. Takes his coat off the hook.

EXT. RECRUITING OFFICE - DAY

Two towns over, a flag in the window. Ray's car at the curb. He crosses to the door — and in the dark glass of it he catches himself: a man, squared up, the loaded gun heavy in his coat.

INT. RECRUITING OFFICE - CONTINUOUS

A tired RECRUITER (late 20s) glances up halfway. Ray stops inside, his hand in his coat, around the grip. His hand on the revolver is dead steady. Not a tremor. For half a breath — the white fist shaking under the tavern table. Then the steady hand again. Something moves behind Ray's eyes. Not a decision. A recognition.

RECRUITER
Help you?

Ray looks at the boy who signed his brother. He takes the revolver out of his coat and sets it on the desk between them. And picks up the pen.

RECRUITER
You enlisting?

RAY
I'm taking my brother's slot.

He signs. Neat, even script.

EXT. JUNE'S PORCH - DUSK

JUNE (30s) stands behind the screen door, arms crossed.

JUNE
You. Enlisting. I fell for the smartest man in three counties — the one who'd never be fool enough to go.

RAY
I know.

JUNE
Then who are you?

He has no answer. For the first time in his life, no words at all. He goes.

EXT. TRAINING GROUND - DAY

Ray in fatigues among younger men, hauling himself over the wire, the obstacle course. A SERGEANT barks. Ray's quick mouth is no use here. He keeps his head down. He learns to move.

EXT. TROOP STATION - DAY

Ray ships out from the same boards Tommy left from. Ruth sees him off, dry-eyed, the way she saw the other men go. The train pulls. There is no getting off it now.

EXT. FIREBASE - TREELINE - DAY (WAR)

Hell. Mortars walking the line, red dirt jumping, a SOLDIER an arm from Ray simply gone. Ray drops behind a berm — and waits for the cold hands. The freeze. The porch. He is certain, in this moment, that he is about to be the coward he always was.

The cold hands don't come.

The roar drops away to almost nothing. And Ray is already moving — low, fast, sure — out into the open, hauling a pinned, screaming kid back by his webbing, his other hand putting rounds down the line. A man who has found the one tool his hands were built for. He drags the kid behind the berm and holds the corner. And under the noise, in his own chest, calm as Sunday:

RAY (V.O.)
There you are.

INT. DELANEY'S TAVERN - NIGHT (LATER)

The same smoke, the same booth. A NEW KID holds the floor with a borrowed certainty.

NEW KID
A rich man's war and a poor man's funeral, and not one of you can even name the company.

SULLY, beside him, spots Ray on a stool by the door — older now, weather in his face — and lights up, hoping for the old show.

NEW KID
You were over there. Go on. Tell me I'm wrong.

The room leans in for the king. Ray looks at the kid a long moment — the fast mouth, the safe little war you win every night without once standing up. And feels no pull at all. He sets a coin on the bar, stands, and crosses to the kid's shoulder — the room braced for the dismantling — and just nods, once, like a man tipping his hat to a stranger.

He walks out into the daylight.

EXT. ANNIE'S DOORSTEP - DAY

Ray on the step. Annie opens the door.

ANNIE
He used to say you were the brave one. The one with all the words.

RAY
He was wrong about the words.

She looks at his hands. Steady. She steps back to let him in.

FADE OUT.
