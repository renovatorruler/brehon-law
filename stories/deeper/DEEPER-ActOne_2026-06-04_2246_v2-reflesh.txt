DEEPER
(working title)

ACT ONE  --  second pass, re-fleshed


FADE IN:

INT. KEPLER HALL - THIRD FLOOR - NIGHT

A bucket on wheels. A gray mop going back and forth across waxed
linoleum, slow, even, almost tender.

The man pushing it is GARY SOBEK, late forties, gray work shirt,
his name stitched over the pocket. A face built for being
forgotten. He mops like a man who has decided the mop is beneath
him and is being gracious about it anyway.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          They think the job is the mopping.

A water fountain on the wall is running, won't shut off. Gary
sets down the mop. Pops the cover with a key off his own ring.
Looks at the guts of it for two seconds and pinches a corroded
float, and the water stops. He does it without thinking, the way
you'd swat a fly. Then he picks the mop back up.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          The mopping is what I let them see.

Down the hall, a night class lets out. Students file past him
without seeing him, the way you don't see a fire extinguisher.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          Every building this size makes a ton of
          waste a day. Paper, food, the other thing.
          They pay men like me to make it disappear.
          And nobody, in the whole history of this
          place, has ever once asked me where it goes.

He waits for the hall to empty. Leans the mop on the wall. Walks,
unhurried, toward the lecture hall they just left.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I ask.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - NIGHT

A big tiered room, dark but for the exit signs. A wall of green
chalkboard, four panels wide. On the last panel, up in a corner,
somebody has left a problem. Dense. An integral sign curled like
a question. Below it a professor's note: LEAVE UP.

Gary comes down the steps the way a priest comes down an aisle.
Sets down a canvas tool bag. Takes out, with care, one piece of
yellow chalk he has brought from home.

He studies the problem. A long time. We cannot tell if he
understands it. Neither, you suspect, can he.

Then he writes. Fast, big, filling the board beneath the problem
with symbols, arrows, a number he circles twice, a word that
might be German. He underlines something. Steps back, chest
going, and looks at what he has made the way a man looks at a
sunrise he personally arranged.

At the bottom he signs it, small and neat:

                              G. ALLAN SOBEK.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          You don't sign the small ones. You sign
          the ones they'll come looking for you over.

He climbs the steps. At the top he turns and takes the board in
one last time, the way you'd look at a child asleep. Then the
janitor turns off the lights on the genius and goes back to his
mop.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - MORNING

Sun. The room fills with undergrads. PROFESSOR LIND, sixty,
cardigan, the gentle exhaustion of a man who has explained the
same thing four thousand times, comes in talking to a grad
student, DEV.

                    DEV
          ...and Hammond's doing your Tuesday section
          next year, so.

                    LIND
          (not unhappy)
          Hammond. Sure. Good for Hammond.

A man being quietly shelved, and at peace with the shelf. He sees
the board. The yellow scrawl under his problem.

                    LIND (CONT'D)
          (mild)
          Oh, for God's sake.

                    DEV
          (grinning)
          The phantom strikes again.

                    LIND
          Third time this month.

He picks up an eraser. Pauses, head tilted, genuinely trying to
parse it. We push in. He is not threatened. He is not amazed. He
is a man reading a menu in a language he doesn't speak, deciding
it isn't worth the trouble.

                    DEV
          What's it say?

                    LIND
          It doesn't.

Three strokes. Gone. Chalk dust in the sunlight. He's already
moving on.

                    LIND (CONT'D)
          Okay. Sequences. Where were we.

PUSH past him, out the door, to the hallway window. Gary is
there. Cart parked. Watching through the glass as his work
vanishes under the old man's hand.

His face does not fall. It does the opposite. A small, sad,
knowing nod. The nod of a man who has just had something
confirmed.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          They don't erase what's wrong. Wrong, they
          laugh at. He didn't even let his students
          see it. Ask yourself why.


INT. MEADOWBROOK CARE CENTER - DAY REROOM - DAY

A pastel room that smells like applesauce and bleach. IRENE
SOBEK, late seventies, neat hair, sits by a window with a jigsaw
two-thirds done. Bright eyes that arrive and leave. Gary sits
beside her, holding a paper cup of the bad coffee, and for the
length of this scene he is simply a son.

An AIDE, twenty-five, kind, comes by with meds in a little cup.

                    IRENE
          (to the aide, proud, conspiratorial)
          This is my son. He's up at the university.

                    AIDE
          (charmed)
          Oh, nice. What do you teach?

A beat. Gary looks at the floor. At his mother. At the floor.

                    GARY
          ...Systems, mostly.

                    AIDE
          Wow.

                    IRENE
          He was tested, you know. As a boy. Mrs.
          Vorhees. She said to me, she said, Irene,
          this one. A mind like this comes along
          once. In front of everybody she said it.

                    GARY
          (gently, an old worn groove)
          Ma.

                    IRENE
          Well she did.

The aide moves on. Irene squeezes a puzzle piece that does not
fit into a slot that does not want it, patient, sure. Then she
looks at Gary's boots.

                    IRENE (CONT'D)
          (a small cloud)
          You take those off at the courthouse, Walt.
          They just did the floors.

Gary goes still. Walt was his father. The custodian. Thirty years
of courthouse floors. He does not correct her. He reaches over
and pats her hand with the same hand his father used to.

                    GARY
          Already did, Ma. Left 'em at the door.

                    IRENE
          (settling, pleased)
          Good boy.

She goes back to the puzzle. Gary watches her place the wrong
piece and not notice, and love her, and we understand the whole
man without a word of it being said.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          You spend your life waiting to be seen by
          the right person. And the one who saw you,
          who really saw you, you can't hold it
          against the world that they're gone.


INT. O'BANNON'S - NIGHT

A real bar. Tradesmen, the game low, peanut shells. A back booth
where GARY holds court over SAL DiPIETRO, fifties, foreman's
build, foreman's patience, and two crew guys, TINO and BIG MIKE,
three beers deep and happy.

On the table, weighed down by a pitcher, a roll of blueprints.

                    SAL
          (unrolling them)
          Just look at it. Two minutes. The mechanical
          on the Whitmore job. Petrocelli's own guy
          stamped it and it's wrong, I can feel it's
          wrong, I just can't say where.

                    GARY
          (waving it off)
          Sal.

                    SAL
          Two minutes. For me.

Gary sighs like it costs him something. Pulls the prints over.
And here is the thing the whole movie turns on: he is good at
this. His finger goes down a riser diagram, stops, traces, stops.

                    GARY
          ...Your return air's fighting your supply
          here. Whoever drew this put the damper
          downstream of the tee. It'll hammer all
          winter and nobody'll know why.

Sal slaps the table, vindicated, delighted, turning to the others.

                    SAL
          SEE? See this guy? I been saying for a
          week something's off in that wing and this
          guy reads it like a menu in thirty seconds.

                    BIG MIKE
          (toasting)
          To Gary.

                    TINO
          (warm, dumb, fatal)
          He shoulda been the engineer. Instead a us
          mutts. Remember back when he took the fall
          for--

                    SAL
          (fast, flat, a door closing)
          Tino.

Tino stops. A history sits down at the table for a second and
then gets up and leaves. Gary doesn't look up from the prints.
Sal watches him with thirty years in his eyes.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (quiet, just to Gary)
          Come on the job. I'm not asking you to be
          nothing you're not. I'm asking you to be
          exactly that. Eight months. Union.

For one second Gary is just a man being handed his own life by
someone who loves him.

Then the prints become evidence.

                    GARY
          You ever notice they don't hire the guys
          who can read these? They hire the guys who
          drew it wrong. You ever notice that?

                    SAL
          (tired)
          It's a damper, Gar.

                    GARY
          Everything's a damper. Till you see the
          whole system.

Sal rolls up his prints. Slow. Puts them under his arm. The offer
is still on the table and they both know it and it is getting
older.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          Sal's the best man I know. Which is exactly
          why they keep him where he is.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY

Fluorescent, formica. Gary in a booth with a spiral notebook,
hand cupped around the page. DONNA, forties, kind eyes, twelve
years behind this counter, arrives with the pot. Refills him
unasked. Sets down a slice of pie he did not order.

                    DONNA
          Marcus made the Dean's List. Second
          semester.

                    GARY
          (not looking up)
          Who's Marcus.

                    DONNA
          (a small beat)
          My son. Marcus. Fort Benning. I've showed
          you the picture maybe ten times.

                    GARY
          Right. Marcus. Good. That's good.

She refills the sugar caddy that does not need it, just to stay
at the table a second longer.

                    DONNA
          You know, all that, whatever you're always
          working on in there. You could do something
          with a head like that. For money, I mean.
          People pay for a head like that.

For half a second the door opens. He looks up at her. Really at
her. And she's right there, holding it.

                    GARY
          That's the thing nobody gets, Donna. The
          ones who'd pay are the ones who can't
          afford for me to be right.

The door closes. Donna nods, the way you nod at weather. Taps the
table twice. Goes.

We catch the top of his page, block capitals, underlined three
times: WHERE DOES IT GO. And below, smaller, like a confession:
and who decided i shouldn't know.

From the counter Donna watches him not eat the pie she brought
him anyway.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          A normal life is a beautiful thing. I think
          about it sometimes. The way you think about
          a country you'll never get a visa for.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - DAY

A concrete pit, older than the building above it. A real
excavation: a section of floor jackhammered open, pumps running,
a smell that arrives before the image and makes you turn your
head.

RAY HALLORAN, fifties, facilities director, tie loosened, stands
at the lip of the hole with a VANGUARD ENVIRO FOREMAN, both of
them looking down into it like men at a grave.

                    HALLORAN
          So it's the lateral.

                    FOREMAN
          Lateral's shot. 1920s clay. It didn't crack,
          Ray, it pancaked. Whole run, building to main.

                    HALLORAN
          (crouching, looking at the pipe with real
           knowledge, almost wistful)
          Look at that bedding. No granular base, they
          just laid it in dirt and prayed. A hundred
          years they got out of it. You gotta respect
          it a little.

                    FOREMAN
          You sound like you miss it.

                    HALLORAN
          (standing, knees)
          I wanted to build 'em. Went to school for
          it, two years. Now I just keep 'em from
          falling down on people.
          (beat, the peace of it)
          It's a living.

A SUPERVISOR, LOPEZ, comes down the stairs with a clipboard,
holds it out to Halloran.

                    LOPEZ
          Sobek again. Two no-calls this week. And he
          was down here Tuesday, in the tape. I want
          him written up.

Halloran takes the clipboard. Looks at the line. Nine years of a
spotless file behind it. He sighs the sigh of a man who carries
other men's weather.

                    HALLORAN
          He's having a rough patch. Leave it with me.

                    LOPEZ
          Ray.

                    HALLORAN
          Leave it with me, Maria. The man's worked
          here longer than your car's been alive.

He initials something that makes the write-up go away. Decent. A
man saving a man, in a basement, over a hole full of the thing
the whole film keeps mistaking for treasure.

High above them, unseen on the stairs, a flashlight beam. Gary.
Watching the director and the contractor murmur over the secret
hole. He cannot hear the words. He does not need to.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          The man who runs the building, down in the
          one room I'm not cleared for, at ten in the
          morning, with a contractor nobody can name.
          You start to feel it then. The shape of it.


INT. GARY'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

Small, clean in the way of a man with too much time. One wall is
becoming Something: a campus map, index cards, the photographed
logbook, lengths of string he has put up and taken down and put
up again, unable to stop.

On the desk, half-buried, a thick rubber-banded manuscript, the
title page yellowed: A UNIFIED THEORY OF SYSTEMS, by G. Allan
Sobek. Beside it, in a cheap frame, a boy of nine with a ribbon
and a science-fair volcano, beaming, certain of everything.

A clock radio glows 2:11 AM. A voice fills the room, late-night
AM, warm and ruined.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          ...and they'll call you paranoid. That's
          the tell, friend. Paranoid's just the word
          the comfortable use for the informed. We
          got a caller, line four, you're on The
          Lateness, go ahead.

Gary sits on the edge of the bed, phone in hand, the way you sit
before you do something. He dials. A beat. Then his own voice
comes out of his own radio on a half-second delay, and it clearly
does something to him to hear himself out there, in the world, at
last.

                    GARY
          (into phone)
          Yeah, hi. First-time caller. I, uh. I clean
          a university. The whole plant. And I started
          noticing where the waste goes. The disposal.
          Off the books. And the more I look the more
          I think I'm not supposed to be looking.

A long professional silence. Gary's knuckles white.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          What's your name, friend.

                    GARY
          ...Gary.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Gary. You sound like a smart man. You sound
          like a man who's been told his whole life
          he's too smart for the room he's in. That
          right?

Gary's eyes go wet. Instantly. Like a struck match. Across the
room the nine-year-old beams from the frame.

                    GARY
          (barely)
          ...Yeah.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          A mind like that doesn't end up with a mop
          by accident, Gary. A mind like that gets
          put somewhere it can't do any damage. You
          ever think of it that way?

He has not. In forty-eight years. We watch the thought enter him
and change the chemistry of his whole face. He stands. He has to.

                    ROYCE (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          Keep looking. And write it down. They can't
          disappear what's already on paper.

                    GARY
          I will. Thank you. I mean it. Thank you.

He hangs up. Stands in the middle of his sad clean apartment, lit
up like a man just told, for the first time since he was nine,
that the worst thing about his life is the best thing about him.

He looks at the wall of string. It no longer looks crazy to him.
It looks like the beginning of something.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - NIGHT

Now Gary's flashlight finds the steel door past the excavation.
Newer than the wall. A keypad. A laminated sign: AUTHORIZED
PERSONNEL - HAZMAT REMEDIATION IN PROGRESS.

He photographs the sign. The door. The keypad. A logbook hangs on
a nail, golf pencil on a string. He flips it. Dates. Times, all
after midnight. A company name: VANGUARD ENVIRO.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          You want to hide a thing, you don't put up
          a sign that says secret. You put up a sign
          so boring the eye slides off it.

From behind the door: a deep, wet, industrial GURGLE. A pump
kicking on. Liquid moving in volume.

Gary goes still. To him it is the machine itself, finally,
breathing. He presses his ear to the cold steel, reverent, while
three inches away a sump pump moves four hundred gallons of raw
sewage out of a building that has, boringly, catastrophically,
backed up.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          That night I stopped being a man who cleaned
          a building, and became a man it was hiding
          things from.


SERIES OF SHOTS - THE WORK, AND THE COST

-- The apartment wall, no longer embarrassed. Full. The string
goes where it wants. The manuscript sits unopened under it all,
a foundation no one is allowed to inspect.

-- Gary in his parked car at 1 a.m., photographing a white
VANGUARD van through a used long lens he cannot quite work.

-- A Sunday. Meadowbrook. Irene by the window with the same
puzzle, alone this week, watching the door that doesn't open.

-- Delmore's. A fresh slice of pie going cold in an empty booth.
Donna clears it without a word and does not bring another.

-- O'Bannon's, the back booth. The crew, minus one. Sal looks at
the empty side of the table, then at the door, then back at his
beer.

-- A job site at dawn. A union foreman calling a name down a line
of hard hats. "Sobek? Anybody seen Sobek?" Nobody has. Sal crosses
the name off a list with a carpenter's pencil and does not throw
the pencil, though he wants to.

-- The notebook, a page headed in that grand neat hand: A
PRELIMINARY MAP OF THE THING. Gary looks at it. Crosses out
PRELIMINARY.


INT. ROYCE'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

A garden unit that smells the way the radio sounds. Stacks. A
second life's worth of paper. A reel-to-reel. A career, boxed,
the lid off.

ROYCE is smaller than his voice. Sixties, a good blazer gone
shiny, the dignity of a man holding a door for a building already
condemned. He pours Gary coffee into a real cup and saucer, the
one formal object in the room.

Gary's binder is open between them. Royce turns the pages slowly,
and does Gary the one thing nobody ever has. He reads it like
it's real.

                    ROYCE
          (tapping)
          The van. Three nights, all after one.

                    GARY
          Three I'm sure of.

                    ROYCE
          And this. "Facilities contingency." Goes up
          every October.

                    GARY
          Six years running.

Royce sits back. Takes off his glasses. The performance, if it is
one, is seamless, and we will never be entirely sure.

                    ROYCE
          You know what they did to me, Gary?

                    GARY
          ...The post.

                    ROYCE
          The post. The journals. The friends. You
          find out fast who your friends were. They
          don't argue with you. Nobody warns you about
          that part. They don't say you're wrong. They
          just start being worried about you. Real
          gentle. Real kind. Worried.

Gary thinks, hard, of Sal rolling up the prints. Of Donna's two
taps on the table. Something in him goes cold and certain.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          And here's what I need you to hear, son.
          Because I wish to God somebody'd said it to
          me in time.

He leans in. Takes Gary's wrist. It lands like therapy. It is the
single warmest moment in the film.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          It is not your fault that you see it. You
          didn't do anything wrong. Seeing it is not
          the sickness. They just need you to think
          it is.

Gary, undone, looks down at his own handwriting so it cannot be
seen what his face is doing.

                    GARY
          (thick)
          I should go. I got the east wing at six.

Royce smiles, kind, and does not say that Gary was fired from the
east wing two days ago, because Gary has not told him, because
some things you keep even from the one man who believes you.

                    ROYCE
          Course you do.
          (gentle)
          Take it to whoever runs the building. Look
          him in the eye. And Gary. Watch his hands.
          A liar protects his middle.

Gary gathers the binder, holds it to his chest, goes out into the
dawn a soldier. Behind him, alone, Royce sits with his cold real
coffee in its real cup. We hold one beat too long. Long enough to
wonder whether he believes a word, or whether being believed in
is just the last warm thing he has. He turns on the radio. Static,
then music. He starts to wash the one cup.


INT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - HALLORAN'S OFFICE - DAY

Carpet tiles. A dying plant. Halloran on the phone, holding up a
finger, mouthing sorry. Gary sits across the desk, binder squared,
composed.

                    HALLORAN
          (into phone)
          ...the chiller in B, not the, Carol, the
          one we, yes. Friday. Okay. Love you too.
          (hangs up, exhales)
          Gary. Sit, sit. How you doing. Honestly.

The decency is real. He carried this man. Gary slides the binder
across. Sixty pages. A tab system.

                    GARY
          I need you to read all of it. Tonight. And
          I need it on the record that I came to you
          first.

Halloran opens it. The campus map with things circled. The photo
of the door. The budget line, highlighted, three exclamation
points Gary clearly agonized over. We watch a decent tired man
understand, in real time, that his best floor man in a decade has
come unscrewed, and that it is now, somehow, his problem, on a
Thursday.

                    HALLORAN
          Gary. The sub-level. The smell. That's a
          sewage main. Hundred-year-old lateral, it
          collapsed, Vanguard's been pumping it five
          weeks because we can't close the building
          during midterms. It's a hole, Gary. It's a
          hole full of what you think it's full of.

A beat. Gary, gently, with enormous compassion, the way you'd
correct a frightened man:

                    GARY
          That's exactly what you'd have to say.

                    HALLORAN
          ...What?

                    GARY
          No, it's perfect. Disgusting and boring and
          nobody wants to look. It's a genuinely
          excellent cover. I respect it. I'm not being
          sarcastic.

Halloran closes the binder. Closes his eyes. Opens them. Picks
his words like a man crossing a creek on stones.

                    HALLORAN
          You've missed eleven shifts. Found in three
          locked areas. Lopez says you shot her car.
          I have carried you nine years because you're
          the best I ever had and your file's clean
          back to the day you started. I can't anymore.
          I'm sorry. I have to let you go.

And there it is. Gary does not erupt. Gary does the small sad
knowing nod. The one from the hallway window. Confirmed.

                    GARY
          (quietly, almost relieved)
          Two days.

                    HALLORAN
          What?

                    GARY
          I gave you the binder Tuesday. It's Thursday.

He stands, gathers the evidence they tried to bury by firing the
man who carried it. At the door he turns. Not angry. Something
almost like pity.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          You did it too fast, Ray. A smarter man
          would've waited a month.

He leaves. Halloran sits alone, looks at the door, looks at his
dead plant, picks up the phone to call somebody about this, and
cannot for the life of him think who.


EXT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - PARKING LOT - NIGHT

Late. A half-empty lot under sodium light. Gary in his parked
car. Has been for hours. The binder rides shotgun like a
passenger.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I drove home. I want that known. I drove
          home first.

A door. HALLORAN comes out, suit bag over his shoulder, tie off,
just a tired man who wants his couch. Beeps a sensible sedan.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I made it to my own street. Sat in my own
          driveway. Thought about the eggs I'd make.
          The bed I'd get into.

Gary's hand on his keys. Not turning them.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I thought: if I go inside and sleep,
          then tomorrow I'm just a guy who got fired.
          A guy whose mother's wrong about him. And
          I could not. God help me, I could not be
          that guy.

Halloran's headlights. He backs out. Gary's engine turns over.

CUT TO:


INT. GARY'S CAR / EXT. SUBURBAN STREET - NIGHT

Gary follows the sedan two cars back, hands at ten and two,
obeying every traffic law on his way to break the only one that
counts. Sweating. Terrified. A man who has never done anything in
his life.

The sedan pulls into a normal sad nice cul-de-sac. Gary parks
across the street. Kills the lights. Halloran gets out. Yawns.
Grabs his suit bag. Heads for his door, keys out, thumbing mail,
the most defenseless animal on earth: a man at the end of his own
driveway.

In the car, Gary's breathing. The dome light as he opens his
door. A tire iron in his hand, which he looks at like he doesn't
recognize his own arm.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I keep telling people the next part isn't
          the important part.

He crosses the street. Fast. Behind Halloran now. Halloran
half-turns at the sound, mail in hand, "can I help y--"

SMASH CUT TO:

INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - NIGHT

A water heater. A furnace. A drain in the concrete floor. And
RAY HALLORAN, zip-tied to a kitchen chair, a thin line of blood
at his hairline, gagged with what is unmistakably a clean, folded
dish towel. His eyes huge. His chest heaving. The decent man.
The one who saved him. The disappointed life that worked.

Across the basement, under a bare bulb, GARY stands holding the
tire iron in both hands, staring at the man in the chair with the
dawning, nauseous, total horror of someone realizing they are
awake.

Long silence. The furnace ticks.

Gary sets the tire iron down on the dryer. Very carefully. As if
quiet could undo it. He pulls a second chair around. Sits, facing
his prisoner, knees almost touching. Takes out the spiral
notebook. Clicks the pen.

And Gary Sobek, forty-eight, unemployed, the smartest man he
knows, leans toward the terrified stranger he has chained in his
basement, and says, with the shining patience of a man who has at
last gotten someone to sit still and listen:

                    GARY
          Okay. Start at the beginning. Where does it
          go.

Halloran screams into the dish towel.

Gary writes it down.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT ONE.
