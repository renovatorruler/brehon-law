DEEPER
(working title)

ACT TWO  --  second pass, the race


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY ONE - MORNING

Gray light through a dirty window well. Overnight the basement has
become something between an office and a cell: a card table, the
binder, the wall map taped to cinderblock, a space heater angled
with awful courtesy at the prisoner.

RAY HALLORAN, zip-tied to the kitchen chair, the dish towel down
around his neck now. Gary lifts a paper cup of water with a bent
straw to his lips.

                    GARY
          Small sips. You'll cramp.

                    HALLORAN
          (drinks, then, low and fast)
          Listen to me. Carol calls the police this
          morning. By tonight there are people looking.
          You have hours, Gary. Hours. Drive me to a
          hospital, say I fell, and I back your play, I
          swear to God.

That word, hours, goes into Gary like a clock starting. He glances
at the window well, the daylight, the world out there beginning to
move. He has no plan. He grabbed this man on a sidewalk two nights
ago with no plan at all, and the only way out he can see now is
through: get it, get the truth, fast, before the hours run out. He
clicks his pen.

                    GARY
          Then we go quick. The contingency line. The
          money. Where does it go.

                    HALLORAN
          It goes to fixing the SEWER, Gary, it's a
          collapsed lateral, it's the most boring--

                    GARY
          (writing: SUBJECT STALLING)
          We don't have time for the boring version.
          I'm trying to help you. They will hang this
          on you too, you know that? The disposal? You
          signed for it. You're in it whether you
          talk or not.

Halloran stares at him. A sane man watching a drowning man explain
that the water is a conspiracy. We see it. Halloran sees it. The
difference is which of them is tied to the chair.


INT. KEPLER HALL - FACILITIES OFFICE - DAY ONE - AFTERNOON

LOPEZ stands over a desk phone on speaker. A second supervisor,
older, BRINKMAN, leans in the doorway. Halloran's office, dark
behind them.

                    LOPEZ
          He's not answering. Carol's not answering.
          And I called the regional office about this
          summit he supposedly got sent to.

                    BRINKMAN
          And?

                    LOPEZ
          There's no summit. Nobody sent Ray anywhere.
          He emailed her he's in Columbus and there's
          no Columbus.

A beat. Two ordinary people at the front edge of understanding that
something is wrong.

                    BRINKMAN
          ...Call campus police. And Lopez. When they
          ask who had a problem with him.

                    LOPEZ
          (already there)
          Yeah. I know who.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY ONE - NIGHT

The TV on low. Gary at Halloran's laptop, sending the cover email,
getting the texture right, "love you, R," a man forging a life.

On the TV, the local news. Weather. A water-main story. A dog that
saved a family. The ordinary surface of the world, and not one
word, anywhere, about a leak, a cover-up, a secret under a
university. Gary watches it with a small, growing outrage.

                    GARY
          (to the empty kitchen)
          Nothing. They've got the whole thing wrapped
          and nobody's even looking.

He doesn't yet understand that the world is about to look very
hard, in exactly the wrong direction.


INT. RIVERSIDE PRECINCT - FRONT DESK - DAY TWO - MORNING

CAROL HALLORAN, coat over her robe, two grown KIDS flanking her,
one on the phone. She leans across the counter at a DESK SERGEANT.

                    CAROL
          His car is in the driveway. His car has not
          moved. He didn't pack his pills. He texts me
          from the bathroom of his own house and now
          there is nothing, two days of nothing, and
          you keep saying men leave, my husband did not
          leave, my husband is gone.

The car. The pills. The sergeant stops treating it like a man who
walked out and starts treating it like a man who was taken. He
pulls a different form.

                    DESK SERGEANT
          Last contact.

                    CAROL
          An email I don't believe. About a conference
          that doesn't exist.

He picks up the phone, and somewhere in the building a machine that
does not lose interest begins, quietly, to turn.


INT. GARY'S CAR - DAY TWO - NIGHT

A dark lot. Gary hunched over the prepaid, the manuscript-grade
focus, talking to Royce.

                    GARY
          He gave me something. A name. The Founders'
          account. He says the money never touches a
          public budget, it runs through Advancement
          as gift money.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          (a man receiving scripture)
          Advancement. Of course. Of course it's the
          donor side, that's where the real money has
          no daylight on it. Gary. Do you understand
          what your man just handed you?

                    GARY
          He keeps trying to take it back. He says he
          made it up. He says he's scared.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          They always say they made it up. The second
          after they tell the truth, the fear comes
          and they recant. You push past the recant.
          You're close. You can feel you're close,
          can't you.

Gary can. God help him, he can.


EXT./INT. GARY'S NEIGHBORHOOD - DAY THREE - DAY

Gary, hood up, walks to his mailbox, and stops.

Stapled to the telephone pole at the corner: a flyer. HALLORAN'S
face, the good ID-badge photo, smiling. MISSING. A number. A
reward.

And down the block, a second one. And on the next pole, a third.
His own quiet street, papered with the face of the man in his
basement.

Gary stands very still. To him, the street tilts and reveals its
true shape: they are papering the neighborhood, they have mobilized,
they are frightened, and frightened is what they get when somebody
is close. He pulls one flyer down, folds it, pockets it. Evidence.

                    GARY (V.O., as he walks back)
          That's when I understood the size of it. You
          don't put a man's face on every pole on a
          street unless you need everyone on that street
          looking for one particular person.

He is right about that. He has the particular person wrong.

Inside, the TV. CAROL HALLORAN, on the local news now, a microphone
in her face, her voice coming apart.

                    CAROL (ON TV)
          Ray, if you can hear this. If somebody has
          you. He takes a pill in the morning and a
          pill at night and he has not had either one
          in three days, please, he is not a strong
          man, he just looks--

Gary turns it off. For one second his hand stays on the dark screen,
and something moves in him, the man under the mission, a flicker.

Then he files it where it has to go to survive: she doesn't know
what her husband is part of. And if she's on television, then they
put her there. He goes to the basement door.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY THREE - DUSK

SAL on the porch. Gary holds the door the familiar eight inches.

                    SAL
          Your mother's home called me. You're listed
          as her contact and you missed two Sundays and
          they didn't know who else to try.

That lands somewhere Royce can't reach.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          And a cop came to the site. Asking about a
          guy named Sobek. Whether he's the type to do
          something stupid. I told him no. I stood
          there and told a cop no.
          (beat, the whole thirty years)
          So now I'm asking you, and I'm not gonna
          argue whatever's in your head, I never could
          win that. I'm just asking. As your friend.
          Whatever you're holding. Put it down. Come
          back.

It lands. The man under the mission surfaces, reaches, almost.

From below them both, a sound. A chair leg, scraping concrete.
Twice.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (alert)
          What's that.

                    GARY
          (the door already closing)
          Furnace. I gotta go, Sal.

The door shuts. Sal stands looking at it, and goes from worried to
afraid, and walks to his truck not knowing he just stood three feet
from the answer and chose to believe his friend.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY THREE - NIGHT

Gary at the counter, shaking, Sal's voice still in the room. Right
at the edge of putting it down. His hand finds the prepaid.

He calls the only other number in it.

                    GARY
          (unsteady)
          My oldest friend came. He thinks I'm sick. A
          cop's asking about me by name. Maybe I'm--
          maybe this is--

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          (gentle, fatal)
          Stop. Listen to me, because it cost me
          everything to learn this. The small ones leave
          first. Not because they're bad. Because what
          you're carrying is too big for them and it
          frightens them. That's not your friend failing
          you. That's just the size of the thing. And
          the cop. Gary. They only send the cop when
          you're close enough to matter.

Gary closes his eyes. Chooses. We watch him choose. The man who
loves him, or the man who needs him.

                    ROYCE (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          Finish it. Before they can bury it. You're so
          close.

                    GARY
          (the crack sealing)
          ...Yeah. Okay. I'm not gonna lose my nerve.

He hangs up, and he doesn't look sick anymore. He looks decided. He
opens the basement door, and the last ordinary thing in him stays
up in the kitchen, alone, where he set it down.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY FOUR - DAY

Halloran is thinner, grayer, a bruise of fatigue under each eye. He
has done the arithmetic captivity teaches: the truth earns another
long night and the legal pad; agreement earns water, a blanket, the
heater up. So he gives Gary what Gary wants, and he has gotten
frighteningly good at it.

                    HALLORAN
          (low, not meeting his eyes)
          The Founders' account routes through three
          shell foundations. I set up the wire codes
          myself, years ago, I didn't know what they
          were for. There's a man in Advancement. Voss.
          He's the one you want. He's the one who'd
          know about the, the material. Where it really
          goes.

It is, every word, the desperate fiction of a man buying a night
with the only currency that works. Gary writes with a hand that
shakes.

                    GARY
          (reverent)
          Voss. Advancement. The shells.
          (looking up, wet-eyed)
          You did good, Ray. You did a brave thing just
          now. I want you to know that. History's going
          to know that.

Halloran turns his face to the cinderblock so Gary can't see what a
man looks like the moment he understands the lie has become a life
sentence, because now he can never stop telling it.


EXT. UNIVERSITY ADVANCEMENT BUILDING - DAY FOUR - NIGHT

Glass and brick, dark for the night. Gary, in his good shirt, a
visitor's tag he printed at home clipped to his chest, walks the
exterior like a man casing a vault. He photographs a loading dock.
A keypad. A donor wall of engraved names, which he shoots, panel by
panel, certain one of them is the thread.

Inside, through the glass, the most boring room on earth: cubicles,
a phone-bank, a banner reading EVERY GIFT BUILDS THE FUTURE. A lone
DEVELOPMENT OFFICER works late, eats a sad desk salad, makes a call
asking an old alum to consider a bequest.

Gary watches the man through the glass with the eyes of a man
watching the enemy's nerve center. We watch a guy hit his quota.

A SECURITY GUARD rounds the corner of the building. Gary turns his
back, smooth, a man who has learned a little, and walks, unhurried,
the visitor tag and the racing heart, out to the dark and the car,
carrying photographs of a fundraising office he is certain is the
bottom of the world.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY FOUR - NIGHT

The corner booth, empty. DONNA wipes it down out of habit. She takes
the folded job clipping from her apron, soft now at the creases,
looks at it a long moment.

Then she drops it in the bus tub with the dirty plates, turns the
booth's little card from RESERVED to OPEN, and seats a family of
four, and pours their coffee, and laughs at the dad's joke. That is
how a person stops waiting.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY FIVE - DAY

A plain sedan at the curb. DETECTIVE FOSS, forties, unhurried, on the
porch with a notebook. Gary holds the door the eight inches.

                    FOSS
          You filed a complaint against Ray Halloran.
          Then he fired you. Then he vanished. Your
          name keeps floating to the top of my list,
          Mr. Sobek, and I've learned to trust the
          float.

                    GARY
          I never got past the lobby the day they let me
          go. Check your turnstile log. It'll have me
          and it'll have the time.

True, and exact, which is what makes him good at this. Foss writes
nothing. She is reading the house behind him: the drawn curtains,
the smell of a man living wrong, the dead grass.

                    FOSS
          Mind if I use your bathroom. Long way back to
          the precinct.

A beat. Below them, three feet of floor, then Halloran.

                    GARY
          (even)
          Main's out on the whole street. They're
          digging it up. No water in the house.

Foss holds his eyes one beat past comfortable. Then nods, pockets
the notebook, steps down off the porch.

                    FOSS
          Take care of yourself.

Gary sees: they sent someone, gently, to measure how much he knows.
We see: a detective with nothing solid and a very bad feeling. She
gets in her car. She does not start it. She writes his name and
underlines it, and begins to pull the thread of him, the way he
pulls his threads, except hers is real, and it is short.


INT./EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - DAY FIVE - NIGHT

Gary's car is gone. INSIDE, the basement.

Halloran, alone, has spent the hours since the woman's voice came
through the floor doing the only thing left a man who has glimpsed a
door: working his raw right hand against the wet plastic of the tie
until, finally, it is slick enough.

His hand comes free.

Up the stairs on rubber legs, through the kitchen, fumbling the
chain, the night air actually on his face, one foot on the porch--

Headlights swing across the lawn. Gary's car.

A bad, brief, terrible scramble in the doorway, two soft men who
have never hit anyone, and it ends with Halloran back inside, the
door shut, both of them on the floor, gasping, neither hurt and both
undone.

Halloran has tasted the porch. And Gary, his back against the door,
knows now what he has refused to know for five days: that a detective
has his name and a hostage has felt the night air, and that the slow,
careful, secret version of this is finished. The hours Halloran
promised him on the first morning are gone.

He looks at Halloran ruined on the floor, at the binder, at the
photographs of the donor wall, and a terrible light comes up in him,
the light of a drowning man who has decided the only way to live is
to make everyone watch him go under.

                    GARY
          (quiet, almost calm)
          Okay. We can't keep it quiet anymore. So we
          stop trying.
          (he is already seeing it)
          We make them bury it in front of everybody.
          We go public.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT TWO.
