DEEPER
(working title)

ACT ONE  --  fourth pass, humanized


FADE IN:

INT. KEPLER HALL - THIRD FLOOR - NIGHT

A bucket on wheels. A gray mop going back and forth across waxed
linoleum, slow, even, almost tender.

The man pushing it is GARY SOBEK, late forties, gray work shirt,
his name stitched over the pocket. A careful, contained man.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I want to say one thing up front, so we
          understand each other. I'm not a janitor.

A water fountain on the wall runs, won't shut off. Gary sets down
the mop. Pops the cover with a key off his own ring, looks at the
guts of it for two seconds, pinches a corroded float. The water
stops. He picks the mop back up.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I'm a man who is, at the moment, for reasons
          mostly not his fault, doing a janitor's job.

Down the hall a night class lets out. Students stream past him
without seeing him. One nearly walks into the cart, doesn't
apologize, doesn't register a person was there.

Gary watches them go. No bitterness you could name.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - NIGHT

A big dark tiered room. A wall of green chalkboard. On the last
panel, up in a corner, a problem somebody left up. Dense. An
integral sign curled like a question. A note beneath it: LEAVE UP.

Gary comes down the steps unhurried. Sets down a canvas tool bag.
Takes out, with a little ceremony, one piece of yellow chalk he
brought from home. He glances back up at the doors. He's got maybe
twenty minutes before the lock-up sweep, and a janitor caught
chalking a professor's board is a janitor with a problem.

He studies the problem. A long while. We can't tell if he
understands it. There's a real chance he half does. A better chance
he doesn't.

Then he writes. Fast, filling the board beneath it with symbols,
arrows, a number he circles twice. Somewhere above, a door bangs.
He freezes, chalk up, for a five-count. Nothing. He finishes,
faster now, steps back, breathing a little hard. Proud of it. A
little afraid of it.

He almost signs it. He doesn't. He gathers his bag, climbs the
steps, stops at the top for one more look, then kills the lights
and goes back to the mop.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - MORNING

Sun. Undergrads filing in. PROFESSOR LIND, sixty, cardigan, the
gentle fatigue of a man who has explained the same thing four
thousand times, comes in mid-sentence with a grad student, DEV.

                    DEV
          ...and Hammond's taking your Tuesday section
          next year, so. They told you, right?

                    LIND
          (no wound at all)
          They told me. Good for Hammond.

He reaches the board. Sees the yellow writing under his problem.
Doesn't break stride.

                    DEV
          Phantom again?

                    LIND
          Mm.

He doesn't read it. Glances at it like a wrong number and wipes it
off, three strokes, talking the whole time.

                    LIND (CONT'D)
          Okay. Sequences. Somebody tell me where we
          left Cauchy.

Chalk dust hangs in the sun. He's already back in the lecture.

PUSH past him, out the door, to the hallway window. Gary. Cart
parked. He watched the whole thing through the glass.

He doesn't nod. He doesn't narrate it. He stands there a second
longer than a working man should, holding a wet mop, while the
thing he made last night becomes a smear, then nothing.

His free hand drifts an inch toward the push-bar of the door, as if
to go in and say wait. It stops there. The bell's rung, the room is
full of people who get to be in it, and he is a man with a mop. He
pushes the cart on.


INT. MEADOWBROOK CARE CENTER - DAY ROOM - DAY

Pastel, applesauce and bleach. IRENE SOBEK, late seventies, neat
hair, a jigsaw two-thirds done, bright eyes that come and go. Gary
beside her with a paper cup of bad coffee. For this scene he is
simply a son.

An AIDE, twenties, kind, brings meds in a little cup.

                    IRENE
          (proud, to the aide)
          This is my son. He's up at the university.

                    AIDE
          Oh, nice. What do you do up there?

A beat. Gary looks at the floor. At his mother.

                    GARY
          Maintenance. Systems.

                    AIDE
          Important stuff.

                    IRENE
          He was tested, you know. As a boy. Mrs.
          Vorhees said to me, in front of everybody,
          she said, Irene, this one. A mind like this
          comes along once.

                    GARY
          (an old groove, gentle)
          Ma.

                    IRENE
          Well she did.

The aide moves on. Irene presses a puzzle piece that doesn't fit
into a slot that doesn't want it. Then she clouds, looks at his
boots.

                    IRENE (CONT'D)
          You take those off at the courthouse, Walt.
          They just did the floors.

Gary goes still. Walt was his father. Custodian. Thirty years of
courthouse floors. He doesn't correct her. He pats her hand.

                    GARY
          Already did, Ma. Left 'em at the door.

                    IRENE
          (settling)
          Good boy.

She goes back to the puzzle, places the wrong piece, doesn't
notice. He watches her, and lets her.


INT. O'BANNON'S - NIGHT

A real bar. Tradesmen, the game low, peanut shells. A back booth.
GARY with SAL DiPIETRO, fifties, foreman's build and patience, and
two crew guys, TINO and BIG MIKE, three beers in and happy. A roll
of blueprints under a pitcher.

                    SAL
          (unrolling them)
          Two minutes. The mechanical on the Whitmore
          job. Petrocelli's own guy stamped it and
          it's wrong, I can feel it's wrong, I can't
          say where.

                    GARY
          Sal.

                    SAL
          For me.

Gary sighs like it costs him. Pulls the prints over. And here is
the thing the movie turns on: he is good at this. His finger goes
down a riser diagram. Stops. Traces. Stops.

                    GARY
          Your return air's fighting your supply.
          Whoever drew it put the damper downstream of
          the tee. It'll hammer all winter and nobody'll
          know why.

Sal slaps the table, turning to the others.

                    SAL
          SEE? A week I been saying something's off in
          that wing, this guy reads it like a menu.

                    BIG MIKE
          (toasting)
          To Gary.

                    TINO
          (warm, dumb)
          Shoulda been the engineer. 'Stead a us mutts.
          Remember when he took the fall--

                    SAL
          (fast, flat)
          Tino.

Tino stops. Something old sits down at the table and gets up and
leaves. Sal looks at Gary. Thirty years in it.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (quiet, just to him)
          You know who they hand the engineering to?
          Not the guy who can read the print. Come on
          the job. Eight months, union. I'm not asking
          you to be anything you're not. I'm asking you
          to be exactly that.

For one second Gary is just a man being handed his life by someone
who loves him.

                    GARY
          (a smile, and real bitterness, and no theory
           in it)
          They don't hire the guy who can read it, Sal.
          They hire the guy whose father knew a guy.
          That's the whole secret. There's no secret.

                    SAL
          So come read prints for me. I'm the guy.

                    GARY
          (looking away)
          I got something going right now. I can't.

Sal rolls up the prints. Slow. Puts them under his arm. The offer
stays on the table, getting older, between two men who love each
other and one of whom is already gone.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY

Fluorescent, formica. Gary in a booth with a spiral notebook, hand
cupped around the page out of habit. DONNA, forties, kind eyes,
twelve years on this counter, brings the pot, refills him unasked,
sets down pie he didn't order.

                    DONNA
          Marcus made the Dean's List. Second semester.

                    GARY
          (surfacing)
          ...Who's Marcus.

                    DONNA
          (small beat)
          My son. Fort Benning. I've shown you the
          picture maybe ten times.

                    GARY
          Right. Good. That's good, Donna.

She tops off a sugar caddy that doesn't need it, to stay another
second.

She slides a folded square of newspaper across the table, soft at
the creases from a few days in her apron.

                    DONNA
          Community college out on Route 9. Hiring a
          facilities supervisor. Run the whole plant,
          benefits, the works. I circled the number.
          You'd walk into that.

Half a second, the door opens. He looks at the clipping. At her.
She's right there, holding it out, having carried it for days.

                    GARY
          They pay for the right diploma to say what
          they already decided, Donna. Not the guy who
          actually knows. I'm too old to audition for
          that.

He doesn't pick it up. The door closes. She lets it sit one more
second, hoping. Then she folds it back into her apron, two taps on
the table, and goes. From the counter she watches him not eat the
pie she brought him anyway.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          A normal life is a beautiful thing. I think
          about it more than you'd guess. The way you
          think about a country you'll never get the
          visa for.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - DAY

A concrete pit older than the building above it. A section of
floor jackhammered open. Pumps running. A smell that arrives
before the picture and turns your head.

RAY HALLORAN, fifties, facilities director, tie loosened, stands
at the lip of the hole with a VANGUARD ENVIRO FOREMAN, both
looking down like men at a grave.

                    HALLORAN
          So it's the lateral.

                    FOREMAN
          Pancaked. Whole run, building to main. 1920s
          clay, no granular base. They laid it in dirt
          and prayed.

                    HALLORAN
          (crouching, real knowledge, almost wistful)
          Hundred years they got out of it, though. You
          gotta respect it a little.

                    FOREMAN
          You sound like you miss it.

                    HALLORAN
          (standing, knees)
          Wanted to build 'em. Two years of school for
          it. Now I keep 'em from falling on people.
          It's a living.

LOPEZ, a supervisor, comes down with a clipboard.

                    LOPEZ
          Sobek again. Two no-calls. And he was down
          here Tuesday, on the tape. I want him written
          up.

Halloran takes the clipboard. One line, and nine years of a clean
file behind it. He sighs the sigh of a man who carries other men's
weather.

                    HALLORAN
          He's having a rough patch. Leave it with me.

                    LOPEZ
          Ray.

                    HALLORAN
          Leave it with me, Maria. Man's worked here
          longer than your car's been alive.

He initials the write-up out of existence.


INT. GARY'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

Small. Too clean. A man with too much time. The walls are bare. On
the desk a thick rubber-banded manuscript, title page yellowed: A
UNIFIED THEORY OF SYSTEMS, by G. Allan Sobek. Beside it, framed, a
boy of nine with a ribbon and a science-fair volcano, beaming,
certain of everything.

A clock radio: 2:11 AM. A voice, late-night AM, warm and ruined.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          ...they'll call you bitter. A man who blames
          the world for his own failures. We got a
          caller, line four, you're on The Lateness.

Gary, in his undershirt, not even meaning to, picks up the phone
and dials. He's lonely. It's two in the morning. That's all this
is, at first.

                    GARY
          (into phone)
          Yeah. Hi. First-time caller. I just had your
          show on. I work at a university. I clean it.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          You clean it.

                    GARY
          And I went to school. Some. I read. And I
          guess I called because you said before, the
          thing about being smart and ending up
          nowhere, and--

He stops, embarrassed, a grown man almost crying to a radio.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          What's your name, friend.

                    GARY
          ...Gary.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Gary. Let me ask you something, and really
          sit with it. A mind like yours. A man who can
          do what you can do. You ever wonder whether
          ending up with a mop was an accident?

Silence. The sentence goes into him like a key into a lock he
didn't know was there.

                    ROYCE (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          Or whether a mind like that is exactly the
          kind they make sure stays where it can't do
          any damage.

Gary stands up. He has to. Across the room the nine-year-old beams
from the frame. For the first time in forty years the gap between
that boy and this apartment has an explanation that is not *you
weren't good enough.*

                    GARY
          (barely)
          ...Yeah. I never thought of it like that.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Most don't. They're not allowed to.
          (warm)
          You keep your eyes open down there, Gary. And
          you call me back.

He hangs up. Stands alone in his bare clean apartment, holding a
feeling he has never had and already can't live without: that it
was never his fault.

He looks at the bare wall. For the first time it looks like it's
missing something.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - NIGHT

Gary's flashlight. He's past the excavation now, where he isn't
cleared to be. A steel door, newer than the wall. A keypad. A sign:
AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL - HAZMAT REMEDIATION IN PROGRESS. A logbook on
a nail, entries all after midnight, a company name: VANGUARD ENVIRO.

He photographs the door. The sign. The logbook, page by page.

Then, up the corridor, a sound. Boots on concrete. A second
flashlight beam swinging this way.

Gary kills his light. There's nowhere to be, and he is somewhere he
will be fired for being. He folds himself behind the pump housing,
into the dark and the smell, and stops breathing.

A VANGUARD WORKER, coveralls, respirator around his neck, comes to
the steel door and punches a code. The door cracks, a wall of warm
stink rolls out, and through the gap, for one second, Gary sees:
black, a churn of something moving, the orange snake of a pump hose.
The worker steps through. The door booms shut.

We know what's in there. We watched Halloran explain it. Gary,
crouched in the dark with his heart going, knows only that a man in
a respirator just badged into a sealed room at two in the morning,
and that he was nearly caught seeing it.

He waits a full minute. Then slips out the way he came. His hands
are shaking, and it isn't fear. They shake the way they'd shake the
first time anyone told him he mattered.


SERIES OF SHOTS - THE WORK, AND THE COST

-- The bare wall, bare no longer. A campus map. Photos. String. It
grows a little every night, each piece reasonable on its own, until
the wall isn't.

-- Gary in his parked car at 1 a.m., photographing a white VANGUARD
van through a used lens he can't quite work.

-- Sunday. Meadowbrook. Irene at the window with her puzzle, alone
this week, watching a door that doesn't open.

-- Delmore's. A slice of pie going cold in an empty booth. Donna
clears it and does not bring another.

-- A job site at dawn. A foreman calling down a line of hardhats.
"Sobek? Anybody seen Sobek?" Nobody has. Sal crosses a name off a
list with a carpenter's pencil and does not throw the pencil,
though he wants to.


INT. ROYCE'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

A garden unit that smells the way the radio sounds. Stacks. A
career, boxed, lid off. ROYCE in person is smaller than his voice,
sixties, a good blazer gone shiny. He pours Gary coffee into a real
cup and saucer, the one formal object in the room.

Gary doesn't sit. He sets the binder down on the stacks like a man
laying down a weight.

                    GARY
          I've got it all. All of it. And I don't know
          what to do with it. Tell me what to do.

He didn't come to be believed. He's past that. He came for an
order. Royce takes his time. Opens the binder, turns the pages
slowly, and does Gary the one thing no one ever has: he reads it
like it's real.

                    ROYCE
          (tapping)
          The van. Three nights, all after one.

                    GARY
          Three I'm sure of.

                    ROYCE
          And the budget. "Facilities contingency." Up
          every October.

                    GARY
          Six years running.

Royce sits back. Takes off his glasses. Whatever this is, it's
seamless, and we'll never be entirely sure he doesn't believe it
himself.

                    ROYCE
          You know what they did to me, Gary.

                    GARY
          ...The post.

                    ROYCE
          The post. The journals. The friends. And
          here's the part nobody warns you about. They
          don't argue with you. They don't say you're
          wrong. They start being worried about you.
          Real gentle. Real kind. Worried.

Gary thinks of Sal rolling up the prints. Of Donna's two taps on
the table. Something in him goes very still.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          So hear me. Because I wish to God somebody'd
          said it to me in time.

He leans in, takes Gary's wrist. It is the kindest thing anyone has
done for him in years.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          It is not your fault that you see it. You
          didn't do anything wrong. Seeing it isn't the
          sickness. They just need you to believe it
          is.

Gary looks down at his own handwriting so it can't be seen what his
face is doing.

                    GARY
          (thick)
          I should go.

                    ROYCE
          Take it to whoever runs that building. Look
          him in the eye when you do.

Gary gathers the binder to his chest and goes out into the dawn.
Behind him Royce sits alone with his cold coffee in the real cup.
Hold one beat too long. Long enough to wonder whether being
believed in is the last warm thing he has. He turns on the radio.
Static, then music. He washes the one cup.


INT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - HALLORAN'S OFFICE - DAY

Carpet tiles. A dying plant. Halloran on the phone, finger up,
mouthing sorry. Gary across the desk, binder squared, composed.

                    HALLORAN
          (hanging up)
          Gary. Sit. How you doing. Honestly.

He carried this man. Gary slides the binder across. Sixty pages.
Tabs.

                    GARY
          I need you to read all of it. And I need it
          on the record that I came to you first.

Halloran opens it. The map with things circled. The photo of the
door. The budget line, three exclamation points Gary clearly
agonized over. A decent tired man watching his best floor man in a
decade come loose, on a Thursday.

                    HALLORAN
          The sub-level. The smell. Gary, that's a
          sewage main. The lateral collapsed, we've had
          Vanguard pumping it five weeks because we
          can't close the building during midterms.
          It's a hole. Full of what you think it's full
          of.

Gary takes this in. Nods slowly. Reasonable. He doesn't say
cover-up. He says the most reasonable thing in the world:

                    GARY
          Then it'll be in the work orders. The permits.
          The disposal manifests. All public. Show me,
          and I'll go home, and I'll feel like an idiot,
          and I'll buy you a beer.

A beat. Halloran, who manages forty buildings and signs a hundred
forms a week, cannot, off the top of his head, put his hand on the
paperwork for a five-week emergency dig, and to say so out loud, to
this man, right now, sounds insane.

                    HALLORAN
          ...I'd have to pull it.

                    GARY
          (not gloating)
          Okay.

Halloran closes the binder. Closes his eyes. Opens them.

                    HALLORAN
          You've missed eleven shifts. You've been
          found in three locked areas. I have carried
          you nine years because you are the best I
          ever had. I can't anymore. I'm sorry, Gary. I
          have to let you go.

A long beat. Gary doesn't erupt. He picks up his binder and stands.

                    GARY
          (quiet)
          I gave you that Tuesday. You're firing me
          Thursday.
          (at the door)
          You should've waited a month. Would've looked
          better.

He leaves. Halloran sits alone, looks at the door, looks at his
dead plant, picks up the phone to call somebody about this, and
can't think who.


EXT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - PARKING LOT - NIGHT

Late. Half-empty lot, sodium light. Gary in his parked car for
hours. The binder rides shotgun.

A door. Halloran comes out, suit bag on his shoulder, tie off, a
tired man who wants his couch. Beeps a sensible sedan.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I drove home. I want that known. I made it to
          my own street. Sat in my own driveway.

Gary's hand on his keys, not turning them.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I thought: if I go inside and make the
          eggs and go to sleep, then tomorrow I'm just a
          man who got fired. A man his mother is wrong
          about.

Halloran's headlights. He backs out.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I could not be that man. Not for one more
          night. It was never about being right.

Gary's engine turns over.

CUT TO:


INT. GARY'S CAR / EXT. SUBURBAN STREET - NIGHT

Gary follows the sedan two cars back, ten and two, obeying every
law on his way to break the only one that counts. Sweating.
Terrified. A man who has never done a single thing in his life.

The sedan pulls into a sad nice cul-de-sac. Gary parks across the
street, kills the lights. Halloran gets out, yawns, grabs his bag,
heads for his door, keys out, thumbing the mail. A man at the end
of his own driveway, the most defenseless thing there is.

In the car, Gary's breathing. The dome light as the door opens. A
tire iron in his hand, which he looks at like he can't account for
his own arm.

He crosses the street, fast. Halloran half-turns at the sound, mail
in hand, "can I help y--"

SMASH CUT TO:

INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - NIGHT

A water heater. A furnace. A drain in the floor. And RAY HALLORAN,
zip-tied to a kitchen chair, a thin line of blood at his hairline,
gagged with a clean folded dish towel. The man who saved him.

Across the basement, under a bare bulb, GARY stands holding the
tire iron in both hands, staring at the chair with the total,
nauseous horror of a man realizing he is awake.

The furnace ticks. A long silence.

Gary sets the tire iron down on the dryer, very carefully, as if
quiet could undo it. He pulls a second chair around, sits, knees
almost touching his prisoner's. Takes out the spiral notebook.
Clicks the pen. Leans in.

                    GARY
          Just show me the paperwork. That's all. Show
          me it's what you say it is, and I'll let you
          go, and I'll be the idiot. I want to be the
          idiot. You have no idea how bad I want that.

Halloran, gagged, stares at him.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          (sitting back, pen ready, almost pleading)
          So. From the beginning. Where does it go.

Halloran screams into the dish towel.

Gary writes it down.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT ONE.
