DEEPER
(working title)

ACT ONE


FADE IN:

INT. KEPLER HALL - THIRD FLOOR - NIGHT

A bucket on wheels. A gray mop going back and forth across waxed
linoleum, slow, even, almost tender.

The man pushing it is GARY SOBEK, late forties, gray work shirt
with his name stitched over the pocket. A face built for being
forgotten. He mops like a man who has decided the mop is beneath
him and is being gracious about it anyway.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          They think the job is the mopping.

He stops. Down the hall, a night class lets out. Students file
past him without seeing him, the way you don't see a fire
extinguisher.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          The mopping is what I let them see.

He waits for the hall to empty. Then he leans his mop against the
wall and walks, unhurried, toward the lecture hall they just left.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          Every building this size makes a ton of
          waste a day. Paper, food, the other thing.
          They pay men like me to make it disappear.
          And nobody, in the whole history of this
          place, has ever once asked me where it goes.

He pushes open the lecture hall door.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I ask.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - NIGHT

A big tiered room, dark except for the exit signs and a wash of
streetlight. Down front, a wall of green chalkboard, four panels
wide.

On the last panel, up in the corner, somebody has left a problem.
Dense. Greek letters, a long fraction, an integral sign curled
like a question. Below it, in a different hand, a professor's
note: LEAVE UP.

Gary comes down the steps the way a priest comes down an aisle.
He sets down a small canvas tool bag. Takes out, with care, a
single piece of yellow chalk he has brought from home.

He studies the problem. A long time. We cannot tell if he
understands it. Neither, you suspect, can he.

Then he writes.

He writes fast and big, filling the empty board beneath the
problem with symbols, arrows, a number he circles twice, a word
that might be GERMAN. He underlines something. He steps back,
chest going, and looks at what he has made the way a man looks at
a sunrise he personally arranged.

At the bottom he signs it, small and neat:

                              G. ALLAN SOBEK.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          You don't sign the small ones. You sign
          the ones they'll want to find you for.

He gathers his bag. Climbs the steps. At the top he turns and
takes the board in one last time, the way you'd look at a child
asleep.

Then the janitor turns off the lights on the genius and goes back
to his mop.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - MORNING

Sun now. The room fills with undergrads, backpacks, coffee.

PROFESSOR LIND, sixty, cardigan, the gentle exhaustion of a man
who has explained the same thing four thousand times, comes in
talking to a GRAD STUDENT, DEV, twenties.

                    DEV
          ...so I told her office hours are
          Thursday, but she emailed anyway, so.

Lind isn't listening. He's looking at the board. At the yellow
scrawl under his problem.

                    LIND
          (mild)
          Oh, for God's sake.

                    DEV
          (grinning)
          The phantom strikes again.

                    LIND
          Third time this month.

Lind picks up an eraser. Pauses, head tilted, genuinely trying to
parse it. We push in on his face. He is not threatened. He is not
amazed. He is a man reading a menu in a language he doesn't speak,
deciding it isn't worth the trouble.

                    DEV
          What's it even say?

                    LIND
          It doesn't.

And he wipes it away. Three strokes. Gone. Chalk dust in the
sunlight. He's already moving on.

                    LIND (CONT'D)
          Okay. Sequences. Where were we.

PUSH past him, out the door, to the hallway window.

Gary is there. Cart parked. Watching through the glass as his
work vanishes under the old man's hand.

His face does not fall. That's the thing. His face does the
opposite. A small, sad, knowing nod, the nod of a man who has
just had something confirmed.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          They don't erase what's wrong. Wrong, they
          laugh at. They erase what they can't have
          getting out.

He pushes the cart on.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          He didn't even let his students see it.
          Ask yourself why.


INT. O'BANNON'S - NIGHT

A real bar. Not a set. Tradesmen, a TV with the game low, peanut
shells. A booth in back where GARY holds court over three guys
still in work clothes.

SAL DiPIETRO, fifties, foreman's build, foreman's patience, sits
across from Gary the way you sit across from a brother. TINO and
BIG MIKE flank, three beers deep and happy.

                    GARY
          ...all I'm saying is, you take any system.
          Any closed system. Plumbing, money, a body.
          It's all the same diagram. Inputs, outputs,
          and the part nobody's allowed to look at.

                    BIG MIKE
          The part nobody's allowed to look at.

                    GARY
          Correct.

                    TINO
          Which part is that.

                    GARY
          (patient, to a child)
          That's the part nobody's allowed to look
          at, Tino. If I could tell you which part,
          they'd be doing a hell of a bad job.

Tino nods slowly, defeated by logic. Big Mike laughs and knocks
his shoulder. They love him. That's clear. He's the smart one and
he's theirs and they wear it proudly, like a cousin who went to
college.

Sal watches Gary the way you watch weather coming.

                    SAL
          Hey. Genius. Drink your beer.

Gary drinks his beer. Sal leans in, quieter, the others arguing
about the game now.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          We start the Whitmore job Monday. The big
          renovation. It's eight months, it's union,
          it's real money.

                    GARY
          Mm.

                    SAL
          I told Petrocelli I got a guy. Smart guy.
          Reads prints like a book.

                    GARY
          You did the prints thing again.

                    SAL
          I did the prints thing because it's true.
          You see things, Gar. You always saw things.

A beat. For one second Gary is just a man being offered a hand by
a friend who loves him. Something soft crosses his face.

Then it closes.

                    GARY
          I appreciate it. I do. But I'm into
          something right now.

                    SAL
          Into something.

                    GARY
          At the school. I can't get into it.

                    SAL
          You can't get into it.

                    GARY
          Not here.

Sal looks at him a long moment. Decides, again, to let it go. He
sits back.

                    SAL
          Offer's open. Petrocelli owes me till he
          dies.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          Sal's the best man I know. Which is why
          they keep him exactly where he is.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY

Fluorescent, formica, two-dollar coffee. Gary in a booth with a
spiral notebook, writing fast, hand cupped around the page like a
kid hiding a test.

DONNA, forties, kind eyes, twelve years behind this counter,
arrives with the pot. She refills him without being asked. Lingers.

                    DONNA
          You eating, or just renting the booth.

                    GARY
          (not looking up)
          Renting the booth.

                    DONNA
          For you it's free.

She says it warm. It hangs there. He doesn't catch it. He never
catches it. She waits one more second than she should, then taps
the table twice and goes.

Gary writes. We catch the top of the page, block capitals,
underlined three times:

                    WHERE DOES IT GO.

He stares at it. Then, almost shy, he adds a second line, smaller,
like a confession:

                    and who decided i shouldn't know.

From the counter, Donna watches him not eat the pie she's about to
bring him anyway.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          A normal life is a beautiful thing. I think
          about it sometimes. The way you think about
          a country you'll never get a visa for.


INT. GARY'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

Small. Clean in the way of a man with too much time. One wall is
starting to become Something: a campus map, index cards, a
photograph of the sub-basement door, a few lengths of string he
has clearly put up, taken down, and put up again, embarrassed and
unable to stop.

A clock radio glows 2:11 AM. A voice fills the room, late-night
AM, warm and ruined.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          ...and they'll tell you you're paranoid.
          That's the tell, friend. Paranoid is just
          the word the comfortable use for the
          informed.

Gary sits on the edge of the bed, phone in hand, the way you sit
before you do something.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          We got a caller. Line four. You're on The
          Lateness, go ahead.

Gary swallows. Dials. A beat. Then his own voice, on a half-second
delay, comes out of his own radio. It clearly does something to
him to hear it.

                    GARY
          (into phone)
          Yeah. Hi. First-time caller.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Welcome in.

                    GARY
          I work at a university. I'm not gonna say
          which. And I clean it. The whole physical
          plant. And about a month ago I started
          noticing some things about where the, the
          waste goes. The disposal. Off the books.
          And the more I look the more I think I'm
          not supposed to be looking.

Silence on the line. A long professional silence. Gary's knuckles
white on the phone.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          What's your name, friend.

                    GARY
          ...Gary.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Gary. I want you to listen to me. You sound
          like a smart man. You sound like a man
          who's been told his whole life he's too
          smart for the room he's in. That right?

Gary's eyes go wet. Instantly. Like a struck match.

                    GARY
          (barely)
          ...Yeah.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          A mind like that doesn't end up with a mop
          by accident, Gary. A mind like that gets
          put somewhere it can't do any damage. You
          ever think of it that way?

Gary has never, in forty-eight years, thought of it that way. We
watch the thought enter him and change the chemistry of his whole
face. He stands up. He has to stand up.

                    GARY
          No. No, I never. But that's. Yeah.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Keep looking. And Gary? Write it down.
          Everything. They can't disappear what's
          already on paper.

                    GARY
          I will. I am. Thank you. Thank you, I
          mean it.

He hangs up. Stands in the middle of his sad clean apartment at
2 a.m., lit up like a man who has just been told, for the first
time, that the worst thing about his life is the best thing about
him.

He looks at the wall of string.

It no longer looks crazy to him. It looks like the beginning of
something.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          You spend your whole life waiting to be
          seen. And you never once think about who's
          doing the looking.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - NIGHT

Concrete. Older than the building above it. Gary's flashlight
finds pipes, a floor drain, a smell that arrives before the image
does and makes him pull his collar up.

A steel door, newer than the wall around it. A keypad. A
laminated sign: AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL - HAZMAT REMEDIATION IN
PROGRESS.

Gary photographs the sign. Photographs the door. Photographs the
keypad.

A LOGBOOK hangs on a nail by the door, the kind with a stub of
golf pencil on a string. He flips it. Entries. Dates. Times, all
after midnight. Initials. A company name: VANGUARD ENVIRO.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          Hazmat. Remediation. You want to hide
          something, you don't put up a sign that
          says secret. You put up a sign so boring
          the eye slides right off it.

From somewhere behind the door: a deep, wet, industrial GURGLE.
A pump kicking on. Liquid moving in volume.

Gary goes still. To him it is the sound of the machine itself,
finally, breathing.

He presses his ear to the cold steel, eyes closed, reverent,
while on the other side of three inches of door a sump pump moves
four hundred gallons of raw sewage out of a building that has,
boringly, catastrophically, backed up.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          That night I stopped being a man who
          cleaned a building.

He opens his eyes.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And became a man it was hiding things from.


SERIES OF SHOTS - THE WORK

-- The apartment wall, no longer embarrassed. Full now. Map,
photos, the logbook page photographed and pinned. The string goes
where it wants.

-- Gary in his parked car outside a chain-link lot at 1 a.m.,
photographing a white VANGUARD ENVIRO van through a long lens he
clearly bought used and cannot quite work.

-- Gary at the library microfilm, the only man under seventy,
scrolling decades of campus news with the focus of prayer.

-- His phone, three missed calls. SAL. SAL. SAL. He turns it face
down.

-- Delmore's. Donna sets pie in front of him. He doesn't look up.
She watches the part in his hair for a moment, then takes her own
small disappointment back behind the counter.

-- The notebook, a page now headed, in that grand neat hand:
A PRELIMINARY MAP OF THE THING. He looks at it. Crosses out
PRELIMINARY.


INT. ROYCE'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

A garden unit that smells like the radio sounded. Stacks. A second
life's worth of paper. A reel-to-reel. A man's whole career,
boxed, with the lid off.

ROYCE himself is smaller than his voice. Sixties, a good blazer
gone shiny, the dignity of a man holding a door for a building
that's already condemned. He pours Gary a coffee in a real cup
and saucer, the only formal thing in the room.

Gary's binder is open on the table between them. Royce turns the
pages slowly. He does Gary the one thing nobody has ever done.

He reads it like it's real.

                    ROYCE
          (tapping a page)
          This. The van. You clocked it three nights,
          all after one.

                    GARY
          Three I'm sure of.

                    ROYCE
          (turning back)
          And the budget thing. The line item.
          "Facilities contingency." Goes up every
          October.

                    GARY
          Every October for six years.

Royce sits back. Takes off his glasses. The performance, if it is
one, is seamless, and we will never be entirely sure.

                    ROYCE
          You know what they did to me, Gary?

                    GARY
          ...The post.

                    ROYCE
          The post. The journals. The, the friends.
          You find out fast who your friends were.
          They don't argue with you. That's the part
          nobody warns you about. They don't say
          you're wrong. They just start being
          worried about you. Real gentle. Real kind.
          Worried.

Gary thinks, hard, of Sal on the porch. Of Donna's two taps on the
table. Something tightens in him.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          And here's what I need you to hear, son,
          because I wish to God somebody'd said it to
          me in time.

He leans in. Takes Gary's wrist. It lands like therapy. It is the
single warmest moment in the film so far.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          It is not your fault that you see it. You
          didn't do anything wrong. Seeing it is not
          the sickness. They just need you to think
          it is.

Gary, undone, looks down at the binder, at his own handwriting, so
it can't be seen what his face is doing.

                    GARY
          (thick)
          I should go. I'm cleaning the east wing at
          six.

                    ROYCE
          Course you are.
          (beat, gentle)
          Take the contingency line to whoever fired
          the building. Look him in the eye when you
          do it. Watch his hands.

                    GARY
          His hands.

                    ROYCE
          A liar protects his middle. Watch the hands.

Gary nods like he's been handed a sword. Gathers his binder, holds
it to his chest, and goes out into the dawn a soldier.

Behind him, alone, Royce sits with his cold real coffee in its
real cup. We hold on him one beat too long. Long enough to wonder
whether he believes a word of it, or whether being believed in is
just the last warm thing he has left.

He turns the radio on. Static, then music. He starts to do the
dishes.


INT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - HALLORAN'S OFFICE - DAY

Carpet tiles. A dying plant. The office of a man two rungs above
janitor and a thousand miles from power: RAY HALLORAN, fifties,
facilities director, a tie he clearly hates, the harried decency
of middle management eternal.

Gary sits across the desk, binder squared in front of him,
absolutely composed. Halloran is on the phone, holding up one
finger, mouthing sorry.

                    HALLORAN
          (into phone)
          ...no, the chiller, the chiller in B, not
          the, Carol, the one we, yes. Yes. By
          Friday. Okay. Okay.
          (hangs up, exhales)
          Gary. What can I do you for.

Gary slides the binder across. Sixty pages. A tab system.

                    GARY
          I need you to read all of it. Tonight. And
          I need you to understand that I came to you
          first. I want that on the record. I came
          to you.

Halloran opens it. Page one is a campus map with things circled.
He flips. A photo of the sub-level door. He flips. The budget line,
highlighted, with three exclamation points that Gary, a careful
man, clearly agonized over before adding.

Halloran is not a stupid man. He is a tired one. We watch him
understand, in real time, that the most reliable employee on his
night crew has come unscrewed, and that this is now, somehow, his
problem, on a Thursday.

                    HALLORAN
          Gary. The sub-level in Kepler. The smell.

                    GARY
          (leaning in, watching the hands)
          Yes.

                    HALLORAN
          That's a sewage main. It's a hundred-year-
          old building, the lateral collapsed, we've
          had Vanguard pumping it and digging it out
          for five weeks because we can't shut the
          building during midterms. It's, Gary, it's
          a giant hole full of, of, you know what
          it's full of.

A beat. Gary, gently, with enormous compassion, the way you'd
correct a frightened man:

                    GARY
          That's exactly what you'd have to say.

Halloran stares at him.

                    HALLORAN
          ...What?

                    GARY
          No, it's perfect. I'd say the same thing.
          It's airtight. I'm not being sarcastic. It's
          a genuinely excellent cover because it's
          disgusting and boring and nobody wants to
          look. I respect it.

Halloran closes the binder. Closes his eyes. Opens them. Picks his
words like a man crossing a creek on stones.

                    HALLORAN
          Gary, you've missed eleven shifts. You've
          been found in three locked areas. Lopez
          says you photographed her car.

                    GARY
          (this lands; recalibrating)
          ...Lopez reported that.

                    HALLORAN
          (missing it entirely)
          I have been carrying you because you're the
          best floor man I ever had and your file is
          spotless going back nine years. But I can't
          anymore. I'm sorry. As of today I have to
          let you go.

And there it is.

Gary does not erupt. Gary does the small sad knowing nod. The one
from the hallway window. The one that means: confirmed.

                    GARY
          (quietly, almost relieved)
          Two days.

                    HALLORAN
          What?

                    GARY
          I gave you the binder Tuesday. It's
          Thursday.

He stands. Gathers the binder, the evidence they tried to bury by
firing the man who carried it. At the door he turns. He is not
angry. He looks at Halloran with something almost like pity.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          You should be more careful. You did it too
          fast. A smarter man would've waited a
          month.

He leaves. Halloran sits alone, looks at the door, looks at his
dead plant, and reaches for the phone to call somebody, anybody,
about this, and cannot for the life of him think of who.


EXT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - PARKING LOT - NIGHT

Late. A half-empty lot under sodium light. Gary sits in his
parked car. Has been for hours. The binder rides shotgun like a
passenger.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I drove home. I want you to know I drove
          home first.

A door. HALLORAN comes out, suit bag over his shoulder, tie off,
just a tired man who wants his couch. He fishes for keys. Beeps a
sensible sedan.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I made it to my street. I sat in my own
          driveway.

Gary's hand on his own keys. Not turning them.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I thought about the contingency line.
          And the van. And the old man saying watch
          the hands.

                    GARY (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          And I thought: if I go inside, and I make
          the eggs, and I go to sleep, then tomorrow
          I'm just a guy who got fired.

Halloran gets in his sedan. Headlights. Backs out.

Gary's engine turns over.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I could not. I could not be that.

CUT TO:


INT. GARY'S CAR / EXT. SUBURBAN STREET - NIGHT

Gary follows the sedan, two cars back, hands at ten and two, a man
obeying every traffic law on his way to break the largest one
there is. He is sweating. He is terrified. He has clearly never
done anything in his life.

The sedan pulls into a driveway in a normal sad nice cul-de-sac.
Gary parks across the street. Kills the lights.

Halloran gets out. Yawns. Grabs his suit bag. Heads for his door,
keys out, thumbing through mail, the most defenseless animal on
earth: a man at the end of his own driveway.

In the car, Gary's breathing. The dome light as he opens his
door. A tire iron in his hand, which he looks at like he doesn't
recognize his own arm.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I keep trying to tell people the next part
          isn't the important part.

He crosses the street. Fast. Behind Halloran now. Halloran half-
turns at the sound, mail in hand, "can I help y--"

SMASH CUT TO:

INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - NIGHT

A water heater. A furnace. A drain in the concrete floor.

And RAY HALLORAN, zip-tied to a kitchen chair, a thin line of
blood at his hairline, gagged with what is unmistakably a clean,
folded dish towel. His eyes are huge. His chest heaves.

Across the basement, under a bare bulb, GARY stands holding the
tire iron in both hands, staring at the man in the chair with the
dawning, nauseous, total horror of someone realizing they are
awake.

Long silence. The furnace ticks.

Gary sets the tire iron down on the dryer. Very carefully. As if
quiet could undo it.

He pulls a second chair around. Sits, facing his prisoner, knees
almost touching. He takes out his spiral notebook. Clicks his
pen.

And Gary Sobek, forty-eight, unemployed, the smartest man he
knows, leans toward the terrified stranger he has chained in his
basement, and says, with the shining patience of a man who has
finally, finally gotten someone to sit still and listen:

                    GARY
          Okay. Start at the beginning. Where does
          it go.

Halloran screams into the dish towel.

Gary writes it down.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT ONE.
