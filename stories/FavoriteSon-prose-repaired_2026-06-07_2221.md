# Favorite Son — prose (self-repaired)

The hotel keycard slides into the lock, turns stiffly. The door opens to a room with the curtains half-drawn, sunlight cutting a strip across the carpet. A woman sits on the edge of the bed, adjusting her blouse, her hair still damp from the shower. Reese stands by the window, back to her, hands in his pockets, looking down at the street below. He does not turn when the camera clicks begin—not the first, not the third, not the one that catches his face in profile, eyes narrowed, mouth set. The footage is short: the keycard swipe, the door opening, the woman’s laugh as she tosses a dress onto the chair, Reese’s hand resting for two seconds on her waist before he pulls away. No words. No names. Just the mechanics of presence, absence, and the way light falls on a public man’s private moment.  

Two hours later, he stands at the podium in the Commission’s press bay, tie loose, face smooth as stone. The screen behind him shows only static. He does not flinch when the first reporter shouts the question. He does not deny it. He says only, “It was recorded without consent. It does not reflect the truth of my conduct, but it does reflect the truth of the moment we’re in.” He pauses. The silence stretches. Then he adds, “Ruth would’ve called this a trap.” He walks out before the applause ends, the door clicking shut behind him. The footage is already looping in the break rooms, on the bus stops, in the hands of children scrolling past breakfast. No one watches for the woman. No one watches for him. They watch for the gap between the man and the myth—and how easily it fills with noise.

Claire slides the file across the desk. The paper edges are slightly bent from being carried too long in her coat pocket. Reese does not pick it up. He looks past her, at the window where rain streaks the glass in uneven lines. Outside, a delivery van idles at the curb, its engine a low hum. She watches his hands—flat on the desk, fingers relaxed, no tremor. She watches the way his eyes stay fixed on the rain, not on her, not on the file. He says nothing. She stands. The chair scrapes once, sharply, against the tile floor. She does not look back as she walks to the door. The file stays where she left it. She does not touch the zipper on her bag until she is three blocks away, on the bus, leaning her forehead against the cold window. The rain blurs the city into streaks of gray and wet asphalt. Her left hand rests on the bag. Her right hand stays still.

He opens the file again. The pages are still warm from her touch. He flips to the last page, where her signature sits—clear, firm, no hesitation. He picks up the pen, taps the barrel once against the desk. His thumb finds the edge of the envelope tucked inside the file—thin, unmarked, sealed with a single strip of tape. He does not open it. He slides it back in, smooths the cover sheet, closes the folder. The van outside revs its engine, tires crunching as it pulls away. He stands. He walks to the door, turns the deadbolt. The lock clicks once, twice. He does not look at the window. The rain has stopped. A streetlamp flickers on across the street, casting a long, wet rectangle on the sidewalk. He waits until the light stops blinking. Then he goes back to his desk, sits down, and opens the file to the first page.

Reese walks the clerk down the corridor, the man’s shoulders hunched, eyes fixed on the scuff marks along the baseboard. His wrists are bound, but the cuffs are loose—just tight enough to remind him he’s not free. At the end of the hall, the clerk stops. He turns, looks toward the observation room where the gallery sits behind tinted glass. His wife is there, standing, one hand pressed flat against the window. Reese doesn’t look back. He nudges the clerk forward with his elbow, not hard, just enough. The clerk takes one step, then another. The door to the holding cell opens. Reese waits until the clerk steps inside, then shuts it behind him. The lock engages. The clerk does not move. His wife’s hand remains on the glass, fingers spread, palm white. Reese walks away. The corridor lights hum overhead, steady, unblinking.

The folder lies open between them on the desk. Claire’s thumb drags across the top sheet—photocopy, slightly warped at the corners. She doesn’t look up. Her voice is flat, neutral, the tone she uses when quoting a witness or reading a deposition. “She’s a plant.” Reese doesn’t correct her. He watches her eyes—how they narrow just past the first paragraph, how they skip the second, land again on the third line of the fourth page. She closes the folder. Her left hand trembles once, then she tucks it under the edge of the desk. She opens her mouth to speak, then closes it. The silence stretches. Outside, a bell rings somewhere in the building—low, metallic, unchanging. She picks up her pen, taps it twice against the folder’s spine. Not a question. Not a demand. Just the sound of a tool being checked before use.

The garage door is still up, the engine off, keys in the ignition. Her head rests against the steering wheel, one hand curled around the gearshift, the other hanging limp over the door frame. The car smells of exhaust fumes, old coffee, and the faint metallic tang of dried blood where her temple pressed into the wheel. Reese stands just inside the doorway, coat still on, rainwater pooling at his feet. He doesn’t step closer. He doesn’t touch her. He reads her name aloud—Claire Soto—into the recorder he holds, his voice cracking not on the first syllable but on the second, as if the word had weight he hadn’t expected. He turns off the recorder. The silence after is the same as the silence before, except now it’s filled with the hum of the overhead light and the slow, steady drip of water from his sleeve onto the concrete floor.

Reese sets the report on the desk, pages stacked square, corners aligned. The pen he offers has a worn grip, the cap clicked off and left on the desk beside it. Claire’s left hand trembles as she takes it, but her grip is firm. She signs her name in three clean strokes—Claire Soto—and sets the pen down with the cap still off. Reese picks up the report, slides it into a manila folder, and slides the folder into a metal box bolted to the wall. He turns the key once, then twice. The box clicks shut. He does not look at Claire. He does not speak. The only sound is the hum of the overhead light, and the slow, steady drip of water from his sleeve onto the concrete floor.

A young lawyer stands at the counter, waiting for the coffee. She wears a navy suit, the kind with sharp shoulders and no wrinkles. Reese sets the cup in front of her—black, no sugar. She thanks him. He nods. He says the line he said to Claire. She looks up, eyes bright, and says, “I’m not sure I deserve it yet.” He does not correct her. He does not smile. He watches her take the cup, watches her fingers tighten just once around the paper sleeve. He turns and walks out, the door chimes behind him. She sips the coffee. Her left hand trembles. She does not put it down.
