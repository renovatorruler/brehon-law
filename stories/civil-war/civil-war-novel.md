## 1

Desmond set the axe to the log again and missed the split again, and the wood rolled off the block into the dirt.

Nia came out on the porch in the gray dress and watched him miss a while.

"You're chopping it like it owes you money," she said.

"Go on inside."

"Mama does it better than you."

"Inside, Nia."

She didn't go in. She sat on the step with her knees up under the dress and looked down the road, the way they all did now. The house stood at the end of a long dirt track that ran off a county road that ran off another, and the only reason they were still alive was that nobody had reason to come down it.

Della had the rifles apart on the kitchen table, wiping them down while she talked to herself about supper.

They kept their rules: nobody past the tree line alone, no lamp in the window after dark, somebody on the road from first light to last.

Nia saw the dust first. She didn't shout, just said "Daddy" and lifted her chin to where the track met the road, where something kicked up a haze with no wind to do it.

Desmond counted four riders.

He sent the girl up the stairs with a look and didn't watch her go. He heard a window slide above him, Della's gun coming to rest on the sill, then the second window, the girl at it. He leaned the axe against the block like a man who meant to get back to it, and stood in the yard with his hands where they showed.

The riders came up easy, in no hurry. Four men, dark as he was, dusty and well-mounted, rifles across their laps. The one in front walked his horse near to the porch before he stopped it. He looked Desmond over, the homespun and the broke-down shoes, and smiled.

"Which plantation you run off from?"

"Don't know what you mean."

"No?" He hung his hands on the horn. "We're after a man. Came through three, four days back. Tall, took a ball in the leg. Would've looked like he needed a place to lie up."

"Nobody comes out this far."

"Somebody did." The man looked the place over, the house, the well, the lean-to. "Bring your wife out. The girl too. We'll all talk about it."

"They got nothing to tell you I can't."

"Wasn't a request."

One of the others got down off his horse and started for the porch, taking his time. Desmond stepped into his way, not fast, just enough to stand between him and the door.

"There's no man here," Desmond said. "Ride on north, you'll cut his trail at the river. I wouldn't waste the daylight on us."

The man on the ground looked back at the leader. The leader sat his horse and chewed on it, looking at the house and the empty road north, and Desmond watched him weigh a houseful of women and a yard full of work against a stranger's leg and come up short.

"Mount up," the leader said.

The one on the ground spat, gave Desmond a last look, and climbed back on.

They were turning the horses when the young one at the back went still. He was looking past the lean-to at the bay tied in the shade, and the saddle on the rail beside her, silver let into the cantle, worth more than the house and everything in it.

"That's his horse," the young one said. "That's —"

Desmond shot him off it before he got the rest out.

After that it went fast. The windows came open above him and Della fired and then the girl, and the riders couldn't get the horses turned in the close of the yard. One went down and stayed down. Another took it high and held on somehow and got himself gone, and the last one went with him, the two of them low over their horses' necks, back down the track the way they'd come.

Desmond stood with the pistol up until there was nothing left to point it at. Then he went inside and up the stairs.

"Pack what you can carry," he said. "Ten minutes."

## 2

The realtor's name was Brad, and he had a way of announcing every room like it was the best one in the house.

"Now this," Brad said, "is your bonus room. Media room, home gym, man cave — whatever speaks to you."

What spoke to Desmond was the window. One window, street side, and past it the cul-de-sac curled back on itself with a single way in and out. He stood there working it the way he worked everything — where's the door, where's the second door, what happens when the first one fails.

"You're doing the thing," Della said, beside him, smiling out at the yard so Brad wouldn't catch her not smiling at her husband.

"I'm looking at the house."

"You're looking at the exits."

"One road in, Dell. One road in is one road out." He kept it low. "I keep telling you. We should be looking at land. Couple acres east of here, some space around us, something we can actually—"

"A compound." She almost laughed. "You want to raise our daughter in a compound."

"I want to raise our daughter."

"Desmond." She looked at him then, still smiling. "It's going to be okay. People have been calling the end of the country since before there was a country. We're going to buy a house, gripe about the HOA, complain about the heat after thirty years of St. Louis winters, and be fine."

"Schools out here are phenomenal, by the way," Brad said from the hall.

"See?" Della said. "Phenomenal schools."

Desmond looked back out the one window.

Outside, Nia found the only other kids on the street.

They were in the next yard over, a boy and a smaller girl, running something back and forth that took a lot of shrieking. The boy looked about her age. Nia went to the chain-link, hooked her fingers in it, and watched, waiting to be noticed. The smaller girl noticed first and stopped, and then the boy stopped.

Then the screen door banged and the mother came out fast, talking. The talk wasn't for Nia. It went over her head in a language she didn't have one word of. She didn't need the words. She had the face, and the way the woman caught the little girl by the wrist and turned her with it, and the way she put herself between her kids and the fence without once looking at what was on the other side of it.

Nia took her fingers out of the chain-link.

She knew it without the words. She'd known it without the words before.

She was almost back to her own yard when she heard the gate.

The boy had come through it. He stood a second like he wasn't sure why, then crossed the grass with his hands in his pockets and stopped a careful few feet off.

"She's not always like that," he said.

"It's okay."

"It's not, though." He looked back at his house, then at her. "You want to see something? There's a frog living in the storm drain by the hydrant. He's the size of a softball."

Nia looked at him.

"I'm serious," the boy said. "He's enormous."

"Okay," Nia said, and went with him.
