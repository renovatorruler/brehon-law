# (working title — untitled) — Story Bible

Companion to the manuscript (`civil-war-novel.md`). Holds the design: the world, the structure, the cast, the method, the metaphors, and per-scene notes. Voice and scene discipline live in `docs/VOICE_GUIDE.md`.

## What it is

A novel. A second American civil war, told through an **ensemble of families** who'd never have chosen each other. The subject is **American identity** — whether "American" is something you're born or something you become — *not* the left/right culture war.

**The aim (the transformation):** the reader closes the book with their love for the American idea rejuvenated — earned, never declared. The melting pot, proven under fire by the people with the least reason to believe in it. Hold every page to the sermon discipline: nobody names the theme, the flag never waves, the feeling is paid for in concrete acts and real graves.

**The engine of decline (backstory, never exposition):** old immigrants burned their boats — no way home, so they *became* American. New immigrants never burned anything: cheap flights, a phone that's always home, a second passport in the drawer. One foot out the door, loyalty never fully poured. The war's pressure burns the boats for them.

## The world

**Why the war.** Not two armies. A country that came apart: the shared "we" thinned over a generation → a hard economic shock (money crisis, the safety net fails) → an atrocity pinned on one group → reprisals → fragmentation. Power drops to militias, enclaves, private security. Bosnia in American sprawl.

**The enemy — the Authority.** It ends the open killing the only way it can imagine: by keeping everyone with their own kind. Not jackboots — the captured *helping* institutions (emergency management, public health, the universities, the NGOs) fused into a bureaucracy of care pointed at separation. A lanyard, not a uniform. Fronted by **the Inquisitor** ("Mother") — soft-voiced, righteous, certain she's saving everyone. Behind her, a power bloc that profits from a permanently sorted, dependent, frightened population.

**The zones.** The Authority registers everyone by origin and moves them into "safe communities" for their protection. The metaphor cast in concrete: forced urbanization / the already-segregated American city with the polite fiction stripped off — the diverse metro where a hundred languages never share a table, made mandatory. A gilded replica of the old country: a velvet cell. Three spaces, three meanings — the **zone** (sameness by force), the **home country** they flee to (sameness by choice), the **building** (difference, chosen, alive = the real America).

**Sealed borders.** Leaving is criminalized. The wall faces inward: a refuge people are paying to flee is a prison, so the lie requires the wall. (Legitimacy; labor/brain-drain; population-as-power in a faction war; the hidden hand guarding its captive economy.)

## The structure

**The inverse-coyote story.** America is the place the world dies to sneak *into*; this is a book about families paying a smuggler to sneak *out*. *Casablanca* run backwards. The image is the thesis — no one has to say a word.

**Flight becomes fight.** They spend the book trying to burn their *American* boats (flee) and find they won't burn — home isn't home. What burns is the home-country fantasy. They become American the moment they turn and choose to stay and fight for the place.

**The braid.** The escape is the shared spine (the cage); the families are the different animals — six distinct arcs and reasons, braided by the escape and converging at the stand. **Order: anchor → reveal → fan out.** Don't fan to the other families until the reader has the frame and a family they'd bleed for.

## The cast

**The native anchor — the pilot.** A disillusioned native who smuggles people out for money, dead inside about the country (the inverse Rick). Through these families he gets his country back; the man who sold the escape ends up the one who can't leave. The reader's surrogate (the upset native).

**The six families** — each a different answer to "what makes an American":
- **Israeli** — somewhere to go (a real, waiting exit; stays anyway).
- **Arab** — nowhere to go (home is rubble); fights to be *seen* as American. *(Keystone pair with the Israelis: one can leave and won't, one can't and isn't let in.)*
- **Indian** — thought they'd made it (assimilated, affluent); the sort doesn't care.
- **Nigerian** — true believers (love the country harder than any native); faith on trial. The moral engine.
- **Hispanic** — here first (six generations; the border crossed them); the angriest.
- **Asian / Korean** — the collaborators (buy safety by helping the Authority); guilt, then the turn.

**The anchor family (first chair) — Desmond's family.**
- **Desmond** — a Black systems engineer who ran a data center (kept ten thousand machines breathing at 3 a.m.). His systems mind is his **lens**, kept submerged: he reads the world in failure modes, single points of failure, redundancy, backups — shown in behavior, never named, until the post-reveal payoff. *(The "logs/sed" callback: in ch. 1 he can't split a wood **log**; later, once we know him — "Only logs I ever split, I split with sed. On a machine the size of this house. Never once needed an axe.")* The broader device: every family's pre-collapse self leaks through as the lens they can't help seeing by.
- **Della** — his wife; gentle once, a schoolteacher type, now cleans the rifles without thinking. The hope to his dread.
- **Nia** — the daughter; a watchful kid who already knows hard things. ~12 in Dallas, ~16 and hardened at the estate.
- **Path:** St. Louis (the old life) → a Dallas suburb (the move, as it starts going bad) → later, on the run, a colonial-era estate where they end up in period clothes = the opening.
- **The Korean boy** (next door in Dallas) — first crossing of a line his mother drew; a thread that pays off later.

## Method

Voice: **Elmore Leonard** — lean, dialogue-forward, flat, invisible. Every scene is built and checked on **Peshat / Remez / Derash / Sod** + the **Mamet** drama, then run through the slop pass. All of it in `docs/VOICE_GUIDE.md`.

## Scene notes

**Scene 1 — the estate.**
- *Drama:* the lead rider wants Desmond to give up the man he's hiding, or prove he isn't there; Desmond wants them gone believing there's nothing here — a bluff with his family's lives on it. *Now:* they've tracked the man here, he's shot, he can't run. Turns when the bluff nearly works and a horse betrays it.
- *Peshat:* a Black family bluffs four armed riders off their farm; the bluff breaks on the fugitive's horse; they shoot clear.
- *Remez:* the slave patrol — the hunt, the harbored fugitive, "which plantation you run off from," and the gut-turn that the hunters are his own.
- *Derash:* America's oldest sin doesn't stay buried; a man is measured by who he'll shelter when it comes back.
- *Sod:* it isn't the past — it's the future. (Kept fully off the page.)

**Scene 2 — Dallas (the reveal).**
- *Drama:* Desmond wants Della to see the danger he sees; Della wants him to let them have a normal life; the realtor wants the sale. Outside: Nia wants in with the neighbor kids; the Korean mother shuts her out; the boy crosses anyway. *Stakes* (invisible to them): Desmond is right. *Now:* they're about to sign.
- *Peshat:* a family tours a Dallas house; the husband frets and floats a compound, the wife teases him down; the daughter is rebuffed by the Korean mother, and the boy crosses the yard to her.
- *Remez:* the war in seed — the mother walling her kids off is the sort in miniature; Desmond's dread is the first tremor; the boy crossing the line is the melting pot's first act.
- *Derash:* how thin the ice under the ordinary dream is; division and decency both start in a driveway; a person is measured by which line they'll cross.
- *Sod (detonates here):* it isn't 1870 — it's now, and these are the same people. The dream is where the nightmare came from.
