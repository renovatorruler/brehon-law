DEEPER
(working title)

ACT TWO


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - MORNING

Gray light through a dirty window well. Overnight the basement has
become something between an office and a cell. A card table. The
binder. The wall map taped to the cinderblock. A space heater
angled, with awful courtesy, toward the prisoner.

RAY HALLORAN, zip-tied to the kitchen chair, the dish towel down
around his neck now. A paper cup of water with a bent straw waits
on a stool. Gary lifts it to Halloran's lips.

                    GARY
          Small sips. You'll cramp.

                    HALLORAN
          (drinks, then, low and careful)
          Gary. My wife. Carol. She has called the
          police by now. You have a day. Maybe. You can
          still walk this all the way back. Drive me to
          a hospital, tell them I fell, and I will back
          you up. I swear to God. I will back your play.

Gary clicks his pen. Dates a fresh page. Underlines it twice.

                    GARY
          I want to do this right, so nobody can ever
          say it was coerced.

He takes out an index card and reads from it, carefully.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          You have the right to remain silent. Anything
          you say can and will--

                    HALLORAN
          Gary.

                    GARY
          --be used. Do you understand these rights as
          I have read them to you.

                    HALLORAN
          There is nothing to understand. There is a
          collapsed sewer pipe and a man having a
          breakdown in his basement, and the man is you.
          You hear me? It's you.

Gary writes, in a clean steady hand: SUBJECT DECLINES TO ANSWER. He
is not cruel. He is not gloating. He is a man finally permitted to
do important work.

                    GARY
          The contingency line. The money. Start there,
          and we'll go as long as it takes.

Halloran sees a sane man cannot reach him. We see the same thing.
The difference is Halloran is in the chair.


INT. RIVERSIDE PRECINCT - FRONT DESK - DAY

CAROL HALLORAN, coat over her robe, leans across a counter at a
DESK SERGEANT who has heard this a thousand times.

                    CAROL
          He did not go to a conference. He emailed me
          he went to a conference, but he didn't pack
          his pills, and he would never leave his pills,
          and he texts me from the bathroom of his own
          house, Sergeant, that is the kind of man he
          is.

                    DESK SERGEANT
          Ma'am, I gotta tell you, nine times in ten,
          the husband turns up. Give it forty-eight--

                    CAROL
          He has a model railroad. Grown man. A little
          town, he paints the little people. You don't
          build a town for thirty years and then walk
          out on it.

The sergeant looks at her. Something in the specificity gets
through. He pulls a form.

                    DESK SERGEANT
          (writing)
          Last seen.

                    CAROL
          Wednesday. He said he had to let somebody go.
          A man he liked.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY

Gary at Halloran's own laptop, hunting and pecking with the focus
of a surgeon. On screen, a half-written email from Halloran's
account to Carol.

                    GARY (V.O., reading as he types)
          "...regional facilities summit, Columbus, last
          minute, you know how they spring these. Cell's
          no good in the conference center. Don't worry.
          Love, R."

He reads it back. Frowns. Deletes "Love, R." Types "Love you. R."
A man who will get the texture right or die trying.

A KNOCK at the front door. Gary freezes. Through the curtain, a
shape, a uniform. He does not breathe.

                    MAILMAN (O.S.)
          Package for Sobek, needs a signature.

Gary opens the door eight inches. A bored MAILMAN, a clipboard.
Behind Gary, distant, below, a single muffled THUMP from the
basement.

                    MAILMAN
          (not hearing it, or not caring)
          Sign here.

Gary signs. The name he signs, without thinking, is not his own.
The mailman doesn't look. He never looks. Nobody ever looks. Gary
closes the door and leans his back against it and for one second
is just a man in over his head.


INT. KFRG-AM - BROADCAST BOOTH - NIGHT

Royce alone in the dead booth, a prepaid phone to his ear, lit up
brighter than the board.

                    ROYCE
          (into phone)
          Say that again. Slowly.

INTERCUT - GARY, in his car in a dark lot, hunched over a matching
prepaid, a man who has started thinking about how calls get traced.

                    GARY
          I have a source. Inside. The director of the
          whole physical plant. And he's talking.

                    ROYCE
          (a man receiving scripture)
          A source. Gary. Do you understand what you're
          holding. They have ended people for a tenth of
          this. You keep him talking, and you keep him
          breathing, because right now that man is the
          most important human being in this country and
          you and I are the only two who know it.

                    GARY
          He keeps saying it's a sewer.

                    ROYCE
          'Course he does. That's the training. You push
          past the sewer, son. The truth is on the far
          side of the most boring thing they can hand
          you. Push.

Gary nods in the dark. Royce hangs up and sits in the silent booth,
holding the dead phone, the only man alive who believes Gary is a
genius, which is the only thing keeping Royce a prophet instead of
a sad old man in a folding chair. He needs it more than Gary does.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY

SAL stands on the porch. Gary holds the front door at the same
eight inches, his body filling the gap.

                    SAL
          Three weeks, Gar. Nobody's seen you in three
          weeks. You missed the Whitmore start. Petrocelli
          fills the spot Monday and I can't stop him.

                    GARY
          I've been busy.

                    SAL
          With what. Look at me and tell me with what.

Gary looks at him. Doesn't tell him.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (quieter, the whole thirty years in it)
          This isn't you. I don't know what it is, but it
          isn't you, and I'm not gonna stand out here and
          argue about whatever's in your head, because I
          never could win that and I'm not gonna try. I'm
          just asking you. As your friend. Come back.
          Whatever you're holding onto. Put it down and
          come back.

It lands. For one second something in Gary's face gives, the man
under the mission surfacing, reaching.

From the basement, below them both, a sound. A chair leg, scraping
concrete. Twice.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (alert)
          What's that.

                    GARY
          (the door already closing)
          Furnace.

                    SAL
          Gary--

                    GARY
          The furnace, Sal. I gotta go.

The door shuts. Sal stands on the porch a long moment, looking at
the closed door of a man he has loved since they were boys, and
something in him goes from worried to afraid. He takes out his
phone. Doesn't know who to call. Puts it away. Walks to his truck.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - NIGHT

Gary stands at the counter, shaking, Sal's voice still in the room.
For one long beat he is right at the edge of it, of putting it
down. His hand finds the prepaid phone.

He calls the only other number in it.

                    GARY
          (into phone, unsteady)
          My friend came by. My oldest friend. He thinks
          I'm sick. He looked at me like I'm sick.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          (gentle, fatal)
          I know, son. I know exactly how that feels. I
          want you to hear something, and I want you to
          hold it close, because it cost me everything to
          learn it. The small ones leave first. Always.
          Not because they're bad. Because they're small,
          and what you're carrying is too big for them,
          and it frightens them. That's not him failing
          you. That's just the size of the thing.

Gary closes his eyes. Chooses. We watch him choose. The man who
loves him, or the man who needs him.

                    ROYCE (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          You're so close. Don't you dare lose your nerve
          now.

                    GARY
          (steadying, the crack sealing over)
          No. No, I'm not gonna lose my nerve.

He hangs up. He does not look sick anymore. He looks decided. He
opens the basement door and goes down to his work, and the last
ordinary thing in him stays up in the kitchen, alone, where he
left it.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - NIGHT (DAYS LATER)

Halloran is thinner. Grayer. A bruise of fatigue under each eye. He
has done the arithmetic that captivity teaches: the truth earns him
another long night and the legal pad; agreement earns him water and
a blanket and the heater turned up.

So he begins to give Gary what Gary wants.

                    HALLORAN
          (quietly, not meeting his eyes)
          ...It's not in facilities.

Gary goes still. Sets down the soup.

                    GARY
          What's not.

                    HALLORAN
          What you're looking for. The money. It was
          never going to be in facilities, that's the
          maintenance side, that's nothing.
          (a slow breath, building it)
          It moves through Advancement. The development
          office. The Founders' account. They run it as
          gift money so it never hits a public budget.
          That's why you couldn't find it. You were
          digging in the wrong building.

It is, every word, the desperate improvisation of a man buying his
life with the only currency that works. Gary's hand is shaking too
hard to write.

                    GARY
          (barely)
          Say the name again. Slow.

Gary sees: the source has cracked, the dam has broken, it is real
and it is bigger than he dreamed. We see: a frightened man has
learned the shape of his captor's hunger and is feeding it, one
invented sentence at a time, to live another night.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          (writing now, fast, reverent)
          The Founders' account. Okay. Okay. I knew it
          went deeper. I knew it.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY

The corner booth sits empty, has for weeks. Behind the counter,
DONNA wipes it down out of habit, sets a fresh napkin dispenser,
the small maintenance of a place held for someone.

She takes the folded newspaper clipping out of her apron, the
community college job, soft now at the creases. She looks at it.

Then she drops it in the bus tub with the dirty dishes, and turns
the corner booth's little RESERVED card around to OPEN, and seats a
family of four there, and pours their coffee, and laughs at the
dad's joke, and that is how a person decides to stop waiting.


INT. MEADOWBROOK CARE CENTER - DAY

Irene at her window with a new puzzle, half done. The kind AIDE sits
beside her, doing the edges.

                    IRENE
          My son's coming Sunday. He's at the university.

                    AIDE
          (gently, who has heard it four Sundays running)
          I know he is, hon. The edges first. Always the
          edges.

Irene fits a piece of blue sky into blue sky, content, certain,
waiting for a Sunday that has stopped arriving.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY

A plain sedan at the curb. DETECTIVE FOSS, forties, unhurried,
stands on the porch with a notebook. Gary holds the door the
familiar eight inches.

                    FOSS
          Ray Halloran. You filed a complaint against
          him. Then he let you go. Then he disappeared.
          I'm working through everybody who had a reason
          to be unhappy with the man, and your name kept
          floating to the top of the list.

                    GARY
          I was unhappy with the man. I didn't make him
          disappear.

                    FOSS
          When'd you see him last.

                    GARY
          The day they walked me out. I never even got
          upstairs.

True, every word, which is what makes him good at this. Foss writes
nothing. She is reading the house behind him, the drawn curtains,
the smell of a man living wrong.

                    FOSS
          Mind if I use your bathroom? Long drive back.

A beat. Below them, the basement. Halloran. Three feet of floor.

                    GARY
          (even)
          Plumbing's out. Whole street. They're digging
          up the main.

Foss holds his eyes one beat past comfortable. Then she nods, puts
the notebook away, steps off the porch.

                    FOSS
          You take care of yourself, Mr. Sobek.

Gary sees: they have sent someone, gently, to find out how much he
knows. We see: a tired detective with nothing solid and a very bad
feeling. She gets in her car. She does not start it. She writes
Gary's name in her notebook and underlines it, and begins to pull
the thread of him, exactly the way he pulls his threads, except that
hers is real.


INT./EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - NIGHT

Gary's car is gone from the drive. INSIDE, the basement.

Halloran, alone, has spent the hours since the detective's voice
came faintly through the floor doing the only thing left to a man
who has glimpsed the door: working his right hand against the zip
tie until the skin is gone and the plastic is wet, and now, finally,
slick enough.

His hand comes free.

He is up the stairs on rubber legs, through the kitchen, fumbling
the chain on the front door, the night air actually on his face,
one foot on the porch--

Headlights swing across the lawn. Gary's car.

A bad, brief, terrible scramble in the doorway, two soft men who
have never hit anyone, and it ends with Halloran back on the floor
of his own captivity, the door shut, both of them gasping, neither
hurt and both undone.

The spell is broken. Halloran has tasted the porch. And Gary, on the
floor with his back to the door, knows now that a detective has his
name and a hostage has felt the night air, and that the slow careful
version of this is over.

                    GARY
          (to the ceiling, to himself, almost calm)
          Okay. Then we stop waiting. We bring it out
          into the open, and we let them try to bury it
          with everybody watching.

He looks at Halloran, ruined on the floor, and at the binder, and at
the wall map, and there is a terrible light coming up in him.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          We go public.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT TWO.
