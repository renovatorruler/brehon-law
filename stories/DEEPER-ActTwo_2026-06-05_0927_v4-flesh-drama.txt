DEEPER
(working title)

ACT TWO  --  third pass, fleshed


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY ONE - MORNING

Gray light through a dirty window well. Overnight the basement has
become something between an office and a cell: a card table, the
binder, the wall map taped to cinderblock, a space heater angled
with awful courtesy at the prisoner.

RAY HALLORAN, zip-tied to the kitchen chair, the dish towel down
around his neck. Gary lifts a paper cup of water with a bent straw
to his lips.

                    GARY
          Small sips. You'll cramp.

                    HALLORAN
          (drinks, then, low and fast)
          Carol calls the police this morning. By tonight
          there are people looking. You have hours, Gary.
          Hours. Drive me to a hospital, say I fell, I
          back your play, I swear to God.

That word, hours, goes into Gary like a clock starting. He glances
at the window well, at the daylight, at the world out there
beginning to move. He has no plan. He grabbed this man off a
sidewalk two nights ago with no plan at all, and the only road he
can see now runs straight through the middle of it: get the truth,
fast, before the hours are gone. He clicks his pen.

                    GARY
          Then we go quick. The contingency line. The
          money. Where does it go.

                    HALLORAN
          It goes to the SEWER, Gary. A collapsed lateral.
          The most boring--

                    GARY
          (writing: SUBJECT STALLING)
          We don't have time for the boring version.
          And I'm trying to help you. They'll hang the
          disposal on you too, you signed for it, you're
          in this whether you talk or not.

Halloran stares at a sane man's nightmare: a drowning man calmly
explaining that the water is a plot. We see it. Halloran sees it.
The only difference is which of them is tied to the chair.


INT. KEPLER HALL - FACILITIES OFFICE - DAY ONE - AFTERNOON

LOPEZ over a speakerphone, Halloran's dark office behind her. A
second supervisor, BRINKMAN, in the doorway.

                    LOPEZ
          He's not answering. Carol's not answering. So
          I called the regional office about this summit
          they supposedly sent him to.

                    BRINKMAN
          And.

                    LOPEZ
          There's no summit. Nobody sent Ray anywhere.
          He emailed her he's in Columbus, and there's
          no Columbus.

Two ordinary people at the front edge of understanding that
something is wrong.

                    BRINKMAN
          Call campus police. And Lopez. When they ask
          who had a problem with the man.

                    LOPEZ
          (already there)
          Yeah. I know exactly who.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY ONE - NIGHT

The TV low. Gary at Halloran's laptop, hunting and pecking the cover
email, getting "love you, R" exactly right, a man forging a life.

On the TV, the local news: weather, a water-main story, a dog that
saved a family. The ordinary surface of the world, and nowhere in it
a leak, a cover-up, a secret under a university. Gary watches it with
a small, rising outrage.

                    GARY
          (to the empty kitchen)
          They've got the whole thing wrapped and nobody's
          even looking.

He does not yet know that the world is about to look very hard, in
exactly the wrong direction.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY TWO - NIGHT

A bowl of soup, half spooned into Halloran by a man who keeps wiping
the dribble from his chin with a paper towel. The grotesque tenderness
of it. The card table has a second chair now. Gary has been down here
for hours, and he is doing the thing he has waited his whole life to
do, which is talk to someone who cannot leave.

                    GARY
          ...so she stands up, Mrs. Vorhees, in front of
          the whole room, and she tells my mother, this
          one. A mind like this comes along once. I was
          nine. And every single thing after that, every
          mop, every closed door, I'm thinking, okay, this
          is the part before they figure out who I am.

Halloran, who has learned in two days that listening is the only
thing that buys him air, listens. And then, somewhere in there, stops
performing the listening and actually hears him.

                    HALLORAN
          (carefully)
          What happened with the book.

Gary goes still.

                    GARY
          What book.

                    HALLORAN
          You said there was a book. Three hundred pages.
          You said it twice.

A long beat. The one wound he never says out loud, and he is so
starved to be heard that he says it to the man he kidnapped.

                    GARY
          (quiet)
          It came back. The press. One line. "Not for us."
          (beat)
          I didn't open the envelope all the way. I read
          enough to see the shape of it and I put it in
          the drawer.

                    HALLORAN
          (gently, the first true thing anyone's said to
           him in years)
          Gary. A press saying not for us isn't the world
          deciding you're nobody. It's a Tuesday. It
          happens to everybody who ever--

                    GARY
          (the door slamming)
          You don't have to manage me, Ray. Eat your soup.

He stands, takes the bowl, climbs the stairs. Halloran watches him
go, and understands, all at once, that he is not chained to a villain.
He is chained to a wound, which is worse, because you can negotiate
with a villain.


INT. RIVERSIDE PRECINCT - FRONT DESK - DAY TWO - MORNING

CAROL HALLORAN, coat over her robe, her two grown KIDS flanking her,
leans across the counter at a DESK SERGEANT.

                    CAROL
          His car is in the driveway. The car has not
          moved. He didn't take his pills. He texts me
          from the bathroom of his own house, and now
          there's nothing, two days of nothing, and you
          keep telling me men leave. My husband did not
          leave. My husband is gone.

The car. The pills. The sergeant stops filing it under a man who
walked out and starts filing it under a man who was taken. He pulls a
different form, and somewhere in the building a machine that does not
get bored begins, quietly, to turn.


INT. GARY'S CAR - DAY TWO - NIGHT

A dark lot. Gary hunched over the prepaid, talking to Royce.

                    GARY
          He gave me a name. The Founders' account. Says
          the money runs through Advancement as gift money
          so it never hits a public budget.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          (receiving scripture)
          The donor side. Of course. That's where money
          has no daylight on it. Gary, do you understand
          what that man just handed you.

                    GARY
          He keeps taking it back. Says he made it up.
          Says he's scared.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          They always recant. The fear comes the second
          after the truth. You push past the recant. You
          can feel you're close, can't you.

Gary can. God help him, he can.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY THREE - DAY

Halloran has changed his strategy. Reason failed. The truth failed.
So now, with the last clear hours he may have, he tries the only door
left, and it is not a lie and it is not the truth. It is recognition.

                    HALLORAN
          I wanted to be an engineer.

Gary looks up from the binder.

                    HALLORAN (CONT'D)
          Two years of school. I was going to build them.
          And I ended up the man who keeps other people's
          buildings from falling down, and one day in a
          parking lot I just, I let it be okay. I make a
          little train town in my garage. My wife thinks
          it's a hobby. It's a grave, Gary. It's where I
          buried the engineer. And it's fine. That's the
          thing nobody tells you. It's fine.

Gary has gone very still.

                    HALLORAN (CONT'D)
          You and me, we're the same animal. You had a
          kid in a photo you were supposed to become and
          you didn't, and it broke something, and instead
          of burying him you went looking for somebody to
          blame for the funeral. There's no Founders'
          account. There's no goo. There's two guys in a
          basement who didn't turn out to be special.
          (his voice cracking)
          Let me go home. Let us both go home.

For one second it is the closest anyone has ever come. We watch it
reach the edge of him.

And then Gary smiles, sad and certain, and pats Halloran's wrist the
way you reassure a frightened child.

                    GARY
          That's good, Ray. That's really good. They
          train you well.

He goes back to the binder. Halloran closes his eyes. He has tried
reason, and he has tried the truth, and he has tried love, and there
is only one country left where this man will let him live, and it is
the country of the lie, and Halloran, broken, books his passage.


EXT. GARY'S NEIGHBORHOOD - DAY THREE - DAY

Gary, hood up, at his mailbox. He stops.

Stapled to the pole at the corner: a flyer. HALLORAN'S face, the good
badge photo, smiling. MISSING. A number. A reward. And the next pole.
And the next. His own quiet street papered with the face of the man
in his basement.

To Gary, the street tilts and shows its true shape: they have
mobilized, they have papered the whole block, they are frightened,
and frightened is what they get when somebody is close. He pulls one
down, folds it, pockets it. Evidence.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          That's when I understood the size of it. You
          don't paper a street with one man's face unless
          you need every soul on that street looking for
          one particular person.

He is right about that. He has the particular person wrong.

Inside, the TV. CAROL, on the local news now, a microphone in her
face, her voice in pieces.

                    CAROL (ON TV)
          Ray. If you can hear this. If somebody has you.
          He takes a pill in the morning and a pill at
          night and he hasn't had either in three days,
          please, he isn't a strong man, he just--

Gary turns it off. His hand stays on the dark screen. Something moves
in him, the man under the mission, a flicker, the soup, the model
trains, "let us both go home."

Then he files it where it must go to survive: she doesn't know what
her husband is part of, and if she's on television, then they put her
there. He goes to the basement door.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY THREE - DUSK

SAL on the porch. Gary holds the door the familiar eight inches.

                    SAL
          Your mother's home called me. You're her
          contact, you missed two Sundays, they didn't
          know who else to try.

That lands somewhere Royce cannot reach.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          And a cop came to the site. Asking is Sobek the
          type to do something stupid. I stood there and
          told a cop no.
          (the whole thirty years in it)
          So I'm asking you. As your friend. I'm not gonna
          argue whatever's in your head, I never could win
          that. Just put it down. Come back.

It lands. The man under the mission surfaces, reaches, almost.

From below them both: a chair leg, scraping concrete. Twice.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (alert)
          What's that.

                    GARY
          (door already closing)
          Furnace. I gotta go.

The door shuts. Sal looks at it a long moment, goes from worried to
afraid, and walks to his truck not knowing he stood three feet from
the answer and chose to believe his friend.


INT. GARY'S KITCHEN - DAY THREE - NIGHT

Gary at the counter, shaking, Sal's voice and Halloran's voice both
still in the room, both saying the same impossible thing. Right at
the edge of putting it down. His hand finds the prepaid.

He calls the only other number in it.

                    GARY
          (unsteady)
          My oldest friend came. My, the guy downstairs.
          They're both telling me the same thing. That
          there's nothing. That I'm sick.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          (gentle, fatal)
          Of course they are. The small ones, and the
          frightened ones, they always tell you that, and
          do you know why, Gary? Because it would cost
          them everything if you were right. The man in
          your basement most of all. You think he wants
          you to find it? Finish it. Before they bury it.
          You are so close.

Gary closes his eyes. Chooses. We watch him choose the man who needs
him over every man who loves him.

                    GARY
          (the crack sealing over)
          ...Yeah. I'm not gonna lose my nerve.

He hangs up. He doesn't look sick anymore. He looks decided. He opens
the basement door, and the last ordinary thing in him stays up in the
kitchen, alone, where he set it down.


INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - DAY FOUR - DAY

Halloran is thinner, grayer, a bruise of fatigue under each eye. He
has bought his passage to the country of the lie, and the terrible
thing is how good he is at it now, because he was always a man who
knew how buildings really worked.

                    HALLORAN
          (low, not meeting his eyes)
          The account routes through three shell
          foundations. I set up the wire codes myself,
          years back, didn't know what for. There's a man
          in Advancement. Voss. He's the one who'd know
          where the material really goes.

Every word the desperate fiction of a man trading invented sentences
for one more night of breathing. Gary writes with a shaking hand.

                    GARY
          (reverent)
          Voss. The shells. Okay.
          (looking up, wet)
          You did a brave thing just now, Ray. History's
          going to know you did a brave thing.

Halloran turns his face to the cinderblock, so Gary will not see what
a man looks like the instant he understands the lie is a sentence with
no end, because now he can never, ever stop telling it.


EXT. UNIVERSITY ADVANCEMENT BUILDING - DAY FOUR - NIGHT

Glass and brick, dark for the night. Gary, good shirt, a visitor tag
he printed at home, walks the exterior like a man casing a vault.
Photographs a loading dock. A keypad. A donor wall of engraved names,
shot panel by panel, certain one of them is the thread.

Inside, through the glass, the most boring room on earth: cubicles, a
phone bank, a banner reading EVERY GIFT BUILDS THE FUTURE. A lone
DEVELOPMENT OFFICER works late, eats a sad desk salad, makes a call
asking some old alum to consider a bequest.

Gary watches the man through the glass with the eyes of someone
studying the enemy's nerve center. We watch a guy hit his quota. A
SECURITY GUARD rounds the building. Gary turns his back, smooth, a man
who has learned a little, and walks unhurried to the dark and the car,
carrying photographs of a fundraising office he is sure is the bottom
of the world.


INT. MEADOWBROOK CARE CENTER - DAY FOUR - DAY

IRENE at her window, a puzzle half done, alone, watching a door that
does not open. The kind AIDE sits to do the edges with her.

                    IRENE
          He's at the university. He's very busy. They
          keep him very busy there.

                    AIDE
          (who has heard it four Sundays running)
          I know, hon. Important man. Edges first.

Irene fits blue sky into blue sky, certain, content, waiting on a son
who is, at this hour, three miles away, photographing a wall of donor
names with a hostage in his basement, the busiest he has ever been in
his life, at nothing.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY FOUR - NIGHT

The corner booth, empty. DONNA wipes it down out of habit. She takes
the folded job clipping from her apron, soft at the creases, and
looks at it a long moment.

Then she drops it in the bus tub with the dirty plates, turns the
booth card from RESERVED to OPEN, seats a family of four, pours their
coffee, laughs at the dad's joke. That is how a person stops waiting.


INT. RIVERSIDE PRECINCT - DAY FIVE - MORNING

DETECTIVE FOSS plants a printout on the desk of her sergeant,
GUTHRIE, who doesn't look up from his own.

                    FOSS
          I want a search warrant on a private residence.
          Today.

                    GUTHRIE
          On what.

                    FOSS
          A missing man's phone that pinged one tower two
          days and quit. The tower covers a quarter mile.
          Inside that quarter mile lives the one employee
          he fired the same week he vanished, who filed a
          grievance against him first.

                    GUTHRIE
          That's a coincidence and a grudge. That's not a
          body. No judge hands you a man's house for that.

                    FOSS
          He stood on his porch and told me his water main
          was out. Whole street torn up, no water in the
          house. I drove past public works on the way in.
          There's no work on that street. There never was.
          (leaning in, the real thing)
          He didn't lie to me about Halloran. He lied to me
          about plumbing. A man lies about the small thing
          when the big thing is in his basement.

A beat. Guthrie looks at her. Reaches for his phone.

                    GUTHRIE
          (dialing)
          Get me Ramos's clerk.

The machine, which does not get bored, picks up the phone.


EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - FRONT PORCH - DAY FIVE - DAY

The plain sedan still fresh in memory. But now it's Gary on the porch,
keys out, leaving to chase Voss, and he stops, because down the block,
parked, engine off, a car that does not belong, two people in it not
looking at him too carefully.

To Gary: surveillance. They've put a car on him. He is so close they
have stopped pretending. He goes back inside, heart up, and does not
leave, and does not understand that the only thing keeping that car
from his door is a magistrate's signature an hour away.


INT./EXT. GARY'S HOUSE - DAY FIVE - NIGHT

The basement. Halloran, alone since the porch voices came faint
through the floor, has spent every hour doing the one thing left to a
man who has glimpsed a door: working his raw hand against the wet
plastic until it is finally slick enough.

His hand comes free.

Up the stairs on rubber legs, through the kitchen, fumbling the chain,
the night air on his face, one foot on the porch--

Headlights swing across the lawn. Gary, home.

A bad, brief, terrible scramble in the doorway, two soft men who have
never hit anyone, and it ends with Halloran back inside, the door
shut, both of them on the floor, gasping, neither hurt and both undone.

Halloran has tasted the porch. And Gary, his back to the door, knows
at last the thing he has refused to know for five days: a detective
has his name, a car is on his street, and a hostage has felt the open
air. The hours Halloran promised him that first morning are spent.

He looks at Halloran ruined on the floor, at the binder, at the
photographs of the donor wall, and a terrible light comes up in him,
the light of a drowning man who has decided the only way left to live
is to make the whole world watch him go under.

                    GARY
          (quiet, almost calm)
          We can't keep it quiet anymore. So we stop
          trying.
          (already seeing it)
          We make them bury it in front of everybody. We
          go public.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT TWO.
