DEEPER
(working title)

ACT ONE  --  third pass, grounded


FADE IN:

INT. KEPLER HALL - THIRD FLOOR - NIGHT

A bucket on wheels. A gray mop going back and forth across waxed
linoleum, slow, even, almost tender.

The man pushing it is GARY SOBEK, late forties, gray work shirt,
his name stitched over the pocket. A careful, contained man.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I want to say one thing up front, so we
          understand each other. I'm not a janitor.

A water fountain on the wall is running, won't shut off. Gary
sets down the mop. Pops the cover with a key off his own ring,
looks at the guts of it for two seconds, pinches a corroded float
with two fingers. The water stops. He did it the way you'd swat a
fly.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I'm a man who is, at the moment, for reasons
          that are mostly not his fault, doing a
          janitor's job.

He picks the mop back up. Down the hall a night class lets out.
Students stream past him without seeing him, the way you don't
see a fire extinguisher. One nearly walks into the cart. Doesn't
apologize. Doesn't register a person was there.

Gary watches them go. No bitterness you could name. Just a man
being water to other people's stone.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - NIGHT

A big dark tiered room. A wall of green chalkboard. On the last
panel, up in a corner, a problem somebody left up. Dense. An
integral sign curled like a question. A note beneath it: LEAVE UP.

Gary comes down the steps unhurried. Sets down a canvas tool bag.
Takes out, with a little ceremony, one piece of yellow chalk he
brought from home.

He studies the problem. A long while. We can't tell if he
understands it. There is a real chance he half does. There is a
better chance he does not.

Then he writes. Fast, filling the board beneath it with symbols,
arrows, a number he circles twice. He steps back, breathing a
little hard, and looks at what he made the way you look at
something you're proud of and a little afraid of.

He does not sign it. He almost does. He doesn't. He gathers his
bag and climbs the steps, and at the top he stops and takes it in
one more time, the way you'd check on a kid asleep, before he
kills the lights and goes back to the mop.


INT. KEPLER HALL - LECTURE HALL 200 - MORNING

Sun. Undergrads filing in. PROFESSOR LIND, sixty, cardigan, the
gentle fatigue of a man who has explained the same thing four
thousand times, comes in mid-sentence with a grad student, DEV.

                    DEV
          ...and Hammond's taking your Tuesday section
          next year, so. They told you, right?

                    LIND
          (no wound at all)
          They told me. Good for Hammond.

A man being quietly shelved, at peace with the shelf. He reaches
the board. Sees the yellow writing under his problem. Doesn't
break stride. Picks up the eraser the way you pick up a sponge.

                    DEV
          Phantom again?

                    LIND
          Mm.

He doesn't read it. That's the thing. He glances at it the way
you glance at a wrong number and wipes it off, three strokes,
talking the whole time.

                    LIND (CONT'D)
          Okay. Sequences. Somebody tell me where we
          left Cauchy.

Chalk dust hangs in the sun. He's already gone, back into the
lecture, the writing on the board for one night of one man's life
erased in the time it takes to clear your throat.

PUSH past him, out the door, to the hallway window.

Gary. Cart parked. He watched the whole thing through the glass.

He does not narrate it. He does not nod knowingly. He just stands
there a second longer than a working man should, holding a wet
mop, while the most accomplished thing he did this week becomes a
smear and then nothing.

Then he pushes the cart on, and we are left to decide for
ourselves what it cost him, and whether the old man should have
looked.


INT. MEADOWBROOK CARE CENTER - DAY ROOM - DAY

Pastel, applesauce and bleach. IRENE SOBEK, late seventies, neat
hair, a jigsaw two-thirds done, bright eyes that come and go. Gary
beside her with a paper cup of bad coffee. For this scene he is
simply a son.

An AIDE, twenties, kind, brings meds in a little cup.

                    IRENE
          (proud, to the aide)
          This is my son. He's up at the university.

                    AIDE
          Oh, nice. What do you do up there?

A beat. Gary looks at the floor. At his mother.

                    GARY
          Maintenance. Systems.

                    AIDE
          Important stuff.

                    IRENE
          He was tested, you know. As a boy. Mrs.
          Vorhees said to me, in front of everybody,
          she said, Irene, this one. A mind like this
          comes along once.

                    GARY
          (an old worn groove, gentle)
          Ma.

                    IRENE
          Well she did.

The aide moves on. Irene presses a puzzle piece that doesn't fit
into a slot that doesn't want it, patient, certain. Then she
clouds, looks at his boots.

                    IRENE (CONT'D)
          You take those off at the courthouse, Walt.
          They just did the floors.

Gary goes still. Walt was his father. Custodian. Thirty years of
courthouse floors. He doesn't correct her. He pats her hand with
the same hand his father used to.

                    GARY
          Already did, Ma. Left 'em at the door.

                    IRENE
          (settling, pleased)
          Good boy.

She goes back to the puzzle and places the wrong piece and does
not notice, and he loves her, and we understand the whole man and
nobody has had to say it.


INT. O'BANNON'S - NIGHT

A real bar. Tradesmen, the game low, peanut shells. A back booth.
GARY with SAL DiPIETRO, fifties, foreman's build and patience, and
two crew guys, TINO and BIG MIKE, three beers in and happy. A roll
of blueprints under a pitcher.

                    SAL
          (unrolling them)
          Two minutes. The mechanical on the Whitmore
          job. Petrocelli's own guy stamped it and
          it's wrong, I can feel it's wrong, I can't
          say where.

                    GARY
          Sal.

                    SAL
          For me.

Gary sighs like it costs him. Pulls the prints over. And here is
the thing the movie turns on: he is good at this. His finger goes
down a riser diagram, stops, traces, stops.

                    GARY
          Your return air's fighting your supply.
          Whoever drew it put the damper downstream
          of the tee. It'll hammer all winter and
          nobody'll know why.

Sal slaps the table, delighted, turning to the others.

                    SAL
          SEE? A week I been saying something's off in
          that wing, this guy reads it like a menu.

                    BIG MIKE
          (toasting)
          To Gary.

                    TINO
          (warm, dumb)
          Shoulda been the engineer. 'Stead a us
          mutts. Remember when he took the fall--

                    SAL
          (fast, flat, a door closing)
          Tino.

Tino stops. A history sits down at the table and gets up and
leaves. Sal looks at Gary with thirty years in his eyes.

                    SAL (CONT'D)
          (quiet, just to him)
          You know who they hand the engineering to?
          Not the guy who can read the print. Come on
          the job. Eight months, union. I'm not asking
          you to be anything you're not. I'm asking
          you to be exactly that.

For one second Gary is just a man being handed his life by someone
who loves him.

                    GARY
          (a real smile, real bitterness, no theory in
           it)
          They don't hire the guy who can read it,
          Sal. They hire the guy whose father knew a
          guy. That's the whole secret. There's no
          secret.

                    SAL
          So come read prints for me. I'm the guy.

                    GARY
          (looking away)
          I got something going right now. I can't.

Sal rolls up the prints, slow, puts them under his arm. The offer
stays on the table, getting older, between two men who love each
other and one of whom is already gone.


INT. DELMORE'S DINER - DAY

Fluorescent, formica. Gary in a booth with a spiral notebook, hand
cupped around the page out of habit, not paranoia. DONNA, forties,
kind eyes, twelve years on this counter, brings the pot, refills
him unasked, sets down pie he did not order.

                    DONNA
          Marcus made the Dean's List. Second semester.

                    GARY
          (surfacing)
          ...Who's Marcus.

                    DONNA
          (small beat)
          My son. Fort Benning. I've shown you the
          picture maybe ten times.

                    GARY
          Right. Good. That's good, Donna.

She tops off a sugar caddy that doesn't need it, to stay another
second.

                    DONNA
          You could do something with that head, you
          know. For money. People pay for a head like
          that.

Half a second, the door opens. He looks up. Really at her. She's
right there, holding it out.

                    GARY
          People pay for the right diploma to say what
          they already decided. Not for the guy who
          actually knows. I'm too old to go audition
          for that.

The door closes. She nods the way you nod at weather. Two taps on
the table. Goes.

From the counter she watches him not eat the pie she brought him
anyway.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          A normal life is a beautiful thing. I think
          about it more than you'd guess. The way you
          think about a country you'll never get the
          visa for.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - DAY

A concrete pit older than the building above it. A section of
floor jackhammered open. Pumps running. A smell that arrives
before the picture and turns your head.

RAY HALLORAN, fifties, facilities director, tie loosened, stands
at the lip of the hole with a VANGUARD ENVIRO FOREMAN, both
looking down like men at a grave.

                    HALLORAN
          So it's the lateral.

                    FOREMAN
          Pancaked. Whole run, building to main. 1920s
          clay, no granular base, they laid it in dirt
          and prayed.

                    HALLORAN
          (crouching, real knowledge, almost wistful)
          Hundred years they got out of it, though.
          You gotta respect it a little.

                    FOREMAN
          You sound like you miss it.

                    HALLORAN
          (standing, knees)
          Wanted to build 'em. Two years of school for
          it. Now I keep 'em from falling on people.
          (the peace of it)
          It's a living.

LOPEZ, a supervisor, comes down with a clipboard.

                    LOPEZ
          Sobek again. Two no-calls. And he was down
          here Tuesday, on the tape. I want him written
          up.

Halloran takes the clipboard. Looks at nine years of a clean file
behind one line. Sighs the sigh of a man who carries other men's
weather.

                    HALLORAN
          He's having a rough patch. Leave it with me.

                    LOPEZ
          Ray.

                    HALLORAN
          Leave it with me, Maria. Man's worked here
          longer than your car's been alive.

He initials the write-up out of existence. A decent man, in a
basement, over a hole full of the one thing this whole film keeps
getting mistaken for treasure.


INT. GARY'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

Small, clean the way a man with too much time is clean. The walls
are bare. On the desk a thick rubber-banded manuscript, title page
yellowed: A UNIFIED THEORY OF SYSTEMS, by G. Allan Sobek. Beside
it, framed, a boy of nine with a ribbon and a science-fair
volcano, beaming, certain of everything.

A clock radio: 2:11 AM. A voice, late-night AM, warm and ruined.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          ...they'll call you bitter. They'll call you
          a man who blames the world for his own
          failures. We got a caller. Line four. You're
          on The Lateness.

Gary, in his undershirt, not even meaning to, picks up the phone.
Dials. He's lonely. It's two in the morning. That's all this is,
at first.

                    GARY
          (into phone)
          Yeah. Hi. First-time caller. I just, I had
          your show on. I work at a university. I
          clean it.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          You clean it.

                    GARY
          And I went to school. Some. I'm not, I read.
          And I guess I called because you said
          before, the thing about, about being smart
          and ending up nowhere, and--

He stops, embarrassed, a grown man almost crying to a radio.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          What's your name, friend.

                    GARY
          ...Gary.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Gary. Let me ask you something and I want
          you to really sit with it. A mind like
          yours. A man who can do what you can do.
          You ever stop to wonder whether ending up
          with a mop was an accident?

Silence. We watch the sentence go into him like a key into a lock
he didn't know was there.

                    ROYCE (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          Or whether a mind like that is exactly the
          kind they make sure stays where it can't do
          any damage.

Gary stands up. He has to. The nine-year-old beams from the frame
across the room. For the first time in forty years, the gap
between that boy and this apartment has an explanation that is not
*you weren't good enough.*

                    GARY
          (barely)
          ...Yeah. Yeah, I. I never thought of it
          like that.

                    ROYCE (V.O.)
          Most don't. They're not allowed to.
          (warm)
          You keep your eyes open down there, Gary. And
          you call me back.

Gary hangs up. Stands alone in his bare clean apartment, holding
a feeling he has never had before and already cannot live without:
that it was never his fault.

He looks at the bare wall. For the first time it looks like it's
missing something.


INT. KEPLER HALL - SUB-LEVEL - NIGHT

Gary's flashlight. Now he's down past the excavation, where he was
not before. The steel door. Newer than the wall. A keypad. A
laminated sign: AUTHORIZED PERSONNEL - HAZMAT REMEDIATION IN
PROGRESS. A logbook on a nail, entries all after midnight, a
company name: VANGUARD ENVIRO.

He stands in front of it a long time, a man at a threshold.

Then, behind the door, a deep wet industrial GURGLE. A pump
kicking on. Liquid moving in volume.

We know what it is. We watched Halloran explain it this afternoon.
It is four hundred gallons of sewage leaving a building that
backed up.

Gary doesn't know that. Gary knows a sealed door he isn't cleared
for, a sign he can't verify, a company he's never heard of, work
that only happens after midnight, and a sound he can't identify.

He takes out his phone and photographs the door. The sign. The
logbook. His hands are not shaking with fear. They're shaking the
way your hands shake the first time anyone tells you that you
matter.


SERIES OF SHOTS - THE WORK, AND THE COST

-- The bare apartment wall, no longer bare. A campus map. The
photos. String. It grows the way these things grow, one reasonable
step at a time, until one day it isn't reasonable, and there is no
single step where you could say that's the one.

-- Gary in his parked car at 1 a.m., photographing a white VANGUARD
van through a used lens he can't quite work.

-- Sunday. Meadowbrook. Irene at the window with her puzzle, alone
this week, watching a door that doesn't open.

-- Delmore's. A slice of pie going cold in an empty booth. Donna
clears it without a word and does not bring another.

-- A job site at dawn. A foreman calling down a line of hardhats.
"Sobek? Anybody seen Sobek?" Nobody has. Sal crosses a name off a
list with a carpenter's pencil and does not throw the pencil,
though he wants to.


INT. ROYCE'S APARTMENT - NIGHT

A garden unit that smells the way the radio sounds. Stacks. A
career, boxed, lid off. ROYCE in person is smaller than his voice,
sixties, a good blazer gone shiny. He pours Gary coffee into a real
cup and saucer, the one formal object in the room.

Gary's binder is open between them. Royce turns the pages slowly,
and does Gary the one thing no one ever has. He reads it like it
is real.

                    ROYCE
          (tapping)
          The van. Three nights, all after one.

                    GARY
          Three I'm sure of.

                    ROYCE
          And the budget. "Facilities contingency." Up
          every October.

                    GARY
          Six years running.

Royce sits back. Takes off his glasses. Whatever this is, it is
seamless, and we will never be entirely sure he doesn't believe it
himself.

                    ROYCE
          You know what they did to me, Gary.

                    GARY
          ...The post.

                    ROYCE
          The post. The journals. The friends. And
          here's the part nobody warns you about. They
          don't argue with you. They don't say you're
          wrong. They just start being worried about
          you. Real gentle. Real kind. Worried.

Gary thinks, hard, of Sal rolling up the prints. Of Donna's two
taps on the table. Something in him goes very still.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          So hear me. Because I wish to God somebody'd
          said it to me in time.

He leans in, takes Gary's wrist. It lands like the kindest thing
anyone has done for him in years. It is.

                    ROYCE (CONT'D)
          It is not your fault that you see it. You
          didn't do anything wrong. Seeing it isn't
          the sickness. They just need you to believe
          it is.

Gary looks down at his own handwriting so it can't be seen what
his face is doing.

                    GARY
          (thick)
          I should go.

                    ROYCE
          Take it to whoever runs that building. Look
          him in the eye when you do.

Gary gathers the binder to his chest and goes out into the dawn.
Behind him, alone, Royce sits with his cold coffee in the real cup,
and we hold one beat too long, long enough to wonder whether being
believed in is just the last warm thing he has left. He turns on
the radio. Static, then music. He washes the one cup.


INT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - HALLORAN'S OFFICE - DAY

Carpet tiles. A dying plant. Halloran on the phone, finger up,
mouthing sorry. Gary across the desk, binder squared, composed.

                    HALLORAN
          (hanging up)
          Gary. Sit. How you doing. Honestly.

The decency is real. He carried this man. Gary slides the binder
across. Sixty pages. Tabs.

                    GARY
          I need you to read all of it. And I need it
          on the record that I came to you first.

Halloran opens it. The map with things circled. The photo of the
door. The budget line, three exclamation points Gary clearly
agonized over. We watch a decent tired man understand, in real
time, that his best floor man in a decade has come loose, and that
it is now somehow his problem, on a Thursday.

                    HALLORAN
          The sub-level. The smell. Gary, that's a
          sewage main. The lateral collapsed, we've
          had Vanguard pumping it five weeks because we
          can't close the building during midterms.
          It's a hole. Full of what you think it's
          full of.

Gary takes this in. Nods slowly. Reasonable. He does not say it's
a cover-up. He says, quietly, the most reasonable-sounding thing in
the world:

                    GARY
          Then it'll be in the work orders. The
          permits. The disposal manifests. All public.
          You can show me, and I'll go home, and I'll
          feel like an idiot, and I'll buy you a beer.

A beat. Halloran, who manages forty buildings and signs a hundred
forms a week, realizes he cannot, off the top of his head, put his
hand on the paperwork for a five-week emergency dig, and that
saying so out loud, to this man, right now, sounds insane.

                    HALLORAN
          ...I'd have to pull it.

                    GARY
          (gently, not gloating)
          Okay.

And for one half of one second, in a carpet-tiled office, a
reasonable man asked a reasonable question and a reasonable man
couldn't answer it, and you, in the audience, felt something tilt,
and that is the entire movie.

Halloran closes the binder. Closes his eyes. Opens them.

                    HALLORAN
          You've missed eleven shifts. You've been
          found in three locked areas. I have carried
          you nine years because you are the best I
          ever had. I can't anymore. I'm sorry, Gary.
          I have to let you go.

A long beat. Gary does not erupt. He picks up his binder, his
evidence, and stands.

                    GARY
          (quiet)
          I gave you that Tuesday. You're firing me
          Thursday.
          (at the door)
          You should've waited a month. It would've
          looked better.

He leaves. Halloran sits alone, looks at the door, looks at his
dead plant, picks up the phone to call somebody about this, and
cannot think who.


EXT. ADMINISTRATION BUILDING - PARKING LOT - NIGHT

Late. Half-empty lot, sodium light. Gary in his parked car for
hours. The binder rides shotgun like a passenger.

A door. Halloran comes out, suit bag on his shoulder, tie off,
just a tired man who wants his couch. Beeps a sensible sedan.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          I drove home. I want that known. I made it
          to my own street. Sat in my own driveway.

Gary's hand on his keys, not turning them.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I thought: if I go inside and make the
          eggs and go to sleep, then tomorrow I'm just
          a man who got fired. A man his mother is
          wrong about.

Halloran's headlights. He backs out.

                    GARY (V.O.)
          And I could not be that man. Not for one
          more night. God forgive me, that's the whole
          reason. It was never about being right.

Gary's engine turns over.

CUT TO:


INT. GARY'S CAR / EXT. SUBURBAN STREET - NIGHT

Gary follows the sedan two cars back, ten and two, obeying every
law on his way to break the only one that counts. Sweating.
Terrified. A man who has never done a single thing in his life.

The sedan pulls into a sad nice cul-de-sac. Gary parks across the
street, kills the lights. Halloran gets out, yawns, grabs his bag,
heads for his door, keys out, thumbing the mail, the most
defenseless animal there is: a man at the end of his own driveway.

In the car, Gary's breathing. The dome light as the door opens.
A tire iron in his hand, which he looks at like he can't account
for his own arm.

He crosses the street, fast, and Halloran half-turns at the
sound, mail in hand, "can I help y--"

SMASH CUT TO:

INT. GARY'S BASEMENT - NIGHT

A water heater. A furnace. A drain in the floor. And RAY HALLORAN,
zip-tied to a kitchen chair, a thin line of blood at his hairline,
gagged with a clean folded dish towel. The decent man. The one who
saved him. The disappointed life that worked.

Across the basement, under a bare bulb, GARY stands holding the
tire iron in both hands, staring at the man in the chair with the
total, nauseous horror of someone realizing they are awake.

The furnace ticks. A long silence.

Gary sets the tire iron down on the dryer, very carefully, as if
quiet could undo it. He pulls a second chair around, sits, knees
almost touching his prisoner's. Takes out the spiral notebook.
Clicks the pen. And the most ordinary man in the world leans
toward the stranger he has chained in his basement and asks him,
with terrible gentleness, the only question he has left:

                    GARY
          Just show me the paperwork. That's all. Show
          me it's what you say it is, and I'll let you
          go, and I'll be the idiot. I want to be the
          idiot. You have no idea how bad I want that.

Halloran, gagged, stares at him.

                    GARY (CONT'D)
          (sitting back, pen ready, almost pleading)
          So. From the beginning. Where does it go.

Halloran screams into the dish towel.

Gary writes it down.

                                                  CUT TO BLACK.

END OF ACT ONE.
