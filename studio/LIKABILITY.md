# LIKABILITY — engine pass

**Job:** an audience does not automatically like a protagonist because he is
right, wronged, or competent. Those generate RESPECT and INVESTMENT, not
warmth. Warmth needs its own ingredients, separately supplied.

## Three channels (name which one a scene is serving)

1. **LIKING (warmth/charm)** — comedy's currency. Humor, banter, specificity,
   comic fallibility. Does NOT require virtue.
2. **ROOTING (investment)** — willpower under pressure, agency, a goal
   pursued at cost. Works even for characters the audience doesn't like
   (Corleone).
3. **FASCINATION (competence)** — the pleasure of watching mastery, morally
   neutral.

A protagonist needs #2 always. A COMEDY or ENSEMBLE piece needs #1 as much
as #2 — competence and suffering alone read as prestige-drama remoteness,
not company you want to keep.

## The mechanisms

- **Undeserved suffering** (Aristotle's pity/fear: the wrong must exceed
  what's deserved) — usually already present when a script has a clear
  villain; rarely the missing ingredient.
- **Competence** — the pleasure of mastery; usually already present in
  process-heavy scripts (heists, craft, expertise); rarely the missing
  ingredient.
- **Protective loyalty toward someone else** — MUST be witnessed landing:
  show the recipient's reaction. A kind act delivered flatly with no one
  visibly moved by it reads as efficiency, not warmth. The reaction shot
  carries the warmth, not the act itself.
- **Self-awareness / self-directed humor** — a character who already judges
  or gently mocks his own flaws pre-empts audience judgment. Absence of
  this is the single most common miss: dignity without self-awareness
  reads as remote, not warm.
- **Comic fallibility** — trying and getting it a little wrong generates
  tenderness that flawless competence cannot.
- **Camaraderie with visible affection under friction** — banter/insults
  that clearly mean the opposite; audiences want IN the group.
- **The private beat** — a moment the mask slips that ONLY the audience
  witnesses, not the other characters. Cheapest, highest-leverage tool for
  a stoic protagonist specifically. Costs one beat, no new scene.
- **Reluctance** — didn't choose the fight; it was forced on him. Frames
  action as response, not aggression.
- **Capacity for change** — offers hope; audiences warm to people who can
  still become something.

## The audit (run on the protagonist specifically, not the ensemble)

1. List every act of generosity/kindness the protagonist performs. For
   each: does another character VISIBLY react (moved, grateful, surprised)?
   If the act lands in a vacuum, it doesn't count — fix the reaction, not
   the act.
2. Count private-beat moments (mask slips, audience-only access). Zero is
   a gap for any stoic/laconic-by-design protagonist.
3. Count instances of self-directed humor or self-aware acknowledgment of
   his own flaw/tic. A protagonist who is never gently embarrassed by
   himself reads as remote.
4. If the register is comedy or ensemble: is there comic fallibility
   anywhere, or is the character flawless at everything he attempts?
   Flawlessness kills warmth even when it earns respect.
5. Diagnose which channel(s) — liking / rooting / fascination — the script
   already has banked, and which is missing, before prescribing a fix.
   Don't add another suffering beat to a script that's already thick with
   suffering; add the missing channel.

## Standing note (recurring cross-project failure, logged 2026-07-09)

The author's default write of a "good" protagonist is stoic + wronged +
competent — which produces respect, not affection. This has recurred across
Birdy (Free Ross), Ratan Singh (Amal), and Cricket (Four Olds). Treat
"is the protagonist likable, not just admirable" as a standing check on
every new lead, not a note to solve once.
