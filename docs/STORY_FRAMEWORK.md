# The Development Pipeline

The order a story is built in, from a bare idea to a beat list. Each step feeds the
next; do them out of order and the later ones wobble. This is the **process**.
`STORY_SPEC.md` is the **grammar** — what the gates check once the beats exist.

---

## 0 — The idea
Whatever the user hands over: a premise, an image, a "what if." Usually it is plot
("a janitor finds a secret"). Plot is not yet a story.

## 1 — Recognize the transformation
Every story is a transformation worked on the audience through the protagonist. Read
the idea for the change underneath it: who he is at the start, who he is forced to
become. This is the root — the **mirror**. It is not the plot; it is the inner turn
the plot exists to cause.

## 2 — Imagine the two worlds
From the transformation, picture both ends:
- the **ordinary world** — the protagonist's stable starting state;
- the **transformed world** — what he becomes.

These are the mirror's two branches (*before* / *after*). Every beat will hang under
one of them.

## 3 — Write the logline: the for-loop and the exception
The ordinary world is a **loop** — a stable, repeating pattern the protagonist keeps
running, with an invariant he never breaks. The story starts the moment an
**exception** is thrown that the loop cannot catch.

> **format:** *[protagonist running his loop]* **but** *[the exception that violates
> the invariant]* → *[the collision that becomes the story].*

> A cop running out the last days to his retirement — the loop, *get out clean* — is
> partnered with a detective who has had a death wish since his wife died (the
> exception), and the two of them cannot both get what they want.

The loop is the ordinary world; the exception is what forces the transformation. The
logline is the **pitch**; the transformation is the **arc**. Same event, two views.

## 4 — The two Doorways of No Return
The irreversible thresholds. Once the hero is through, there is no undo.
- **Doorway 1** (~end of Act 1): he commits, and cannot return to the loop until the
  problem is solved.
- **Doorway 2** (~three-quarters in): a crisis or a discovery that makes the final
  confrontation inevitable.

*(James Scott Bell, "Plot & Structure.")*

## 5 — The Midpoint: the Mirror Moment
The middle is not a lull, it is the hinge. The protagonist is forced to look at
himself — *who am I, who must I become* — and **stops being passive.** Before the
midpoint he reacts; after it he acts. Find this moment first: it is the linchpin that
makes the beginning and the ending agree.

*(James Scott Bell, "Write Your Novel from the Middle.")* In this engine the root
**is** this mirror, and it lands at the hinge by construction.

## 6 — Write the beats
Now, and only now, the beat list: the concrete events in order, each hanging under
the ordinary or the transformed world, with the two doorways and the midpoint already
nailed in place. Plain, connected prose — one beat introduces what the next needs.

## 7 — Decide the pacing: the point of attack
A story can be told from its true beginning or from the middle of a later moment. The
choice turns on the **burn**:

- **Slow burn** (the opening is quiet, the engine takes a while to catch) → **start in
  the middle of the action.** Open on a charged later moment as a short teaser, then
  cut back ("eight weeks earlier") and let the story catch up to it. *Breaking Bad*
  opens on Walt in a runaway RV recording a goodbye to his family, then goes back to
  the beginning.
- **Fast burn** (the hook lands early on its own) → **tell it straight.** No jump.
  *Kokuhaku* opens at a measured classroom pace and reaches its Act-1 detonation — the
  teacher telling the class the milk they just drank carried infected blood — right on
  time, without needing a flash-forward.

The frame is a **fix for a slow start, chosen on purpose**: a teaser lifted from a
later high point, then a clean jump back. It is never a reordering that buries half
the film in flashback. (That is the mistake to avoid — the beat list stays
chronological; the frame is one teaser on top of it.)
